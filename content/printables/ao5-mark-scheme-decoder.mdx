## What AO5 actually says

AO5 is the **content and organisation** objective on **GCSE English Language** — specifically the creative writing task on Paper 1 Question 5 and the transactional writing task on Paper 2 Question 5. It does **not** apply to English Literature.

The AQA wording asks you to:

> Communicate clearly, effectively and imaginatively, selecting and adapting tone, style and register for different forms, purposes and audiences. Organise information and ideas, using structural and grammatical features to support coherence and cohesion of texts.

Read that twice. The two halves matter equally: the *what* (clear, effective, imaginative communication) and the *how* (structure and cohesion).

## Three things examiners reward

Across the upper bands, AO5 high marks consistently come from the same habits:

- **Precise word choice.** Not bigger words — sharper ones. *Glistened* beats *was wet*. *Fumbled* beats *moved*. The verb usually does the heavy lifting.
- **Varied sentence forms.** Mix short, medium and long. Open with subordinate clauses, with participles, with single-word punches. Monotony tanks the band.
- **Deliberate structural choices.** A hook, a turn, an echo at the end. Shifts in time, focus, or perspective that you can point to on a plan.

If a marker can underline an example of all three within your first 15 lines, you are already in the upper bands.

## Common AO5 mistakes

These are the patterns that pull scripts down. Audit your own writing for them:

- **List-heavy description.** Three adjectives stacked before every noun (*the cold, dark, empty street*). It feels rich; examiners read it as padding.
- **Repetitive openings.** Every sentence starting *I* or *The*. Count your first words on a recent piece — if more than half match, fix it.
- **No hook by sentence three.** If a reader could put the page down without curiosity, the opening has failed. Lead with movement, an image, or a question the rest of the piece answers.

## Five sentence-variety drills

Run these on a 10-minute timer before exam day. Use any prompt — a window, a station, a phone call.

1. **Same idea, five lengths.** Write the same sentence as a 3-word punch, then 8, 15, 22 and 30 words. Pick the best one.
2. **Open five ways.** Take one sentence and rewrite the opening five times: with a verb, an *-ing* participle, a subordinate clause, a one-word fragment, a question.
3. **Verb swap.** Underline every verb in a paragraph. Replace half with sharper alternatives. Read aloud.
4. **Cut every adjective.** Rewrite a paragraph with zero adjectives. Notice what the verbs and nouns now have to do.
5. **Echo the opening.** End the piece with a phrase from the first line — changed slightly. Examiners notice deliberate structure.
