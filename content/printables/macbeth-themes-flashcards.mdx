## What's in the set

This printable gives you a clean, cut-out flashcard deck for the **six core themes** in *Macbeth* that examiners return to year after year:

- **Ambition** — how desire propels and corrodes.
- **Guilt** — the psychological cost of murder.
- **Kingship** — the gulf between Duncan, Macbeth, and the model Malcolm grows into.
- **Fate vs free will** — prophecy, choice, and accountability.
- **Gender** — masculinity, "unsex me here", and the play's anxieties about power.
- **Appearance vs reality** — "fair is foul, and foul is fair" and the politics of seeming.

Each theme gets one card, printed on A4 with crop marks so you can cut a deck of six (or scale up to A5 for a sturdier set).

## Card format

The format is deliberately tight, so every card teaches the same way.

- **Front:** the theme name, in **bold**, with a single guiding question (for example, *"How does Shakespeare present ambition as both engine and trap?"*).
- **Back:** **two anchor quotations** drawn from across the play, plus a short *AO2 reading* explaining how a method (image, sound, structure, form) does the work of meaning.

The two-quote rule matters. One quote often comes from early in the play and one from late, so you can already see a trajectory when you flip the card. That gives you the spine of an argument before you have written a sentence.

## Recommended drill

The deck is designed for a **three-week cycle** built on **5 cards a day**.

- **Days 1-2:** read each card front-to-back, then test yourself by hiding the back and reciting both quotations and the AO2 reading.
- **Days 3-7:** mix the deck and pull five at random; for each, write a 90-second paragraph using one anchor quote.
- **Week two:** swap the test condition. Look only at a quote and try to name the theme, then reconstruct the AO2 reading from memory.
- **Week three:** practise *linking* — pick two cards at random and write a comparative sentence that uses one anchor quote from each.

By the end of the three weeks you will have written more than thirty short, retrieval-led responses across every core theme, which is exactly the muscle the exam asks for.
