This printable quote bank for Charles Dickens's *A Christmas Carol* collects canonical lines and arranges them so students can revise the novella both narratively, stave by stave, and thematically. It is intended to support the wider notes at [/revision/texts/a-christmas-carol](/revision/texts/a-christmas-carol) and the deeper essay guidance at [/analysis/christmas-carol](/analysis/christmas-carol).

## How the bank is organised

Quotes are grouped to suit different revision tasks:

- **By stave:** Stave 1 (Marley's Ghost), Stave 2 (the Ghost of Christmas Past), Stave 3 (the Ghost of Christmas Present), Stave 4 (the Ghost of Christmas Yet to Come), Stave 5 (the End of It).
- **By character:** Scrooge, the Spirits, Bob Cratchit, Tiny Tim, Fred.
- **By theme:** redemption, poverty and social responsibility, family, the past and memory.

Each line is paired with the stave in which it appears so that students can locate it inside Dickens's tightly structured five-part arc.

## Sample AO2 hooks

The three worked hooks below model the kind of precise comment that picks up AO2 marks: a small, accurate observation about Dickens's method tied to a wider argument.

**1. "I wear the chain I forged in life."**
Marley's metaphor turns moral failure into something physical and cumulative. The verb "forged" is doing the heavy work: it makes the chain a deliberate, link-by-link product of choice, not fate. Within Stave 1, the line sets up the rest of the novella as a warning, because the spectral burden Marley describes is the future Scrooge is already preparing for himself. It is a high-utility quote for any question on responsibility or consequence.

**2. "Are there no prisons?"**
Scrooge's question, offered to the charitable gentlemen in Stave 1, is later thrown back at him almost verbatim by the Ghost of Christmas Present. Dickens uses structural repetition to expose the cruelty of Scrooge's earlier rhetoric: a line that first sounded like reasonable bookkeeping is reframed as a refusal of human responsibility. Quoting it twice in an essay, with attention to who is speaking and when, is a strong way to track Scrooge's moral education.

**3. "God bless us, every one!"**
Tiny Tim's blessing is universal in scope ("every one") despite coming from the most physically vulnerable character in the novella. Dickens places the line at structurally significant moments and lets it close the book, so a final paragraph can use it to argue that Dickens grants moral authority not to wealth but to the poor and the suffering, the readers he wants his middle-class audience to recognise as fully human.

## Using the bank in revision

Highlight one strong quotation per stave first, then add a second only when the first is secure. Practise writing a one-sentence AO2 hook for each, modelled on the examples above, and test recall by reproducing the quote and hook from memory the next day.
