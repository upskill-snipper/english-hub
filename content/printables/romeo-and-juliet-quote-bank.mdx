## How to use this quote bank

This printable supports closed-book GCSE preparation, where you must recall quotations from memory. Read each quotation aloud, cover it, then write it from memory. Group quotations by character first, then re-sort by theme to build flexibility for unseen extract questions.

## Three sample AO2 hooks

These show how to move from quotation to language analysis. Use the same structure when planning your own paragraphs.

**1. "What's in a name?"**
The interrogative form positions Juliet as a thinker testing the logic of feud loyalty. The monosyllabic phrasing strips language down, suggesting that family identity is hollow when set against love. The rhetorical question invites the audience to share Juliet's doubt.

**2. "I am fortune's fool"**
The personification of fortune as a controlling force frames Romeo as a passive figure. The metaphor of the "fool" recalls a court jester, manipulated for entertainment, dramatising tragic helplessness immediately after Tybalt's death.

**3. "These violent delights have violent ends"**
Friar Lawrence's antithesis of "delights" and "ends" foreshadows the play's catastrophe. The repetition of "violent" links pleasure with destruction, warning that intense passion contains the seed of its own collapse.
