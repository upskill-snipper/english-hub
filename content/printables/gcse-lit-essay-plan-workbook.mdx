## What's in the workbook

A printable A4 workbook designed to make essay planning a **muscle, not a moment of panic**. Every page repeats the same six-box structure so you stop reinventing the wheel under exam pressure.

The workbook contains:

- **Five blank planning templates** — one per page, identical structure.
- **One worked example** — Macbeth on the supernatural, fully completed.
- **Ten starter prompts** across the most-set GCSE Literature texts.
- **A 30-minute self-administered practice schedule** for the fortnight before the paper.

## The planning template

Every template has the same six boxes. Repetition is the point: by template five, the shape should feel automatic.

- **Thesis line.** One sentence. Argument, not summary. *Shakespeare uses the supernatural to externalise Macbeth's ambition rather than to cause it.*
- **Evidence box 1.** Quotation + the AO2 method you will analyse.
- **Evidence box 2.** A second quotation from a different point in the text.
- **Evidence box 3.** A third, ideally from a structurally different scene.
- **Evidence box 4.** A fourth, including AO3 context where it earns its place.
- **Counter-argument box.** The reading you are pushing back against.
- **Synthesis line.** One sentence that fuses the four points into a final claim — not a restatement of the thesis.

## Worked example: Macbeth on the supernatural

The completed plan shows a thesis (*the supernatural reveals Macbeth's existing desires rather than implanting new ones*), four evidence boxes drawn from the witches' prophecy, the dagger soliloquy, Banquo's ghost, and the apparitions in Act 4, plus a counter-argument acknowledging the witches' agency, and a synthesis line on Shakespeare's Jacobean audience.

You'll see exactly how much you need to write per box — and how little.

## Ten starter prompts

A spread across commonly set texts. Pick one, set a timer, plan in 15 minutes:

1. *Macbeth* — How does Shakespeare present guilt?
2. *Romeo and Juliet* — How is conflict between families used to drive the play?
3. *An Inspector Calls* — How does Priestley present responsibility?
4. *Jekyll and Hyde* — How is the duality of human nature explored?
5. *A Christmas Carol* — How does Dickens present poverty?
6. *Lord of the Flies* — How does Golding present the loss of civilisation?
7. *An Inspector Calls* — How is the older generation presented?
8. *Macbeth* — How is kingship presented across the play?
9. *Jekyll and Hyde* — How does Stevenson use setting to create fear?
10. *Romeo and Juliet* — How is fate presented?

## The 30-minute practice schedule

Run this every other day for two weeks before the paper. The whole session is a self-administered 30 minutes:

- **Minutes 0–2.** Pick a prompt. Write only the thesis line.
- **Minutes 2–17.** Fill the six boxes. No quotations from memory? Leave a gap and keep moving.
- **Minutes 17–22.** Fill missing quotations from the text.
- **Minutes 22–27.** Mark your own plan against the criteria — does the synthesis line earn its place?
- **Minutes 27–30.** Note one thing to fix on the next plan.
