## What's inside the printable

This PDF maps the relationships and ideological positions in Priestley's *An Inspector Calls* onto a single page. Six characters are arranged around the Birling dining table, with **Eva Smith** placed at the centre as the structural absence the play turns on. Each named character is given an **anchor quote** and a **one-line dramatic function**, so you can revise both who they are and what they are *for* in the moral architecture of the play.

## The six characters and their functions

- **Mr Birling — capitalism.** The play's mouthpiece for unregulated industrial self-interest, whose dramatic irony about the Titanic ("unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable") and the impossibility of war discredits his entire worldview before the Inspector arrives.
- **Mrs Birling — inherited respectability.** A defender of class hierarchy who treats charity as gatekeeping; her refusal of help to a pregnant Eva exposes the cruelty inside Edwardian "good form."
- **Sheila — capacity for change.** The clearest moral barometer in the younger generation, whose line "It's better to ask for the earth than to take it" registers her shift from consumer to citizen.
- **Eric — guilt.** A young man whose drinking, theft and treatment of Eva model how privilege metastasises into harm; his confrontation with his parents stages the generation gap as moral rupture.
- **Gerald — transactional ethics.** The smooth fiancé whose affair with "Daisy Renton" is rationalised through a vocabulary of kindness; he ends the play trying to dissolve responsibility by questioning whether the Inspector was real.
- **Inspector Goole — Priestley's voice.** A dramatic device more than a policeman, whose closing speech ("If men will not learn that lesson, then they will be taught it in fire and blood and anguish") delivers the play's socialist thesis directly to the post-1945 audience.

## Eva Smith as the absent victim

Eva never appears on stage. The printable treats her deliberately as a **structural device**: the silent figure whose suffering each Birling has touched, and whose absence forces the audience to assemble her humanity from fragments. Showing her at the centre of the map, rather than as an item on the cast list, makes visible what Priestley does with form — he builds an entire play around an unheard voice to indict a society that ignores her real-world equivalents.
