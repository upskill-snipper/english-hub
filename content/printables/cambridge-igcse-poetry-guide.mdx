## What this guide is

A printable approach sheet for Cambridge IGCSE English students sitting 0500 or 0990. It is built around the unseen poetry task — the highest-leverage skill in the paper, because it transfers to comparison work and prose-response questions.

Method first. We give you a routine that holds against any unseen, plus three worked approaches on canonical public-domain poems.

## The 5-pass reading routine

Most students read an unseen once and start writing. That is where marks are lost. A five-pass routine takes about three minutes and produces five times the material. Each pass has a single job.

1. **Form** — scan the shape. Stanzas? Regular or irregular? Sonnet, ballad, free verse, dramatic monologue? Form is a claim before a single word lands.
2. **Voice** — listen for who speaks. First person? Addressed to someone? Confessional, observational, prophetic? Mark the pronouns.
3. **Image** — circle every image doing work. Not every noun — only the ones doing metaphorical or symbolic labour. Two or three is usually enough.
4. **Structure** — track the movement. Where does the poem turn? What happens at the volta or final stanza? How does the closing line answer the opening?
5. **Shift** — name the shift. Every strong poem changes — in tense, tone, addressee, image-field. Identify the shift and you have your spine.

By the end you should have a one-sentence reading you can defend. That sentence becomes your thesis.

## Three worked unseen approaches

Use canonical public-domain poems for practice. Apply the same routine to each.

- **Approach A — *Ozymandias* by Shelley**. Form: irregular sonnet. Voice: nested narrators. Image: broken statue. Structure: zoom in, then out to "the lone and level sands". Shift: boast to ruin. Thesis: power makes claims time refuses.
- **Approach B — *Remember* by Rossetti**. Form: Petrarchan sonnet. Voice: addressed to a beloved. Image: "darkness and corruption". Structure: octave commands "remember", sestet unsays it. Shift: instruction to permission. Thesis: love asks first for memory, then release.
- **Approach C — *La Belle Dame sans Merci* by Keats**. Form: ballad quatrains. Voice: questioner then knight. Image: "cold hill side". Structure: question, story, return. Shift: enquiry to haunting. Thesis: enchantment leaves its survivors stranded.

Three poems, three structures, three shifts. Practise the routine on each and you will recognise the same machinery in any unseen.

## Comparison-question strategy

When the exam asks you to compare, do not write two essays glued together. Write one essay with two voices.

- **Open with a shared spine** — name the theme that links the poems and stake a claim about how each handles it differently.
- **Alternate, do not silo** — every paragraph should reference both poems.
- **Earn the difference** — examiners reward subtle contrasts. "Both handle grief, but where A externalises it as image, B internalises it as silence."
- **Close with a shift** — name how each poem ends and what that ending argues. Last lines do disproportionate work.

Useful timing: 45 minutes — five planning, thirty-five writing, five proofreading.
