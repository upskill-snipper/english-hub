Comparing two unseen poems is the hardest single moment in the GCSE Literature exam: you have never read the poems before, you have roughly 35 to 40 minutes to write the response, and you must build an argument rather than a list. This printable framework gives you a repeatable five-step process you can practise until it becomes automatic.

## The five-step comparison structure

1. **Read both poems twice.** First reading: get the literal sense and speaker. Second reading: mark shifts, repeated images, and the final line. Spend roughly four minutes here.
2. **Find similarities.** Both poems share a topic by definition (the exam tells you so). Look beyond topic to shared methods: tone, form, perspective, imagery patterns. Note two or three.
3. **Find differences.** Where do the poets diverge? A common pair: one poem accepts something the other resists; one is regular in form, the other ragged. Note two or three.
4. **Pick two or three anchor points.** You cannot compare everything. Choose the points where the contrast is sharpest and most arguable.
5. **Write a thesis.** One sentence that names both poems and stakes a claim. Example: "Both poems present nature as overwhelming, but where Frost finds quiet acceptance, Hopkins erupts into religious ecstasy."

## Annotated worked example

Imagine the exam pairs Frost's "Stopping by Woods on a Snowy Evening" with Hopkins' "Pied Beauty". Both treat the natural world. Both are short. Both end with a turn.

The contrast: Frost's speaker is held by the woods but pulled away by duty ("miles to go before I sleep"). Hopkins' speaker is held by creation and pulled deeper in ("Praise him"). Frost's tetrameter is steady and lulling; Hopkins' sprung rhythm is jolting and ecstatic.

A working thesis: "Both poets stand still before the natural world, but Frost's speaker is reluctantly returning to obligation while Hopkins' is rapturously returning to God."

That single sentence gives you a spine. Every paragraph below it pulls on the same thread.

## Common comparison mistakes

- **False equivalence.** Forcing similarity where there is none, or claiming "both use imagery" as if that distinguishes anything. All poems use imagery.
- **Listing not arguing.** Writing a paragraph on Poem A, then a paragraph on Poem B, with a "similarly" tacked on. Examiners want integrated comparison: discuss both poems in the same paragraph, on the same point.
- **Ignoring form.** Comparing only what the poems say, not how they say it. Form, line length, rhyme, and rhythm carry as much meaning as the words.
- **Writing a third introduction.** You do not have time for a thesis paragraph plus two body paragraphs plus a conclusion plus extras. Get to the argument fast.

## Time allocation in the exam

- Read and annotate: 4 to 5 minutes
- Plan the comparison and write the thesis: 3 to 4 minutes
- Write the response: 25 to 28 minutes
- Check: 2 to 3 minutes
