An Inspector Calls is one of the most exam-friendly texts on the GCSE English Literature spec. Its structure is tight: one act of dramatic time, one dining room, one slow revelation that pulls every Birling into the light. Across AQA, Edexcel, OCR and Eduqas the question patterns are remarkably consistent — characters, themes, and Priestley's social message — and the play rewards revision that goes deep rather than wide.

Five days is enough. But only if you do them right. The plan below assumes around two hours per day, treats the play as a piece of dramatic argument rather than a story to summarise, and ends with timed practice rather than another read-through. If you skip the practice day, your revision will not convert into marks.

## Day 1: The play in one sitting

Start by treating the play as a single experience. Read the whole text in one sitting, or watch a full production: the 1954 Guy Hamilton film, the 2015 Whitechapel BBC adaptation, or a recording of the National Theatre version directed by Stephen Daldry. Watching matters because Priestley wrote a play, not a novel. The lighting cues — "pink and intimate" before the Inspector arrives, "brighter and harder" once he begins — are part of the meaning, and they only land when you see them.

While you watch or read, write five lines for each act. No more. The point is to capture the shape of the act, not to summarise dialogue.

Then map what we will call the moral arc. Take a sheet of paper and split it into seven columns: Mr Birling, Mrs Birling, Sheila, Eric, Gerald, the Inspector, and Eva Smith. For each character, write their reaction to Eva's death at three points: when the Inspector first arrives, halfway through the interrogation, and at the final curtain. The pattern is the play's argument. The younger Birlings move; the older ones do not. Gerald sits between them and chooses his side. By the end of Day 1 you should be able to say, in one sentence, who has changed and who has not.

## Day 2: Characters as ideas, not personalities

The mistake most students make is treating characters as people. They are not. They are ideological positions Priestley has dressed in Edwardian clothes. Day 2 is about translating each character into the idea they carry.

- **Mr Birling** = capitalism. Specifically, the self-made industrialist convinced that "a man has to mind his own business and look after himself and his own." He exists to be discredited, which is why Priestley loads him with wrong predictions about the Titanic and the war.
- **Mrs Birling** = inherited respectability. She is "her husband's social superior" and uses charity as a gatekeeping tool. Her refusal of Eva at the Brumley Women's Charity Organisation is the play's coldest moment.
- **Sheila** = capacity for change. She begins "very pleased with life" and ends as the play's clearest moral voice. Her line "these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people" is the hinge.
- **Eric** = guilt and weak masculinity. He is the Edwardian son the system produces: drinks too much, takes what he wants, then collapses under the weight of it.
- **Gerald** = transactional ethics. He treats Eva's affair as a kindness done to her, and he is the one who, with Mr Birling, tries to argue the whole evening away.
- **Inspector Goole** = Priestley's voice and the play's conscience. Whether he is real, supernatural, or a dramatic device is deliberately unresolved.
- **Eva Smith** = the absent victim. She never speaks. She is the play's accuser precisely because every character has had to imagine her and shape her to fit their version of events.

For each character, write a single paragraph answering: what does Priestley use this person to argue? If you cannot answer that question in one sentence, you do not yet understand them.

## Day 3: Themes that travel

Day 3 is about the themes that recur across exam papers and across boards. Pick four and learn them deeply rather than skimming ten.

**Class and capitalism.** This is the play's spine. Read up on Priestley's wider context: he wrote in 1945, immediately after the Second World War, with a postwar Labour landslide on the horizon and the welfare state being debated. He set the play in 1912, two years before the First World War, in the late Edwardian period when class lines were rigid and "social Darwinism" — the idea that the rich deserved their position by natural superiority — was still respectable. The gap between writing and setting is the engine of the play's irony.

**Gender and respectability.** Mrs Birling and Sheila are the play's two models of female behaviour. Mrs Birling polices respectability; Sheila refuses to. Eva, meanwhile, is sexualised by Gerald, used by Eric, and judged by Mrs Birling. Note how Priestley structures the female characters as a moral spectrum.

**Generational responsibility.** The play splits cleanly along age. The older generation will not learn; the younger will. This is dramatic structure as argument. Priestley is staking his hope on the audience members who are Sheila and Eric's age.

**The chain of events.** The Inspector says, "we don't live alone. We are members of one body. We are responsible for each other." The play's plot is a literal chain — each Birling does one thing, each thing leads to the next, the cumulative effect is a death. This is collective responsibility shown dramatically rather than argued abstractly.

## Day 4: Quotes that examiners reward

Quotation matters, but not in the volume students think. Five embedded, well-analysed quotations beat fifteen recited ones. For Day 4, learn the following and write five lines of analysis on each.

- "If men will not learn that lesson, then they will be taught it in fire and blood and anguish." — The Inspector's final speech. Note the conditional structure ("if... then") and the prophetic register. Priestley wrote this in 1945; the audience had just lived through the lesson.
- "We are members of one body." — Biblical echo (1 Corinthians 12). The Inspector is given the moral weight of scripture without being made overtly religious. Useful for AO3 on Priestley's socialism and Christian-socialist tradition.
- "A man has to make his own way." — Mr Birling, Act One. The line he is given to be wrong about. Priestley undermines it twice over: dramatically, by what follows, and historically, by writing in 1945.
- "Cheap labour." — The phrase Mr Birling uses for his workers. Sheila throws it back at him in Act One: "But these girls aren't cheap labour — they're people." Two characters, same words, opposite meanings — perfect for AO2 on language.

For each quotation, write five lines: what it means literally, what technique Priestley uses, what it tells us about the speaker, what it tells us about Priestley's argument, and how it connects to a theme. Five lines, five jobs done. That is your essay paragraph in miniature.

## Day 5: Timed essay practice

Day 5 is the day that converts revision into marks. Two essays, 45 minutes each, written by hand if your exam is handwritten. Pick one character question and one theme question — past papers from your board are best, but any of these work: how Priestley presents responsibility; how Priestley presents Sheila; how Priestley uses the Inspector to challenge the Birlings; how Priestley presents older versus younger generations.

Mark each essay against the AO chart for your board (AO1 ideas and quotation, AO2 language and structure, AO3 context, AO4 written accuracy where applicable). Be honest. Where did you run out of time? Where did context slot in naturally and where was it bolted on? Where did you quote without analysing?

Do not rewrite the essay. Note three things you would do differently next time, and stop. The aim of this day is not a perfect essay; it is a clear list of habits to fix in the exam itself.

## Common mistakes

Three traps catch most candidates.

**Reading Inspector Goole as supernatural without engaging Priestley's deliberate ambiguity.** "Goole" sounds like "ghoul," yes — but the play never confirms it. Priestley keeps the Inspector's status open so the audience asks the moral question regardless of whether he is real. Treat the ambiguity as a dramatic choice, not a riddle to solve.

**Listing themes without showing how they're built dramatically.** "The play is about responsibility" is not analysis. How is responsibility built? Through the chain of events, through the contrast between generations, through the Inspector's interrogation structure that forces each Birling to confess in turn. Always answer the "how" question.

**Treating Mr Birling as a caricature without engaging his function.** He is a caricature. That is the point. Priestley makes him wrong about everything — the Titanic, the war, the workers — because he wants the audience to reject the worldview Birling represents. Caricature is a deliberate technique here, not a flaw.

## Context that scores AO3

Three context points do most of the work.

The 1912 setting is pre-war, Edwardian, and steeped in social Darwinism. Class hierarchies were rigid; women had no vote; workers had little legal protection. This is the world Mr Birling defends.

The 1945 audience had just lived through two world wars and was about to vote in the Labour government that built the NHS and the welfare state. They knew Birling was wrong because history had told them so.

The two-time-period structure is dramatic irony's engine. Every confident prediction Mr Birling makes — "the Titanic, unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable" — lands on an audience that knows what comes next. Priestley uses the gap between 1912 and 1945 to argue that the audience must not let the older generation's worldview win again.
