Most students walk into the Power and Conflict exam having read all fifteen poems many times. They still forget half the cluster by question two. The problem is almost never effort. It is the way the cluster is being studied.

Re-reading is not the same as remembering. Highlighting is not the same as retrieving. And studying the poems in anthology order is one of the slowest possible routes to recall, because the brain stores information by meaning, not by table of contents.

There is a more efficient way, and it is not a secret. Cognitive science has been pointing at the same three levers for decades: chunking (group ideas by what they share), retrieval practice (force yourself to bring the answer back without looking), and spaced repetition (revisit material at expanding intervals so each review pushes the memory deeper). Stack those three on top of each other and a cluster that felt unmanageable in March can feel solid by exam morning.

What follows is a 10-day plan calibrated to mark schemes for AQA GCSE English Literature Paper 2, Section B. It will not turn you into an examiner. It will turn fifteen separate poems into a small, navigable map you can hold in your head under timed conditions.

## The 15 poems, ordered for memory (not for the anthology)

The anthology lists the poems in a fixed order. Your memory does not store them that way. It stores them by what they share. So before you do anything else, regroup them by theme. The same poem can appear in more than one group, and that overlap is the most useful part of the system, not a flaw in it.

### Conflict between humans

- Bayonet Charge
- The Charge of the Light Brigade
- Remains
- War Photographer
- Exposure

These five sit closest to the lived experience of soldiers, journalists, and the people who carry war home. If a question lands on guilt, trauma, fear, or the gap between battlefield and civilian life, you are looking here first.

### Conflict with self

- My Last Duchess
- Tissue
- Checking Out Me History

This is the cluster of inner voice and identity. The Duke speaks himself into a corner. *Tissue* meditates on what we let define us. *Checking Out Me History* fights the version of selfhood that schooling tried to impose.

### Conflict with nature

- Storm on the Island
- Exposure
- Kamikaze

Three poems where the natural world becomes either an enemy, a witness, or a stage. *Exposure* sits in two groups on purpose: nature is the killer, but human war put the speakers in front of it.

### Conflict with power structures

- Ozymandias
- London
- Tissue
- Kamikaze
- The Emigrée
- The Prelude (extract)

Six poems where the antagonist is a system: empire, the city, paper economies, militarised honour, the regime that erases a homeland, the sublime that humbles a child. Most exam questions reward students who can talk about power without reducing it to one ruler.

Notice how *Tissue*, *Exposure*, and *Kamikaze* show up more than once. That is not laziness. It is exactly what makes them strong comparison choices: they bridge themes, which means they bridge essays.

## Days 1-3: 5-minute summaries each

The first three days are not about memorising anything. They are about orientation. You cannot remember a poem you have not yet seen as a whole shape.

For each poem, do this and nothing else:

- Read the poem twice. The second time, read it out loud. Hearing the rhythm catches things your eye skips.
- Write five lines on a card or in a notebook:
  1. Theme(s) the poem belongs to
  2. Form (sonnet, dramatic monologue, blank verse, free verse, ballad, etc.)
  3. Voice (first person soldier, omniscient narrator, child speaker, dramatic persona, etc.)
  4. One key technique the poet leans on (caesura, enjambment, irony, semantic field, sibilance, anaphora)
  5. One key image you cannot forget

That is it. Five minutes a poem, five poems a day, three days. Fifteen cards by Wednesday night.

Do not try to memorise quotations yet. The point of these three days is to make sure no poem in the cluster is a stranger when you sit down to start the harder work.

## Days 4-6: anchor quote per poem

Now the memory work begins. The trap most students fall into is trying to remember three or four quotes per poem. You do not need that for a top mark. You need one quote per poem that does the most work, and you need to know it cold.

Pick a quotation that:

- Carries a clear technique you can name
- Connects to more than one theme
- Is short enough to write without thinking

Two worked examples to anchor the pattern.

**Ozymandias.** The line you want is the inscription on the pedestal:

> Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!

This single line gives you irony (the works are gone), imperatives (the tyrant is still commanding from beyond death), and a direct address to "the Mighty" that flatters the reader into the king's blind spot. It links to power, decay, and hubris in one move.

**London.** The line you want is from the third stanza:

> mind-forg'd manacles

Three words. A compound metaphor. Self-imposed psychological imprisonment. It links instantly to *Tissue* (paper that controls us), *Checking Out Me History* (the curriculum as cage), and *The Emigrée* (the regime that "branded" the speaker's city). One quotation, four comparisons.

Do this for all fifteen poems. One quote each. Fifteen quotations total.

The non-negotiable rule: spaced repetition.

- Day 4: learn five quotes. End of day, write them out from memory.
- Day 5: write yesterday's five from memory first. Then learn five more. End of day, write all ten.
- Day 6: write the previous ten from memory first. Then learn the final five. End of day, write all fifteen.

If you can write fifteen quotations from memory on Saturday night, you have already done the single hardest thing in this revision cycle. Most students never get there.

## Days 7-8: comparison spine

The exam is one named poem from the cluster plus one comparison poem you choose from the rest. AQA reward students who arrive with comparison links pre-built rather than improvised on the day.

Build five **comparison spines**. Each spine is a thematic pair-cluster of three to four poems with anchor quotations already attached. You are essentially pre-loading five mini-essays.

**Spine 1: power and decay.** *Ozymandias*, *London*, *My Last Duchess*, *Tissue*. Power is built, power crumbles, power is preserved on paper or stone, power haunts a corridor.

**Spine 2: war and guilt.** *Bayonet Charge*, *The Charge of the Light Brigade*, *Remains*, *War Photographer*. The body in motion, the body as cavalry statistic, the body that will not leave the speaker's mind, the body developed in a tray of chemicals.

**Spine 3: memory and identity.** *The Emigrée*, *Checking Out Me History*, *Kamikaze*, *War Photographer*. What we are allowed to remember, what we refuse to remember in the way we were told, what the family chooses not to remember, what the photograph forces other people to remember.

**Spine 4: nature and insignificance.** *Exposure*, *Storm on the Island*, *The Prelude*, *Ozymandias*. Nature as slow killer, nature as siege, nature as humbling teacher, nature as the eraser of empire.

**Spine 5: voice and silencing.** *My Last Duchess*, *Checking Out Me History*, *The Emigrée*, *Tissue*. The Duchess silenced, the speaker reclaiming voice, the regime suppressing voice, the paper that records voice but does not produce it.

For each spine, write the poems on one side of an A4 page and the anchor quotations on the other side. By the end of Day 8 you should be able to recite each spine without the page in front of you.

## Day 9: write 2 timed comparisons

You cannot test recall by reading. You test it by producing.

Pick two past-paper questions from real AQA exemplars (any year). Write each answer in 45 minutes. No notes, no poems open in front of you. Just you, the question, and the spines you have built.

After each one, mark the response against the AQA AOs:

- **AO1:** clear thesis, sustained argument, well-chosen quotations
- **AO2:** language, form, structure analysed with subject terminology
- **AO3:** context as illuminator of the poem, not as a biographical lump

Do not rewrite the essay. Resist the urge. Instead, write three short notes at the bottom: three things to do better next time. That is what your brain will actually use tomorrow.

## Day 10: spaced rest

This is the day most students get wrong. They cram. They re-read every poem. They try to learn three new quotations the night before. None of that helps.

The night before the exam, do this and nothing more:

- Re-read your five comparison spines, not the poems themselves
- Glance through your fifteen anchor quotations once
- Sleep at the normal time

Sleep is not the absence of revision. It is when memory consolidates. The hippocampus replays the day's learning during slow-wave sleep, and the connections you made on Days 4 to 9 actually solidify overnight. A student who sleeps well and revises lightly the day before will outperform a student who sleeps four hours and reviews everything.

## Quote-recall tricks that work

Three techniques that consistently help students under exam stress.

### Acrostic anchors

Take the key words of a quotation and reduce them to first letters. *Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!* becomes **L-W-M-D**. Picture the letters in your hand. Under exam conditions, the letters surface first, and the words pour back through them. You do not need a clever mnemonic for every quote; just the ones that keep slipping.

### Visualisation

Place each poem on a familiar street. *Ozymandias* outside your front door, *London* at the bus stop, *Storm on the Island* at the corner shop, and so on, all the way to school. This is the ancient memory-palace technique, and it works because spatial memory is far older and stickier than verbal memory. When you walk that street in your head, the poems appear in order without effort.

### Write quotes on paper, not screen

Typing skips a step the brain needs. Handwriting forces sequencing — letter by letter, in the order the words actually appear. Studies on motor encoding consistently show better long-term recall for handwritten material than typed material. Use a real pen for the fifteen anchor quotations, even if everything else lives in a Google Doc.

## Closing

If you remember nothing else from this post, remember this. The students who walk into the Power and Conflict exam confident are not the ones who read the poems most often. They are the ones who *grouped* them, *retrieved* them, and *spaced* their revision across enough days that each quotation got at least three rounds of forgetting and recovery.

Ten days. Fifteen poems. Five spines. Fifteen anchor quotations. Two timed essays. One night of real sleep.

That is the whole plan. Start tomorrow morning, and on the day, you will surprise yourself.
