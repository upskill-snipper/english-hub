You don't need to know what AO2 is. You don't need to remember the witches' speech from Macbeth. You don't need to be able to explain dramatic irony or parse a sonnet.

What you do need is a way of asking questions that helps your child think — and the willingness to be the audience while they do that thinking out loud. That's it. That's the whole job.

This piece is the practical version. No jargon. No "sit your child down for a serious chat about Shakespeare." Just the small daily things that, done consistently, move the needle.

## What you don't need to be

A surprising amount of parental anxiety around GCSE English comes from the assumption that you have to be qualified to help. You don't.

- **You don't need to remember Macbeth from school.** Most of us don't. The plot you half-remember is fine. Your child's job is to know it, not yours.
- **You don't need to know the mark scheme bands.** Knowing the difference between a level 4 and level 6 response is the teacher's job. You don't have to score anything.
- **You don't need to mark essays.** Reading them with red pen in hand will make you anxious and your child defensive. Skip it.
- **You don't need to be the teacher.** Parents who try to be the teacher often make things worse — partly because they end up correcting things they're not sure about, and partly because the home stops feeling like home.

The role you're playing is "interested adult who listens." That role is genuinely useful and almost no one else can play it.

## What you DO need to do

There are three things that are within reach of any parent, regardless of their own English background.

**Make space.** A quiet desk, a reading lamp, and a no-phone-in-the-bedroom rule will do more than any amount of nagging about revision. The environment your child works in matters more than the pep talks.

**Ask one specific question per day.** Not "did you revise?" — that gets a one-word answer and ends the conversation. Try: "Tell me one thing you learned about Lady Macbeth today." Or: "What was your English lesson actually about?" Specific questions get specific answers.

**Be the audience for spoken practice.** Some of the most useful revision your child can do is explaining a quote, an idea, or a paragraph plan to another human. You are that human. You don't need to understand all of it. You just need to be there and to nod when something makes sense and frown when it doesn't.

## The 5-minute conversation that helps

Try this. Daily. After dinner is ideal — they're not yet barricaded in their room, you've both eaten, and the timing is short enough that it doesn't feel like an interrogation.

The question is: **"What's one thing you struggled with today?"**

Then your job is to listen. Not to fix. Not to suggest a website or remind them of a deadline. Just to listen while they describe the problem.

Here's the thing about that conversation: the act of articulating a struggle out loud is itself a form of revision. When your child says, "I keep forgetting how to talk about Macbeth's ambition without just retelling the play," they have done more useful thinking in that one sentence than in twenty minutes of staring at a textbook. Your role is to keep them talking long enough for that to happen.

If they shrug and say "I dunno," that's fine. Try again tomorrow.

## Reading aloud — the underrated tool

Twice a week, ask your child to read five lines of one of their set texts aloud to you. Five lines. That's all.

Then talk about what struck you. Not what you think the right answer is — what struck you. "That bit about the dagger sounded angry, didn't it?" Or: "I didn't really know what was going on there — what was happening?"

This is the move that surprises parents. The fact that you're not an English teacher is actually useful here. Your reaction is data. Examiners aren't English teachers reading the text for the hundredth time — they're reading hundreds of essays in a row, and the bits that land hardest are the bits that work for a fresh listener. If something confuses you, it might confuse the examiner too. If something makes you sit up, it might make the examiner sit up.

You don't have to "get" it. You just have to react honestly.

## Quote drilling — without you knowing the quotes

Quote memorisation is one of the most miserable parts of GCSE English revision and one of the easiest to support without any subject knowledge.

Print a stack of quote flashcards (or wait for our quote bank PDF, which is on the way). Each card has the quote on the front and a one-line gloss on the back — what it means, what technique is used, what theme it links to.

You hold the cards. You read the front. They tell you the gloss. Then you flip the card and check.

You do not need to know whether the gloss is right in any deep way. You only need to be able to read what's printed on the back. If their answer matches, they got it. If it doesn't, they put it back in the pile and try again later.

This is the most useful five minutes of revision they'll do in a day, and you can do it while waiting for the kettle to boil.

## Practical homework support

Sometimes your child will hand you an essay and ask if it's any good. Here's the trick.

Don't read it for content. You'll get drawn into questions like "is this the right reading of the witches?" that you can't answer.

Read it for **flow**. Specifically, ask yourself: "Did this paragraph make sense to me as a non-expert?"

That question is more important than parents realise. Top-band essays are often praised for being "controlled" and "coherent" — meaning a reader can follow the argument from one sentence to the next. If you can't follow it, the examiner probably can't either.

If a paragraph confuses you, your feedback is: "I lost the thread here — can you say what this paragraph is actually arguing?" That is a Grade 9-relevant question. They probably need to rewrite — not because the content is wrong, but because the writing is doing too much work in too few words.

You're not marking. You're being the human who reads the essay cold. That role is genuinely valuable.

## What to avoid

Some patterns that come naturally to parents tend to backfire. None of these are character flaws — they're just things to notice and steer away from.

- **"I never understood Shakespeare and I'm fine."** Said warmly, often as reassurance. The problem is it tells your child the work doesn't matter. They've already worked out that you're fine; they need to know you think the effort is worth making.
- **"Just do your best."** Vague enough to be useless. Your child can't action it. Replace with something specific: "Spend twenty minutes on quotes tonight, then you're done."
- **Phone-snatching as motivation.** Tempting, but research on adolescents and self-regulation generally suggests that confiscation creates resentment without building habit. A better move: structured, agreed device-free study windows — phone in the kitchen for forty-five minutes, then back.
- **Comparing to siblings or friends.** "Your brother revised every night at your age." This rarely lands the way the parent hopes. Your child is not your other child, and not their friend. The comparison is almost always heard as "you're worse than them," which is not motivating.

## When to escalate

Most home support gets you most of the way there. But there are signals that something else is going on.

- **If grades are dropping despite hours of effort**, talk to the English teacher. Not in an accusatory way — just to ask what they're seeing in class. Often there's a specific gap that's invisible from home.
- **If your child is anxious about exams** in a way that's affecting sleep or appetite, the school's wellbeing or pastoral lead is the right contact, not the English teacher. Exam anxiety is a wellbeing issue first and a subject issue second.
- **If they refuse to revise at all**, look at sleep, screen time, and friend group before assuming the problem is English. A child who's exhausted, scrolling until 1am, or in the middle of a friendship issue won't have the bandwidth for Lady Macbeth no matter how good the revision plan is.

You're not going to fix all of these. You're going to notice them and ask for help from the people whose job it is to help.
