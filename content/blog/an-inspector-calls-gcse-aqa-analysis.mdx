# An Inspector Calls GCSE Analysis: AQA English Literature Guide

If you can explain *why* Priestley set a 1945 play in 1912, and *how* the Inspector functions as more than just a detective, you are already ahead of most students sitting the AQA English Literature GCSE. This guide shows you how to do both — and how to turn that understanding into essay paragraphs that address AO1 (critical reading and personal response), AO2 (language, form, and structure), and AO3 (contexts and their influence on meaning).

**TL;DR:** *An Inspector Calls* is a morality play structured around a single evening visit. For AQA GCSE essays, focus on Priestley's use of dramatic devices — dramatic irony, tension, and cyclical structure — to explore themes of social responsibility, class, and guilt. Link character behaviour to context (the 1912 setting, the 1945 post-war audience) and analyse language choices in key scenes. This guide shows you how.

---

## Overview & Context: Why the 1912 Setting Matters

### The Titanic and Social Inequality

Priestley sets the play on an evening in April 1912 — the same month the *Titanic* sank. That is not a coincidence. The *Titanic* disaster has become one of the most potent symbols of Edwardian class hierarchy: first-class passengers had a significantly higher survival rate than third-class passengers. Priestley's audience in 1945 would have known this. The Birlings, celebrating Sheila's engagement in their comfortable dining room, are the first-class passengers — entirely insulated from the consequences of their actions on people like Eva Smith.

### Priestley's Socialist Message and Post-War Britain

When the play premiered in 1945, Britain was at a crossroads. The Second World War had just ended, a Labour government was about to win a landslide election, and audiences were actively debating what kind of society they wanted to rebuild. Priestley, a committed socialist, used the play to argue that the old Edwardian values of self-interest and rigid class hierarchy had already led to catastrophe — twice, in two world wars. The 1912 setting allowed him to show those values at their most complacent, just before they collapsed.

### How Context Shapes Interpretation

This dual time frame is crucial for AO3. When Mr Birling confidently declares that "the Germans don't want war," a 1945 audience would have laughed bitterly — they had lived through two German wars. His certainty becomes dramatic irony, and his confidence about everything else (that workers should accept low wages, that his family owes nothing to society) becomes equally suspect. Every time Birling is wrong about history, Priestley signals that he is wrong about morality too.

## The Inspector as Dramatic Device

### Is the Inspector Real or Supernatural?

The Inspector's identity is never resolved. He may be a real police officer, a ghost, a collective conscience, or a theatrical device who exists only to drive the plot. Priestley leaves this deliberately open, and your essay should acknowledge the ambiguity rather than settle it. What matters is his *function*: he is a catalyst who forces each character to confront their share of responsibility for Eva Smith's death.

### How He Controls Each Character

The Inspector never raises his voice. His power comes from stillness and precision. Priestley's stage directions describe him as creating "an impression of massiveness, solidity and purposefulness." He controls the pace of every revelation, showing the photograph to one character at a time — a detail that becomes structurally significant, because we never confirm whether they are all looking at the same woman.

### Dramatic Irony: What We Know vs. What They Know

The Inspector's name — Goole — sounds like 'ghoul', a supernatural being associated with the dead. Whether intentional or not, it colours every scene he appears in. More importantly, the audience understands from early on that the Birlings' world is about to be dismantled. The dramatic irony is not just about historical events; it is built into the play's structure. We watch the family incriminate themselves one by one, unable to stop what is coming.

## Key Themes: Responsibility, Class & Guilt

### Collective vs. Individual Responsibility

The play's central moral argument is that responsibility cannot be divided into neat individual portions. Each Birling contributed to Eva Smith's destruction in sequence: Mr Birling sacked her, Sheila had her dismissed from a second job, Gerald kept her as a mistress and then abandoned her, Eric coerced and stole from her, and Mrs Birling refused her charity. Priestley's point is that this chain of harm is only possible in a society that treats working-class women as disposable.

### Class as a Barrier to Empathy

Mr Birling's refusal to accept blame for sacking Eva is the clearest example of class functioning as a barrier to empathy. His justification — "If we were all responsible for everything that happened to everybody we'd had anything to do with, it would be very awkward" — is played for its absurdity. The word "awkward" is devastating in its smallness. A young woman is dead, and Birling's concern is social inconvenience. Priestley uses Birling as a mouthpiece for every selfish Edwardian attitude he wants his audience to reject.

### Guilt and Denial in the Birling Family

The generational split is where Priestley places his hope. Sheila and Eric accept guilt and grow. Mr and Mrs Birling deny guilt and shrink. By the end of the play, the older generation's relief that "there's no Inspector Goole on the police force" is more chilling than comforting — they have learned nothing, and Priestley makes sure the audience knows it.

## Character Study: The Birling Family & Gerald

### Sheila: From Complicity to Conscience

Sheila begins the play as a somewhat shallow, privileged young woman. Her role in Eva's dismissal from Milwards — driven by jealousy over a dress — is petty and cruel. But Sheila is the character who changes most visibly. By Act 2, her admission "I know I'm to blame" represents a complete moral about-turn. She does not deflect or minimise. This arc is Priestley's argument that the younger generation, if they choose to, can break from their parents' values.

### Eric: Weakness, Alcohol, and Redemption

Eric's confession is the most uncomfortable in the play. His relationship with Eva involved coercion and theft from his father's office. Yet Priestley does not let the audience simply condemn him: Eric's drinking is partly a response to his cold, dismissive father, and his eventual shame is genuine. He is a study in how privilege and neglect can produce moral failure — and how that failure can still, just about, be acknowledged.

### Mr Birling: Pride and Self-Interest

Birling is Priestley's primary target. His opening speeches — celebrating capitalism, dismissing collective responsibility, predicting a peaceful future — are written to be proved wrong at every turn. He is not a villain in the theatrical sense; he is something more dangerous: a respectable man who has never had to examine his own conscience.

### Mrs Birling: Social Status Over Morality

Mrs Birling's refusal to help Eva at the charity she chairs is the coldest act in the play. Her justification — that Eva had "only herself to blame" — reveals a woman so invested in social hierarchy that she cannot extend basic compassion across class lines. Her shock when she realises Eric is the father she has been condemning is one of Priestley's most precisely timed dramatic moments.

### Gerald Croft: The Outsider's Perspective

Gerald occupies an interesting middle position. His affair with Eva was, in some ways, the kindest treatment she received — he genuinely cared for her, at least temporarily. But he still abandoned her. His willingness to investigate the Inspector's identity, and his relief when the Inspector appears to be fraudulent, aligns him more with the older Birlings than with Sheila and Eric.

## Language & Imagery: Priestley's Craft

### The Photograph as a Structural Device

The Inspector's photograph is never shown to the audience. This is a deliberate AO2 choice. By withholding it, Priestley keeps the question of Eva's identity open and forces the audience to consider whether the characters are confessing to harm done to one woman or to a whole class of women. Eva Smith — 'Eva' suggesting life, 'Smith' suggesting everyman — may be symbolic rather than singular.

### Colour, Light, and Darkness

Priestley's opening stage direction specifies that the lighting should shift from "pink and intimate" to "brighter and harder" when the Inspector arrives. This is not incidental. The warm, rose-tinted light of the Birlings' celebration is literally replaced by something harsher and more revealing. For AO2, this is a clear example of Priestley using stagecraft to reinforce theme.

### Dialogue: Formality vs. Emotional Truth

The Inspector's dialogue is unusually direct for the period. He does not observe the social niceties that the Birlings use to deflect and delay. His repeated refusal to be distracted — cutting off Birling's bluster, redirecting Mrs Birling's evasions — creates a rhythm of exposure. The phrase "I don't know" appears across several characters' responses, and each time it signals not ignorance but denial.

## The Ending & Cyclical Structure

### The Twist: Another Inspector Coming

The final telephone call — confirming that a girl has died and a real inspector is on his way — is one of the most effective endings in GCSE drama. It resets the play to its starting point and suggests that the Birlings will have to face their reckoning whether they accept it or not.

### Has Anyone Learned Anything?

Some of them have. Sheila and Eric have changed; their parents have not. The ending is simultaneously hopeful and pessimistic, which is Priestley's point. The possibility of change exists, but it requires active moral choice. The audience in 1945, about to rebuild their society, was being asked to make that choice.

## How to Structure an Essay: AO1, AO2, AO3 in Practice

Here is a worked example on Sheila's character. Weak answers describe rather than analyse.

**Weak answer:** "Sheila feels guilty about what she did to Eva Smith. She says 'I know I'm to blame.' This shows she is sorry."

**Improved answer:** "Priestley presents Sheila as the character most capable of genuine moral growth. Her direct admission — 'I know I'm to blame' — uses the first person without qualification, contrasting sharply with her father's deflecting syntax ('If we were all responsible…'). The bluntness of Sheila's phrasing reflects Priestley's argument that honest self-examination, rather than social performance, is the foundation of real responsibility. Writing for a 1945 audience emerging from collective wartime sacrifice, Priestley positions Sheila's willingness to accept shared guilt as the model for post-war reconstruction."

This paragraph addresses all three AOs: it quotes and interprets (AO1), analyses Priestley's language choices (AO2), and links to 1945 context (AO3).

## Common Essay Questions & How to Tackle Them

AQA questions on *An Inspector Calls* typically ask you to explore how Priestley *presents* something — a character, a theme, or a relationship. The word 'presents' is your cue to focus on AO2: *how* is Priestley doing this, not just *what* is happening.

- **'How does Priestley present responsibility?'** — Use the chain of harm across all five characters. Argue that Priestley presents responsibility as collective and inescapable.
- **'Analyse the role of the Inspector'** — Focus on dramatic function: catalyst, moral voice, ambiguous figure. Use the stage directions and the name 'Goole'.
- **'Explore the theme of class'** — Contrast Birling's self-interest with Eva's vulnerability. Use the *Titanic* context for AO3.

Whatever the question, turn it into a thesis in your opening sentence. Do not summarise the plot — state your argument.
