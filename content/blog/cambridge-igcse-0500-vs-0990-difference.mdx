A frustrating moment a lot of IGCSE students have, often around exam-entry season: they've been quietly revising "Cambridge IGCSE English" for months, only to find out — via a teacher comment, a printed entry sheet, or a parent reading the small print — that the school is actually entering them for **0990**, not **0500**. Or the other way round. And suddenly the past papers they've been hammering through have a different code at the top, and they aren't sure if any of it counts.

The honest answer is: most of it does count, but the codes do mean different things, and it's worth understanding what.

This piece walks through the difference factually. Where Cambridge has published clear information, we'll point at it. Where things get school-specific (paper combinations, coursework, tier choices), we'll say so and tell you to verify with your teacher. Don't take any of this as a substitute for the official Cambridge syllabus document for the year you're sitting — that is the source of truth.

## What 0500 is

**Cambridge IGCSE English — First Language (0500)** is the long-running, well-established Cambridge IGCSE specification for first-language English. International schools have used it for decades. If your parents or older siblings sat IGCSE English at an international school, there's a strong chance it was 0500.

Key features of 0500 as a qualification:

- It's graded on the traditional **A\*–G** scale.
- It tests reading, writing, and (in some combinations) listening and speaking.
- It has historically offered a **coursework option** as an alternative to one of the exam components, depending on the route the school has chosen.
- It has been available in **tiered** routes (Core and Extended) in some years and components — meaning some students sit a paper aimed at lower grade ranges, others sit one aimed at higher grade ranges.

The exact paper combinations and which components your school enters you for can vary year-on-year and country-on-country, so always check the current syllabus PDF on the Cambridge International website for the year you're sitting. The basics — reading comprehension, summary, directed writing, composition, response to a non-fiction text, response to literary or descriptive material — have been recognisable across editions, but the specific paper numbers and timings shift.

## What 0990 is

**Cambridge IGCSE (9–1) English — First Language (0990)** is the more recent specification. It was introduced to provide a 9–1 graded version of the same qualification, aligned with the way UK GCSEs were re-graded.

Key features of 0990:

- It's graded on the **9–1 scale**, with **9** as the highest grade.
- The subject content is aligned with 0500 — meaning the same kinds of texts, the same reading and writing skills, the same emphasis on summary, directed writing, and composition.
- It is widely used by schools that want their students' grades to look like UK GCSE grades (e.g. UK independent schools, UK-curriculum-aligned international schools).

Cambridge designed 0990 so that schools can choose between the two grading scales without having to teach materially different content. That's the part students often don't realise.

## The actual content difference

Here's the simple version, and the honest version:

The **subject content is largely the same**. The skills tested — reading for explicit and implicit meaning, summarising, analysing language, writing for purpose and audience, composing extended pieces — are the same skills. The kinds of texts you'll be asked to engage with are the same kinds of texts. The mark schemes' assessment objectives are essentially the same.

The **grading is different**:

- **0500** uses **A\*, A, B, C, D, E, F, G** (with U for ungraded).
- **0990** uses **9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2, 1** (with U for ungraded), where 9 is the highest grade and is more selective at the top end than A\* was.

The 9–1 scale is more granular at the top: in old money, an A\* roughly maps to grades 9, 8, and (high) 7, so the top end is split more finely. That changes how grade boundaries work but not what the exam asks of you.

A practical implication: if you've heard "you need a 7 to be on track for a 9," that's a 9–1 (0990) framing. If you've heard "you need an A to push for an A\*," that's a 0500 framing. Same students, same skills, different vocabulary.

## Who sits which?

This part is genuinely school-by-school, but a few patterns:

- **International schools** outside the UK system often choose **0500**, partly because that's what they've taught for years, partly because A\*–G is what local universities, employers, and parents recognise.
- **UK independent schools** that offer IGCSE rather than GCSE often choose **0990**, because the 9–1 grading aligns with what UK universities and sixth-form colleges expect from a UK GCSE student.
- **Some schools offer both** — sometimes splitting by year group, sometimes by ability stream, sometimes by family preference.
- **Some students don't know which they're being entered for** until late in the course. This is more common than it should be.

If you're not sure: ask your teacher or your school's exam officer. The syllabus code (0500 or 0990) will be on your statement of entry. It's a quick question with a clear answer.

## Practical revision implications

The honest practical answer: **the prep is mostly the same**.

- The **content you study** — quotes, themes, language techniques, structural devices, conventions of different writing forms — is the same regardless of code.
- The **skills you practise** — annotating an unseen text, writing a summary, planning a composition, analysing a writer's choices — are the same.
- **Past papers cross over.** Because the content is so closely aligned, past papers from 0500 are useful prep for 0990 students and vice versa, with the obvious caveat that the grade boundaries on each paper are different.

What does change:

- **Grade boundaries**, literally. 0990 has 9–1 boundaries; 0500 has A\*–G boundaries. Don't try to convert your raw mark on a 0500 paper into a 9–1 grade using your gut feeling. Use the right boundaries for the right paper.
- **The way teachers talk about targets.** If your teacher refers to "a strong 7" or "borderline 8/9," they're working in 0990 terms.

## What does NOT differ

To make it concrete, here's a shortlist of things that, in practice, are the same across 0500 and 0990:

- The **kinds of texts** on the syllabus — the genres, registers, and types of source material you'll meet.
- The **core skills** — reading, writing, comprehension, summary, analysis of language and structure.
- The **broad exam structure and timing** for equivalent components — the papers exist to test the same things.
- The **mark schemes** and **assessment objectives** are very closely aligned. An examiner reading a 0500 response and a 0990 response is looking for almost identical things.

So: if you've been revising hard and you find out you're sitting the "other" code, you have not wasted your time. You have done the right work.

## What MAY differ — verify with your school

Some things genuinely do depend on the year and on choices your school has made. Don't take any of this as gospel; **confirm with your teacher or exam officer**:

- **Tier of entry.** 0500 has historically had Core/Extended-style tiering in some components; 0990's tiering arrangements may differ. Which tier you're in affects your accessible grade range.
- **Specific paper combinations.** Schools sometimes choose between paper options (e.g. a coursework alternative vs an exam-only route). Which combination you're sitting affects how you should weight your revision across reading, writing, and any speaking/listening elements.
- **Coursework vs exam-only.** Where coursework is an option, schools choose. If you don't know whether you're submitting coursework, ask now, not in the final term.
- **Speaking and listening.** Whether these are assessed as a separate endorsement or built into the qualification depends on the route.

If any of those affect you, the right person to ask is **your subject teacher or your school's exam officer**, and the right document to look at is the **current Cambridge International syllabus PDF for the year of your exam**. Don't rely on a forum post or a years-old guide.
