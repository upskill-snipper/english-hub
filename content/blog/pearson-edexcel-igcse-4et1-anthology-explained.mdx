If you sit Pearson Edexcel IGCSE English Literature, the anthology is the spine of your course. It is the document you will return to more than any other set text, the source of every quotation you will memorise for poetry questions, and the basis for the comparison work that decides a huge slice of your final grade. Students who treat the anthology as a casual reading collection tend to underperform. Students who treat it as a structured comparison toolkit tend to outperform their predicted grades by a band or more.

This guide walks through the whole picture: what 4ET1 actually is, what sits inside the anthology, how it is examined, where most students lose marks, and a four-week revision plan you can drop straight into your calendar. Where specific poem titles or counts are uncertain, I will defer to the canonical list at [/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology](/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology) rather than guess. The cost of a wrong fact in a literature guide is an entire lost essay strategy, so I would rather under-promise than mislead.

## What is 4ET1?

4ET1 is the spec code for Pearson Edexcel International GCSE in English Literature. It is the international counterpart to the UK domestic Literature qualification and is sat by students in international schools across Europe, the Middle East, Asia, and parts of Africa, as well as by UK independent schools that prefer the Edexcel international route.

The qualification is built around three main routes to assessment. Most students sit two written papers:

- **Paper 1** focuses on drama and prose, drawing on set texts you study across the course.
- **Paper 2** focuses on poetry and includes both anthology poems and unseen poetry analysis.

A third route, **Paper 3**, exists as a coursework alternative for centres that have approval to assess part of the qualification through controlled coursework. The anthology features in Paper 2 for the exam route and may also feed into Paper 3 work if your centre uses the coursework option.

The headline takeaway: the anthology is not a side text. It is examined directly, the marks attached to it are substantial, and the assessment style is specific enough that generic essay technique will not save you. You need to know how the anthology poems compare with each other, how to handle an unseen poem alongside an anthology poem, and how to write under time pressure.

## What's in the anthology — poetry section

The anthology is structured around two clear groupings: a modern poetry section and a pre-1900 (sometimes called heritage) section. Across both clusters, the anthology pulls together a carefully curated set of poems chosen to give you variety in voice, form, period, and subject matter. The exact line-up runs to roughly 15 poems in total split between the two groupings, but the canonical list lives at [/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology](/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology) and you should always cross-check it against your most recent specification document before relying on a memorised list.

Rather than risk inventing titles, I want to focus on what consistently matters in the anthology: the recurring themes that the examiner returns to year after year. Four themes dominate, and if you have a strong cross-reference for each of these you will be ready for almost any comparison the exam can throw at you.

**Identity** is the workhorse theme of 4ET1 poetry. Multiple poems explore who the speaker is, who they have become, and how they negotiate the identities imposed on them by family, culture, geography, and history. When the question asks about voice, self, or perspective, you are usually being asked an identity question wearing a different costume.

**Conflict** is a close second. The conflict in 4ET1 poems is rarely literal warfare. It is more often internal — the speaker pulled between two cultures, two languages, two memories, two versions of themselves. When you cluster poems for revision, ask which of them carry an unresolved tension at their centre. Those are your conflict poems.

**Place** matters enormously across the anthology. Poems set in specific landscapes, cities, or rooms use that location to do thematic work. The room becomes a metaphor for confinement; the landscape becomes a metaphor for memory; the city becomes a metaphor for alienation. Students who treat setting as decorative miss the point. Setting in 4ET1 is almost always argumentative.

**Memory** is the fourth recurring theme and arguably the most underrated. Memory poems in the anthology often blur tense — present and past collapsing into each other — and use that blurring to make a claim about how identity is constructed. If you can spot a memory poem and explain why its handling of time matters, you are already writing at a higher band.

When you study the poetry section, do not try to master every poet equally. Pick four anchor poems — ideally one from each theme above — and know them at a level of depth that lets you adapt them to any question. The remaining poems become supporting cast, useful for comparison but not load-bearing on their own.

## What's in the anthology — prose section

Alongside the poetry, the 4ET1 anthology contains a prose section made up of short extracts from both fiction and non-fiction sources. These prose extracts vary in style, period, and tone, and they are designed to give you a working library of texts to compare in unseen-style work.

In some assessment routes, the prose extracts are used as the launching point for comparison tasks where you analyse how a writer constructs meaning, voice, and effect. In others, they sit alongside the poetry as part of the wider canon of texts your teacher will draw on for class analysis. Because the use of the prose section depends on which paper you are sitting and which year of the specification applies, I would urge you again to check the canonical list and the latest specification document at [/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology](/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology) rather than rely on a generic summary.

What is true across all routes: the prose section is a gift if you read it actively. Each extract is a worked example of how a strong writer handles voice, structure, and tone in a confined space. Even if your route does not examine prose extracts directly, reading them sharpens the analytical instincts you bring to poetry.

## Exam structure for the anthology

The anthology shows up most directly in **Paper 2**. The structure of the poetry question on Paper 2 is the single most misunderstood thing about 4ET1, so let me be precise about what you are walking into.

You will face a comparison task. Depending on the precise question structure for your sitting, this will either be:

- A comparison between **one named anthology poem and one unseen poem**, where the question gives you a thematic angle (for example, a question about identity, or about the presentation of conflict) and asks you to compare how each poem handles it; or
- A comparison between **two anthology poems**, where the question gives you a printed poem and asks you to compare it with another anthology poem of your choice on a stated theme.

In both cases, the time allocation works out to roughly **45 minutes per question**, and the marks are weighted enough that a botched comparison will tank your overall paper grade.

The mark scheme rests on three Assessment Objectives:

- **AO1** — your response to the texts and the way you support points with reference to the writing.
- **AO2** — your analysis of how writers use language, form, and structure to create meaning.
- **AO3** — your understanding of context, including the relationships between texts and the contexts in which they were written.

Crucially for IGCSE Literature, **there is no AO5** in the way some other Literature specifications use it. You are not being asked to evaluate critical interpretations or weigh competing readings. You are being asked to read closely, to compare carefully, and to anchor your reading in context. Keep that scope in mind — it stops you wasting time on the wrong kind of analysis.

## How to revise: a four-week plan

The single best decision you can make about anthology revision is to start early and structure it. The plan below is built for four weeks of focused work, around 45 minutes per day. Adjust the dates to your own exam schedule.

**Week 1: read every anthology poem twice and annotate.** First reading is for sense. Second reading is for marginalia. By the end of the week, every poem in your anthology should have at least five lines of your own annotation in the margin — not copied from a guide, written by you. Five lines is a deliberately low bar; the point is that something in your handwriting is on every poem.

**Week 2: cluster by theme and find three comparison spines.** Take your four themes (identity, conflict, place, memory) and group the poems under them. Most poems will belong to more than one cluster. Then, for each theme, write a single sentence that captures a comparison spine — a way of pairing two poems on that theme that produces a specific argument. By the end of the week you should have three spines you could write an essay around.

**Week 3: drill one anchor quote per poem.** Aim for **15 quotes total**, one per poem, each chosen because it carries weight on more than one of your themes. Memorise them. Use spaced repetition — flashcards, voice notes, whatever works. The goal is not encyclopaedic recall; it is having the right quote ready when the question hits.

**Week 4: timed comparison practice.** Sit at least three full 45-minute comparison answers under exam conditions. Do them on different theme spines. Then mark them yourself against the assessment objectives, or have a teacher mark them. The compounding effect of timed practice in week four is huge — students routinely jump a grade band in the final week if they actually do the timed work.

## Where most students lose marks

The marking I have seen on 4ET1 anthology essays clusters around four recurring failures. Knowing them in advance is half the battle.

**Treating poems as standalone objects.** The exam is built around comparison, but students often write two parallel essays — one about poem A, one about poem B — and call it comparison. Examiners can see this immediately. Real comparison weaves the two poems together, sentence by sentence, and uses one to illuminate the other.

**Forgetting AO3 (context).** Because the AO3 weighting feels lighter than AO1 and AO2, students drop context entirely. That is a mistake. A few sharp, specific contextual points — the period a poem was written in, the speaker's cultural position, the literary tradition the poem sits inside — lift your essay into the higher bands without much extra effort.

**Picking quotes that don't help the comparison thesis.** A famous quote is not always the right quote. The right quote is the one that most efficiently supports the argument you are making. If your thesis is about the way memory destabilises identity, do not pick the quote about landscape just because it is the one you remember best. Pick the quote that earns its place in the argument.

**Not practising unseen poetry under timed conditions.** This is the single biggest unforced error in 4ET1 prep. Students drill the anthology poems religiously and then panic when the unseen lands in front of them. Treat unseen practice as half of your comparison preparation, not an afterthought. The anthology gives you the comparison muscle; the unseen forces you to use it on a poem you have never met.
