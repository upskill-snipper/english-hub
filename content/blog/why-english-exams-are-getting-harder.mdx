If you have ever sat down with a past paper from 2014 and then immediately tried a recent one, you already know what this article is about. The papers do not just look different — they feel different. The questions ask for more in less time. The mark schemes reward fewer essays at the top end. The whole exam seems to assume a kind of fluency that the older papers simply did not.

You are not imagining it. GCSE English really is harder than it was ten or fifteen years ago, and there are concrete structural reasons why. This piece breaks down what actually changed, why the bar keeps drifting upwards even when the syllabus stays still, and — more usefully — what to do about it as a student or a parent watching your child wade through Year 10 and Year 11.

## The 2017 reform: what changed

The pivot point is the September 2017 reform of GCSE English Language and English Literature in England. This was part of a wider redesign of GCSE qualifications under the Department for Education, with the explicit goal of making them more demanding and more comparable to the old O-Level standard. The headline changes are easy to list, but each one has a knock-on effect that is bigger than it looks.

Before the reform, GCSE English was graded on the familiar A* to G scale. It was modular in places, meaning students could bank credits across the two years. There was a meaningful coursework component, particularly in English Language, where controlled assessments contributed a chunk of the final grade. And, crucially, Literature exams were open-book — students walked into the hall with a clean copy of the text in front of them.

After the reform, almost all of that disappeared. Grading shifted to the 9 to 1 scale, with grade 9 set deliberately above the old A* to spread out the top end. The qualifications became fully linear: every mark counts at the end of Year 11, no matter what you did in Year 10. Coursework was abolished for both Language and Literature. And Literature became closed-book, meaning students sit the exam without the texts they have been studying for two years.

The skills being tested are, on paper, similar to what came before — analysis, evaluation, comparison, structured writing. But the stakes per paper went up dramatically, and the cognitive load on the day rose with them.

## Closed-book texts — the cognitive load

Of all the reform changes, going closed-book is the one that students and teachers feel most acutely.

Pre-reform, a student writing about Macbeth could turn to Act 2 Scene 1, find the dagger soliloquy, and quote directly from the page. Their attention could focus on what the quote was doing — the imagery, the structure, the link to a wider thematic argument. Memory was barely part of the test.

Post-reform, every quote in every essay must be retrieved from the student's own head. For a typical GCSE Literature student that means having a working memory of dozens of quotes across A Christmas Carol or An Inspector Calls, the same again for Macbeth or Romeo and Juliet, and a separate stockpile for the poetry anthology. None of these can be looked up. They have to be there, accurately, under timed exam pressure, often months after the last classroom reading.

This turns memorisation into a skill in its own right. It is not enough to understand a text well. A student who deeply gets the way Priestley uses the Inspector as a moral mouthpiece can still lose marks because they cannot remember the precise wording of the speech they want to analyse. The result is that revision time gets eaten by quote-learning that, ten years ago, simply was not necessary.

## Mark-scheme creep

The second shift is more subtle but arguably more important: the mark schemes themselves have become harder to satisfy at the top end.

Look at the band descriptors and they appear, on the surface, similar to the pre-reform versions. Top band essays are described as "perceptive", "conceptualised", "convincing", with "judicious" use of evidence. Pre-reform mark schemes used much of the same vocabulary. The words have not changed.

What has changed is what examiners now expect those words to mean. A "perceptive, conceptualised" response under the current AQA, Edexcel, OCR or Eduqas mark schemes is one that does not simply analyse a text — it argues about it. Top band students are expected to come in with a thesis, organise their entire essay around it, and use textual evidence as material for that argument rather than as the point of the analysis itself. Many of the essays that scored A* in 2014 would, today, sit comfortably in band 4 or low band 5 rather than at the top.

There is also a quieter year-on-year ratchet. Grade boundaries are set after each summer's papers based on cohort performance. When students get better at certain question types — when teaching catches up — boundaries shift to maintain the spread. This is not a conspiracy; it is how the system is designed. But the felt experience for students is that the bar at each grade keeps creeping up.

## Unseen poetry: the hardest single question

If you were to nominate the single hardest piece of work in current GCSE English Literature, the unseen poetry question would have a strong claim.

The format is brutal in its simplicity. Students get one poem they have never read, on a topic they have not prepared for, and roughly thirty to forty minutes to write a full analytical response. On many specifications there is also a comparison to a second unseen poem, which compresses the time per text even further.

What makes this question so revealing is that it tests breadth rather than depth. Anything you can revise in advance — quotes, themes, context — is not what is being assessed. What is being assessed is whether you have read enough poetry, of varied periods and styles, that an unfamiliar poem still feels familiar in shape. Have you seen a sonnet before, so you recognise the volta? Have you read enough free verse to be unfazed by the absence of regular rhyme? Have you seen extended metaphors so often that you can spot one without having to look it up?

This is where preparation gaps show up most. A student who has revised their set texts thoroughly but read nothing outside the syllabus can lose ten or fifteen marks here, simply because the poem in front of them looks alien. And because the question carries significant weight on the paper, those lost marks pull the overall grade down sharply.

## How to adapt — the four-strand approach

If the exam has changed, preparation has to change too. Doing more of what worked in 2012 will not get you to where the current top band sits. The most effective approach we see, in students who genuinely move two grades over Year 11, has four strands running in parallel.

The first is memory practice — quote drilling, daily rather than weekly. Closed-book exams reward students who treat quotes the way a musician treats scales. Five to ten minutes a day, every day, on flashcards or written recall, will outperform a two-hour quote-learning session once a week. The brain consolidates spaced repetition far more effectively than mass practice.

The second is thesis-led writing. Every essay you write in practice should open with an argument, not a description. If your introduction reads like the back of the book, you are writing a 2014-style essay in a 2026 exam. Force yourself to take a position — what is the writer doing, and why does it matter? — and then make every paragraph defend that position.

The third is timed practice with stakes. By the second half of Year 11, you should be doing at least one full essay a week under proper exam conditions: closed-book, single sitting, timed to the minute. Without that pressure, your mock-grade prediction is meaningless. The skill of writing well in forty-five minutes is different from the skill of writing well in ninety, and only the first is being tested.

The fourth is breadth reading, especially in poetry. Set aside time to read ten or fifteen poems outside your syllabus before exam season — different periods, different forms, different voices. You do not need to analyse them in depth. You just need them in your head as reference points so the unseen poem feels like one of a family rather than an alien artefact.

## What stays the same

It is worth saying clearly: the texts themselves have not changed. Shakespeare is still Shakespeare. A Christmas Carol still reads the way it always did. The poetry anthologies have been edited at the margins, but the core canon of what a GCSE English student is expected to know is broadly stable.

The subject knowledge content is, in that sense, identical. The historical context for An Inspector Calls is the same context it was in 2010. The language techniques have not been renamed.

And good analysis is still good analysis. A close reading that picks up on a precise verb choice, links it to a broader theme, and connects that theme to authorial intent is just as valuable now as it ever was. The rules of the game have not changed — only the threshold for "good" has moved up.

## The mental model that helps

The unhelpful mental model is the one most students naturally fall into. It runs something like: "this exam is harder than my older sister's was, which is unfair, which means I should be cut some slack." It is true that the exam is harder. It is also true that the unfairness is structural rather than personal. Neither of those facts makes a single mark of difference on results day.

The more useful frame is this: the bar at each grade is higher, so the preparation has to be better, not just longer. A grade 7 today demands the kind of conceptual writing that earned an A in 2014. A grade 9 demands the kind of synthesis that, a decade ago, would have looked like an exceptional Sixth-Form essay.

This means quality matters more than quantity in revision. Five hours of focused, thesis-led essay practice with proper feedback will move you further than twenty hours of re-reading the text and highlighting things in different colours. Identify the specific skills the current mark schemes reward, and build practice around those skills directly.

It also means that your preparation needs to be deliberate. The students who do best now are the ones who treat exam technique as a separate subject from English itself. They do not just know the texts. They know how to write about them in a way that the current mark schemes recognise as top-band work — and they have rehearsed that under exam conditions until it is automatic.
