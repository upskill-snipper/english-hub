The phone call lands on a Monday lunchtime. By Tuesday morning a team is in your reception, and within forty-eight hours one of them is in your faculty workroom asking why you teach _An Inspector Calls_ in Year 10 rather than Year 11. The deep-dive has become the defining feature of the modern Section 5 inspection, and English — by virtue of being a high-volume, high-stakes core subject — is one of the subjects most likely to be selected.

This piece is written for heads of English and department leads who want a realistic preparation checklist. It is not a guide to gaming an inspection. It is a guide to making sure that the genuinely good work your department already does is visible, articulate, and defensible when an inspector spends two days asking searching questions. Where the post mentions specific inspection practice, please cross-check against the current version of Ofsted's School Inspection Handbook — that document is updated periodically and remains the authoritative source.

## What a deep-dive actually involves

A deep-dive is not a single event. It is a connected sequence of activities designed to triangulate one question: is the curriculum in this subject coherently planned, well taught, and producing the intended learning?

In practice, that translates to four overlapping strands that take place across the inspection window:

- **Lesson observations** across multiple year groups and multiple teachers. Inspectors are not looking for a perfect lesson; they are looking for patterns. Are pupils building knowledge cumulatively? Does what is happening in Year 8 set up what will happen in Year 9? Do non-specialist teachers know what they are teaching and why?
- **Work scrutiny** — a sample of exercise books or digital portfolios spanning the cohort, including disadvantaged pupils, SEND pupils, and high-attainers. The inspector is reading for the same patterns: progression, feedback, response to feedback, the visible accumulation of skill.
- **Pupil interviews**. Small groups of pupils talk to inspectors about what they are learning. The killer question is some variant of: "Tell me what you've been studying this term, and how it links to what you did before." If pupils cannot answer that, no amount of documentation rescues you.
- **Department meeting and leader interview**. The HoD sits with one or two inspectors and walks them through curriculum intent, implementation, and impact. This is the conversation in which you justify your sequence, your assessment policy, your reading culture, and your data narrative.

Refer to the most recent Ofsted School Inspection Handbook for the official framing of each of these strands. Do not rely on remembered quotations or paragraph numbers from previous versions; the handbook evolves.

## Curriculum intent — the document you need ready

Every English HoD should have one document that does the heavy lifting in the leader interview. Call it whatever you like — a curriculum rationale, a progression map, an intent statement — but it should sit on a single set of pages and answer a small number of questions clearly.

A practical structure:

- **A Year 7 to Year 11 progression map.** What is taught when. Texts, skills, the assessment objectives in play, and the way these accumulate. A simple grid is more persuasive than prose.
- **Why these texts? Why this order?** This is where most departments wobble. The honest answer is rarely "because it's always been like this." A defensible answer connects text choice to the skills being built — for example, beginning with a contemporary novella in Year 7 to establish a baseline of inferential reading before introducing a Shakespeare unit later in the year. Whatever the rationale, it should be written down.
- **How does the curriculum address disadvantaged pupils?** Not a paragraph about Pupil Premium spending. A paragraph about the curriculum itself — vocabulary instruction, cultural capital, knowledge-building units, reading provision in key stage 3.
- **How are Year 6 transitions handled?** Inspectors increasingly ask about this. What do you know about pupils' starting points? How does Year 7 reflect that knowledge rather than restart from scratch?

Keep this document short. Six to ten pages is plenty. If you cannot fit your intent on ten pages, your intent is probably not yet coherent.

## Implementation — what evidence helps

Implementation is where the curriculum meets the classroom. The relevant evidence is not a stack of glossy schemes; it is whatever you actually use day to day, organised so that an outsider can read it.

Useful items to have to hand:

- **Schemes of work showing AO building.** A scheme that explicitly maps how each unit develops AO1, AO2, AO3 and so on across key stage 4 — and the precursor skills built in key stage 3 — gives inspectors something concrete to discuss. If your schemes still look like fortnightly lesson lists with no skill mapping, that is the first thing to fix in the next twelve months.
- **Assessment policy.** How often are pupils formally assessed? What does the marking look like? How is feedback used by pupils — what is the response cycle? How are gaps identified, and what changes when they are? An inspector will read books to see whether the assessment policy is actually happening.
- **Reading culture.** This is often where English departments are strong but underdocumented. What library access do pupils have? What is the reading-for-pleasure provision in key stage 3 — tutor reading, dedicated lessons, reading lists, author visits, book groups? What about reluctant readers? The argument is more persuasive when you can describe a specific routine ("Year 7 read for fifteen minutes at the start of every English lesson; a class novel runs alongside the unit text") than when you gesture at general principles.
- **SEND provision in English.** Be honest. Describe what is actually in place — not what you wish were in place. Adapted texts, scaffolded writing frames, pre-teaching of vocabulary, the link with the SENDCo, the way IEP targets translate into classroom practice. If something is genuinely weak, name it as a development priority rather than pretending otherwise. Inspectors notice fabrication and they will press.

## Impact — the data narrative

Impact is the strand where heads of department are usually most prepared, because it is the strand that resembles a normal performance conversation. Have the headline numbers ready and the narrative around them rehearsed.

The components:

- **Most-recent published outcomes.** Progress 8 and Attainment 8 contributions from English, English Language and English Literature grade distributions, percentage of pupils achieving grade 4+ and grade 5+, percentage at grade 7+. Compared against national averages where you have them, and against your school's own historical trend.
- **Internal mock data.** Trends across mocks within Year 11, and where possible across Year 10 to Year 11. Inspectors want to see that data is being used to drive intervention, not collected for its own sake.
- **Reading-age progression Year 7 to Year 9.** Whatever assessment you use — NGRT, Star Reader, Lucid LASS, in-house — the trend matters more than the specific instrument. Can you show that pupils' reading ages are moving forward, and especially that the lowest-attaining pupils are catching up rather than falling further behind?
- **The disadvantaged gap.** If there is one — and in most departments there is — what is the size of it, what is the trend, and what have you specifically done? Generic statements ("we apply quality-first teaching") will not survive twenty seconds. Specific actions will: targeted intervention groups, vocabulary catch-up, reading buddies, a redesigned key stage 3 to address gaps, additional tutoring in Year 11.

Do not invent a data narrative. If your last set of results were below national, say so, explain what you have analysed, and describe what is changing as a result. A department that has read its own data clearly is more impressive than one that pretends every cohort was a triumph.

## Pupil voice — the conversation prep

The pupil interviews are the strand most heads of department under-prepare for, partly because they feel impossible to control. They are.

What pupils need to be able to articulate, naturally and in their own words:

- What they are currently learning, and what topic or skill they have been studying for the last few weeks.
- Why this connects to last term — the cumulative story, however roughly told.
- What the most recent assessment looked like, and what feedback they received.
- A book or a writer they have studied that they have an opinion about.

The way to build this is not by drilling answers in the week before the call. It is by running short, low-stakes lunch-time conversations with small groups of pupils across the year — pupil voice as routine, not as crash course. Ask the same questions an inspector would. Listen for what pupils actually retain. If the curriculum story is coherent, pupils will eventually tell it back to you. If it is not, the conversation will surface that, and you will have time to address it before anyone external is in the building.

Avoid scripted answers. Inspectors notice. Pupils repeating identical phrasing across three different groups is a red flag, and it tends to undermine everything else the department has documented.

## Workload realities — what NOT to do

Most departments survive a deep-dive perfectly well. Most of the damage that gets done is self-inflicted in the weeks before the inspection window opens.

Things to avoid:

- **Overhauling everything in six weeks.** A new curriculum that nobody has taught yet, a new assessment policy that nobody has used, a new feedback system that has not bedded in — these create fragility, not strength. Show what you actually do, defend it well, and identify development priorities honestly.
- **Faking a reading culture.** Pupils know whether they have been read to, whether they have access to books, and whether their teachers talk about reading. If the answer is no, putting up a few posters is worse than useless — it advertises the gap.
- **Writing an "Ofsted-ready" set of mock observations.** Lessons designed to be observed are usually obvious. Inspectors are watching dozens of lessons a week; they recognise performance teaching. Carry on with the lessons you would normally teach.
- **Pretending department weaknesses do not exist.** Honesty about a development priority is far more credible than a polished claim that everything is fine. If you know your key stage 3 sequencing is weaker than your key stage 4, say so, and say what you are doing about it.

## Where AI marking fits in the conversation

AI tools are now common enough in English departments that inspectors may ask how you are using them. Have a clear, honest framing ready.

A workable line of argument:

- AI marking is used as a first-pass formative tool. It surfaces patterns, flags clear errors, and gives pupils rapid feedback on technical features of writing.
- The teacher remains in the loop on every script. AI output is reviewed before it is shared with pupils, and the final professional judgement on any assessed piece sits with the teacher.
- The workload time saved has been re-allocated to specific activities — more frequent live feedback, additional small-group intervention, deeper redrafting cycles, more time on reading with the lowest-attaining pupils. Be specific about where the time has gone.

This framing is defensible because it is honest about what AI does and does not do, and because it foregrounds pupils' learning rather than departmental convenience. If you cannot describe what the saved time is being spent on, that is itself worth thinking about before an inspector asks.
