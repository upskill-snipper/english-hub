Every January, a familiar pattern repeats itself in English classrooms. Students who have been working hard, who know their texts, who can talk fluently about Macbeth or Sheila Birling in lessons, walk into a mock paper feeling confident. They walk out two and a half hours later with a creeping sense that something went wrong. A week later, the result is back, and it is a grade — sometimes two — below what their teacher predicted.

The first instinct is to assume the gap is about effort. It almost never is. Students who genuinely have not revised tend to know they have not revised; they are not surprised by the result. The students who feel ambushed by the mock are usually the ones who put in the hours. What tripped them up is something else: a small set of mechanical, repeatable mistakes that the real exam will punish in exactly the same way if nothing changes.

This post walks through the five most common reasons mock grades come in low, plus a bonus mistake that costs marks the night before. None require you to learn more content. All are fixable in the weeks between mocks and the real thing.

## Reason 1: Pacing collapse in the second essay

This is the single biggest reason students drop a grade in English mocks, and it almost never gets diagnosed correctly afterwards.

Here is what happens. The paper has two essays. A student starts well, gets absorbed in the first question, builds momentum, and writes a strong opening essay — only to look at the clock and realise they have spent fifty-five minutes on it. The second essay now has thirty-five minutes instead of forty-five. They rush. The paragraphs get shorter. The conclusion becomes a single sentence. The thesis they had ready never quite arrives.

What this looks like on the mark sheet is brutal. The first essay scores well. The second scores about sixty per cent of what it should have. Mark schemes weight both essays equally, so a grade 7 first essay paired with a grade 5 second essay does not give you a grade 6 — it gives you a paper that reads like a grade 5 candidate who got lucky on question one.

**The fix.** Set a per-essay timer, not a paper timer. If you have ninety minutes for two essays, the rule is forty-five minutes each, full stop, even if you are mid-sentence. Practise this in timed conditions at home. The discipline of stopping mid-paragraph is uncomfortable, which is exactly why it needs rehearsing.

A second, smaller fix: between essays, physically reset. Stand up, ask to leave the room briefly, drink water. Thirty seconds prevents the second essay from inheriting the panic of a rushed first one.

## Reason 2: Quotes from memory drift

Under the cognitive load of a timed exam, quotes degrade. A student who could write "Out, damned spot! Out, I say!" perfectly in a quiet classroom suddenly produces "Get out, damned spot!" in the mock — and is surprised when the marker doesn't credit it as evidence.

This matters because AO1 is partly about *accurate* use of textual reference. A misquoted line is not a half-credit quote. It is, in the eyes of most mark schemes, a paraphrase, and paraphrase scores lower. Three or four drifted quotes can be the difference between a grade 7 and a grade 6.

The drift happens because most quote revision is silent and visual. Students re-read flashcards, copy quotes into notes, look at them. Visual recognition is not the same as active recall under pressure.

**The fix.** Drill quotes out loud, in spoken form. Walk around the room reciting "Out, damned spot. Out, I say." Record yourself saying ten quotes from Macbeth and play them back on the bus. Speak the quote, then write it from memory, then check. Spoken rehearsal forces a different memory pathway, and that pathway is the one that holds up when adrenaline kicks in.

To test which quotes are still fragile, our [revision quiz](/revision/quiz) drills quote recognition under timed conditions — closer to exam pressure than reading them off a list.

## Reason 3: Thesis gets lost mid-essay

Read enough mock scripts and a pattern emerges: paragraph one opens with a clear, confident thesis. Paragraph four argues something subtly different. By the conclusion, the argument has wandered far enough that a marker reading top-down can see the drift in real time.

This costs marks at AO1 (developed argument) and AO2 (analysis serving the argument). It also costs marks in band descriptors that reward "conceptualised" responses, because a wandering essay reads as reactive — the student is responding to whatever the next quote suggests, not building a case.

The drift happens because, somewhere around paragraph two, the student stops thinking about their overall argument and starts thinking only about the quote in front of them. The thesis was real; it just got buried under the next analytical task.

**The fix.** Before you start writing, write your thesis at the top of the question paper in capital letters. One sentence. Then, at the start of every new paragraph, glance at it before you write the topic sentence. Eyes up, read the thesis, then write — that small ritual keeps every paragraph attached to the original argument.

A useful test: at the end of each paragraph, ask "did this push my thesis forward, or did it just talk about a quote?" If it is the second, the paragraph needs reframing.

## Reason 4: Context dropped without integration

AO3 — the assessment objective for context — is one of the most misunderstood parts of GCSE English Literature. Students learn they need context, so they put context in. The problem is *how* it gets put in.

What you often see in mock essays is a sentence like: "Shakespeare wrote Macbeth in 1606." It sits there, free-floating, not connected to the line being analysed, not connected to the argument. The marker clocks it instantly: this is AO3 box-ticking, not AO3 understanding.

The mark scheme is explicit. Context is rewarded when it informs interpretation. Context dropped in as a fact about a date or an author's biography is not interpretation; it is decoration. Once a marker has spotted decoration in one paragraph, they read the rest of the essay differently.

**The fix.** Every context point has to attach to a specific line or moment. Not "Shakespeare wrote in 1606" full stop, but "the witches' authority in Act 1 scene 1 would have unsettled a 1606 audience for whom King James had recently published *Daemonologie*, which is why the play opens with them rather than with Macbeth." That is context doing analytical work.

A quick rule: if you can delete the context sentence and the paragraph still makes the same point, it was decoration. If deleting it weakens the analysis, you've integrated it properly.

## Reason 5: Mock scheme variation

This one is structural rather than personal, and it catches students whose teachers haven't warned them about it.

Schools build their own mock papers. Sometimes they use the most recent past paper, sometimes one from three years ago with a slightly older mark scheme, sometimes a remix of question stems no student could have seen before. The result is that the mock you sit may look subtly different from the format you have been training on — different question phrasing, different extract length, different relative weighting of AO1 and AO2.

Students who have only practised on the latest specimen paper get destabilised. They spend the first five minutes trying to work out what is being asked, when the paper they expected would have been processable in thirty seconds.

**The fix.** Practise on at least three different past-paper formats. Mix recent specimens with older ones. Get familiar with how the same skill — analysis of an extract, comparison across poems, a whole-text Section B essay — can be dressed in different question stems. The goal is to make the format invisible, so when the real paper lands, all your attention goes to the content.

Your English department will usually share past papers on request. If not, the major exam boards publish them on their websites for free.

## Bonus: the night-before mistake

Almost every GCSE English student does the same thing the night before: they re-read the play, or the novel, or the anthology, cover to cover.

It feels productive. It is not. Re-reading gives a comforting sense of familiarity but produces almost no improvement in recall the next day, because the material is already encoded — what's failing is retrieval, not encoding. Re-reading addresses the wrong problem.

Worse, it eats sleep. Students who stay up until midnight re-reading *An Inspector Calls* sit the paper on six hours of sleep, and sleep loss hits exam performance harder than almost any other variable. Working memory shrinks, attention narrows, and the ability to hold a thesis across forty-five minutes of writing degrades sharply.

**The fix.** Replace night-before re-reading with thirty minutes of comparison spines — short prompts like "compare how guilt is shown in two poems" or "what is the play's argument about responsibility?" — written or spoken out loud. This is retrieval practice, and it is the kind of work that pays off the next morning.

Then go to bed early. Eight hours of sleep is worth more than two hours of re-reading every time.

## How to use mock results productively

When the mock comes back low, the natural reaction is to lament. Don't. Mocks are diagnostic, not predictive. Their purpose is to tell you what to fix in the weeks remaining.

Spend thirty minutes auditing the paper. Not skimming, auditing. Specifically:

- Where did the marks go? Identify the paragraph in each essay where the score dropped — was it a weak topic sentence, missing context, a thin conclusion?
- Where did time go? Reconstruct, honestly, how long you spent on each question. If pacing collapsed, that's the first thing to fix.
- Where did quotes drift? Pull every quote you used and check it against the text. Mark every one that came out wrong.
- What surprised you about the format? If the question stems felt unfamiliar, your past-paper diet has been too narrow.

Compare results across your class. Patterns at the cohort level are often more useful than individual scores. If everyone bombed the comparison question, that is the question to drill. If everyone underperformed on context, the whole class needs the fix from Reason 4.
