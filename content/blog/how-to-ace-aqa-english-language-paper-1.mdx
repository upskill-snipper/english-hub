AQA English Language Paper 1 — "Explorations in Creative Reading and Writing" — gives you 90 minutes to work through five questions, with 80 marks on the table. The paper is not particularly difficult in any single moment; what trips students up, almost without exception, is pacing.

The pattern is predictable. A student opens the paper, gets absorbed in Q2, lingers on Q3, writes a magnificent Q4, then looks at the clock with 22 minutes left and realises Q5 — worth half the paper — has barely been touched. The story collapses. The grade slides. The post-mortem is always the same: "I ran out of time."

This guide is the opposite of that experience. It walks you through the paper in real time, tells you exactly how long to spend on each question, what the marker is looking for, and where the marks actually live. Print it, annotate it, time yourself against it. By the time the real exam comes around, the rhythm should feel like muscle memory.

## The paper at a glance

Paper 1 is split into two sections built around a single source — an extract of literary fiction, usually somewhere between 700 and 800 words long. It might be from a 19th-century novel, a contemporary short story, or anything in between. You will not have seen it before.

- **Section A: Reading** — 40 marks across four questions (Q1 to Q4)
- **Section B: Writing** — 40 marks for one question (Q5), with a choice of two prompts

The official AQA recommendation is to spend roughly an hour on Section A and 45 minutes on Section B — but those numbers add up to 105 minutes, and you only have 90. So in practice the realistic split is closer to 60 minutes on reading, 30 on writing, with a handful of minutes upfront to read the source carefully.

## Recommended split

Here is the timing plan that works. It is tight but not panicked, and it gives Q5 the breathing room it needs.

- **0 to 5 min** — read the source, annotate lightly
- **5 to 15 min** — Q1 (4 marks) and start Q2
- **15 to 27 min** — finish Q2 (8 marks)
- **27 to 39 min** — Q3 (8 marks)
- **39 to 55 min** — Q4 (20 marks)
- **55 to 60 min** — plan Q5
- **60 to 90 min** — write Q5 (40 marks)

That is 90 minutes flat. You will notice the reading questions take 50 minutes in total and Q5 gets a planning slot of its own. Both of those choices are deliberate.

## Q1 — list four things (4 marks, 5 minutes)

Q1 asks you to list four things about a specific subject from a specific section of the source. The section is always small — typically the first five to ten lines.

This is the easiest question on the paper and the most common place to lose silly marks. The fix is to do less, not more. Write four short statements. Each one is worth one mark. Either quote directly from the lines you are pointed to or paraphrase closely. Do not analyse, do not interpret, do not pad.

If the question says "list four things about the weather from lines 1 to 5", and the text mentions wind, rain, grey clouds, and cold air, then your answer is four lines long and includes those four things. Done. Move on.

The most common mistake here is straying outside the line range. If you quote from line 12 when you were asked about lines 1 to 5, the marker has to throw it out. Read the boundary numbers carefully.

## Q2 — language analysis (8 marks, 10 to 12 minutes)

Q2 is an AO2 question. You are analysing the writer's choice of language in a specified section, usually a short paragraph or two. The mark scheme rewards detailed exploration of language with subject terminology used accurately.

Pick one or two short, rich quotations and go deep. Two thoughtful paragraphs beats five shallow ones every time. For each quote, identify the technique, explain the effect, and connect that effect to the reader's experience. The classic three-step move — quote, technique, effect on reader — is what gets you into the top band.

Feature-spotting earns nothing. Writing "the writer uses a metaphor" and then moving on is a Band 1 response. The marker wants to see what the metaphor *does*. If a writer compares a character's silence to "a closed iron gate", the analysis is not "this is a metaphor". The analysis is about coldness, hardness, refusal of access, the suggestion that opening the gate would take force. That is where the marks are.

Stay tight. You have 10 to 12 minutes. Two paragraphs, two quotes, sharp focus.

## Q3 — structure (8 marks, 10 to 12 minutes)

Q3 is the question students fear most, and the fear is almost always misplaced. Structure simply means the *shape* of the whole text — how it is built, how the writer moves the reader's attention from beginning to end.

Do not analyse language here. The marker is looking for whole-text awareness: where the focus shifts, where the perspective changes, where the writer zooms in or zooms out. Useful things to track:

- **Openings** — what does the writer show us first, and why?
- **Shifts in focus** — moving from a wide setting to a single character, from outside to inside, from one place to another
- **Time** — flashbacks, jumps forward, slowing down, speeding up
- **Perspective** — narrator distance, who we follow, whose thoughts we hear
- **Endings** — does the text resolve, hold tension, return to its opening image?

Write three or four short paragraphs, each one tracking a different structural choice and explaining its effect on the reader. Use phrases like "the writer opens by", "the focus then narrows to", "the perspective shifts from external to internal". Marker-friendly vocabulary.

## Q4 — evaluation (20 marks, 25 minutes)

Q4 is the highest-stakes reading question. It is worth as much as Q1, Q2, and Q3 combined, and it is the one most students underrate.

You will be given a statement — something a hypothetical student has said about a specified part of the source. You are asked the extent to which you agree. The correct stance is almost never total agreement or total disagreement; the strongest answers usually agree with most of the statement, then partially complicate one element of it.

Build an argument. This is the key word: *argument*. Q4 is not a longer version of Q2. Yes, you will use evidence from the text and yes, you will reference language, but the engine of the response is the position you are taking on the statement. Each paragraph should advance that position.

Structure that works:

- Paragraph 1 — agree with the central thrust of the statement, with evidence
- Paragraph 2 — develop a second supporting strand, with evidence
- Paragraph 3 — complicate or qualify one element, with evidence
- Paragraph 4 — return to the statement and explain your overall view

Twenty-five minutes. Roughly four paragraphs of equal weight. Quote often, quote briefly, evaluate constantly.

The classic Q4 mistake is to forget the evaluation entirely and produce another language analysis answer. If your response could be re-titled "Q2 but longer", you are losing marks. The marker is looking for the words *I think*, *the reader feels*, *the writer succeeds in*, *however*, *to some extent*. Show that you have weighed up the statement.

## Q5 — descriptive or narrative writing (40 marks, 35 minutes)

Q5 is half the paper. You will be given two options — typically one descriptive task (often built around an image) and one narrative task (often a written prompt or opening line). Pick the one that plays to your strength.

The marks split into two parts: 24 marks for content and organisation, 16 marks for technical accuracy — which means spelling, punctuation, grammar, and sentence variety. The technical 16 are surprisingly easy to lose if you rush, so leave a minute or two at the end to read your work back.

Spend your five-minute planning window on a tight skeleton. Don't try to outline a novel. You need:

- An opening image that locks the reader in
- A central character with a clear emotional state
- One small conflict or moment of tension
- A resolution — or, often more powerfully, a deliberate non-resolution

Pick precision over ambition. Markers do not reward students who try to sound like Shakespeare and end up sounding strained. They reward writers who choose the right detail, vary their sentence lengths, and control their tone.

## Common Q5 mistakes

Watch out for these. Each one costs marks at predictable points.

- **Too much plot** — Q5 is not a short story competition. A "story" with five scene changes will read as rushed and surface-level. One moment, deeply rendered, scores higher than five moments scrambled together.
- **No descriptive detail** — telling the reader "the room was scary" is a Band 2 move. Showing them the curtain twitching, the cold dust on the windowsill, the sound of a clock that should have stopped — that is Band 4.
- **Repetitive sentence openings** — six sentences in a row starting with "I" or "The" will tank your sentence variety mark. Vary the opening: a subordinate clause, a single-word sentence, a participle phrase.
- **No clear progression** — if your Q5 has no shape, no shift, no movement from beginning to end, you will plateau in the middle band. Even a single mood-shift is enough to give the piece structure.

## A Q5 opening that works

The opening of Q5 is where most marks are won or lost in the first sixty seconds of reading. Markers form an impression fast.

Show one image, not a setting summary. Compare these two openings:

> I was at the beach with my family. It was a sunny day and we were excited.

> The sand was cooling under my feet, and somewhere behind me my mother was calling a name that no longer felt like mine.

The second opening shows one specific image, hints at character, and plants a question — *whose name? why no longer?* — all in one sentence. The reader is locked in by the end of sentence three. That is the goal.

You do not need to be poetic. You need to be specific. One concrete detail beats ten abstract adjectives.
