PEEL gets a bad rap. You'll hear teachers and tutors mutter that it's "restrictive", that it's "scaffolding for kids who can't write yet", that it's "GCSE-only and you should drop it the moment you hit A-Level". There is a sliver of truth in each complaint — but only if PEEL is being used badly.

Used well, PEEL is the cleanest paragraph structure for under-pressure essays. It gives you a shape to fill when your hand is shaking and the clock has eaten twenty minutes. It stops you from drifting. It forces analysis instead of paraphrase. The trick is what the **P** stands for — and that is where almost every Grade 5 to Grade 7 essay quietly falls apart.

## What PEEL actually means

The acronym is simple. The execution is not.

- **P**oint — your mini-argument for this paragraph (NOT the topic)
- **E**vidence — a quotation, embedded into your sentence
- **E**xplain — language, form, and structure analysis
- **L**ink — back to your thesis, developing it as you go

Read those four lines twice. The most common version of PEEL taught in classrooms has **P** standing for "the topic of the paragraph" — and that single misunderstanding is responsible for thousands of essays that hover frustratingly around Grade 6.

A topic is not an argument. "Macbeth's ambition" is a topic. "Macbeth's ambition is the engine of his self-destruction, not its excuse" is an argument. Examiners reward arguments. They award politely-titled paraphrase a polite 5.

## The most common mistake — Point as topic

Look at these two openings:

> Wrong: *"Macbeth is ambitious."*
>
> Right: *"Macbeth's ambition is willed self-destruction; even when given an exit, he chooses corruption."*

The first is a noun phrase dressed up as a sentence. There's nothing to disagree with, nothing to develop, nothing to prove. It tells the examiner what the paragraph is *about*, but says nothing about what you *think*.

The second is a full sentence with a verb (*is*), a claim (*willed self-destruction*), and a development (*even when given an exit, he chooses corruption*). It commits to an interpretation. The rest of the paragraph now has a job: prove that.

Test your own Points before you write them out:

1. Does it have a verb?
2. Could a reasonable critic disagree with it?
3. Does it make a claim about *meaning*, not just content?

If any answer is no, rewrite. A weak Point cannot be rescued by a strong quotation.

## Evidence — pick the SHORTEST quote that works

Long quotes are the second most common mistake in PEEL paragraphs. Students copy out four lines of verse, leave themselves no time for analysis, and end up summarising what the quote says rather than analysing how it works.

Three rules:

- **Long quotes waste time and bury analysis.** Every word you copy is a word you didn't analyse.
- **4–8 word quotes are usually best.** Short enough to interrogate; long enough to carry meaning.
- **Embed them inside your own sentence.** Don't drop them in standalone.

Compare:

> Weak: *"Macbeth says, 'I have done the deed. Didst thou not hear a noise?' This shows he is panicking."*
>
> Strong: *"Macbeth's clipped declaration that he has 'done the deed' uses the determiner 'the' to convert a singular act of regicide into something almost generic — abstracted, distanced, half-disowned."*

The strong version uses three words from the play and spins five lines of analysis from them. The weak version uses ten words from the play and offers one sentence of paraphrase. Same time on the page, vastly different mark.

## Explain — the 3-layer rule

This is where Grade 7 essays plateau. Most students do one layer of analysis and move on. Grade 9 essays do all three, every paragraph.

- **Layer 1 — identify the technique.** "Determiner", "iambic pentameter", "caesura", "monosyllabic verbs", "polysyndeton", "stage direction".
- **Layer 2 — explain the effect on the reader.** What does the technique *do* to us as we read? What feeling, what shift, what hesitation does it produce?
- **Layer 3 — link to the writer's purpose.** Why did Shakespeare/Priestley/Stevenson make this choice? What thematic, political, or moral idea is being articulated?

All three, every paragraph. If you skip Layer 3 — the writer's purpose — your essay will cap at Grade 7 no matter how clever the language analysis. AO2 is explicitly about how writers *use* language for effect. "Effect" without authorial intent is half a mark.

## Link — what GOOD link looks like

The Link is the part most students phone in. They write *"This links back to the theme of ambition,"* tick it off, and move on. That is not a link. That is a label.

> Weak: *"This links to ambition."*
>
> Strong: *"This paragraph extends the thesis by showing that Macbeth's ambition isn't external pressure — isn't the witches, isn't Lady Macbeth — but his own choice given articulation."*

The strong version *develops* the thesis. It tells the examiner: I haven't just proved my Point; I have moved my whole argument forward. The thesis at the end of this paragraph is sharper than it was at the start.

Good Links use verbs like *extends*, *complicates*, *qualifies*, *deepens*, *contradicts*. They don't repeat the thesis; they refine it.

## Annotated example 1 — Macbeth

Here is a full PEEL paragraph on the line "I have done the deed". Annotations follow.

> *Macbeth's apparent fragility after the murder is performed — not felt. The bald confession that he has 'done the deed' uses the determiner 'the' to convert regicide into something almost generic, an abstracted act stripped of its name. Shakespeare's monosyllables flatten the rhythm of the line, denying the moment the iambic dignity it would otherwise carry, and forcing the audience into the same airless register as Macbeth himself. The effect is a register of distance: Macbeth narrates his own crime as though watching it from across a room. For a Jacobean audience taught that regicide was a metaphysical violation, this linguistic withdrawal is the first symptom of damnation — Macbeth has not yet repented, but he has already begun to disappear from himself. The paragraph thus extends the thesis: Macbeth's tragedy is not that he is overpowered by ambition, but that he willingly absents himself from moral language at the moment he most needs it.*

**Annotation:**

- **P (Point)** — *"Macbeth's apparent fragility after the murder is performed — not felt."* Full sentence, verb, contestable claim.
- **E (Evidence)** — *"'done the deed'"*, embedded in the next sentence. Three words, not a paragraph of verse.
- **E (Explain)** — three layers: determiner *the* (technique), audience effect (*airless register*, *distance*), writer's purpose (*Jacobean audience taught that regicide was a metaphysical violation*).
- **L (Link)** — *"The paragraph thus extends the thesis…"*: develops the thesis from "Macbeth is destroyed by ambition" to "Macbeth absents himself from moral language."

Word count: 167. Grade 9 territory.

## Annotated example 2 — An Inspector Calls

Now the same structure on Priestley's "We are members of one body." Annotations follow.

> *The Inspector's closing imperative is not a warning — it is a verdict that has already been delivered. His insistence that 'we are members of one body' deploys the collective pronoun 'we' to abolish the moral distance the Birlings have spent the play constructing. The metaphor of a single body refuses the social arithmetic Mr Birling has used throughout — the language of staff and shareholders, of cheap labour and competitive markets — and replaces it with a biological inevitability the family cannot opt out of. For a 1945 audience emerging from total war and electing a Labour government within weeks, the line registers as something close to a manifesto: collective responsibility is not aspirational, it is anatomical. The paragraph therefore complicates the thesis: Priestley's didacticism is not merely political instruction but a refusal to let the audience pretend they are spectators rather than participants.*

**Annotation:**

- **P** — *"The Inspector's closing imperative is not a warning — it is a verdict that has already been delivered."* Verb, claim, edge.
- **E** — *"'we are members of one body'"*. Six words, embedded.
- **E** — three layers: pronoun *we* (technique), audience effect (*abolish moral distance*), writer's purpose (*1945 audience… electing a Labour government within weeks*).
- **L** — *"complicates the thesis"*: deepens the reading from "Priestley is political" to "Priestley refuses spectatorship."

Word count: 161. Same shape as the Macbeth paragraph; same level of mark.

## Where PEEL fails

PEEL is not a universal solvent. There are three places it stops working.

- **Long, multi-quote paragraphs.** If you are tying together three or four quotations across a play to argue for a structural pattern, PEEL gets cramped. Try **ICE** (Introduce, Cite, Explain) for flow, or **PETER** (Point, Evidence, Technique, Explain, Reader) when you need to handle a technique-heavy comparison.
- **When the analysis IS the argument.** In top-band essays, the line between "evidence for an argument" and "the argument itself" blurs. PEEL still works, but the structure becomes invisible — you are no longer filling a shape, you are arguing in paragraphs that happen to contain points and evidence.
- **Unseen poetry.** PEEL is too rigid for poems where structure, voice, and form interlock from the first line. You lose marks for fighting your way through the acronym instead of following the poem.

If your paragraph naturally has two pieces of evidence, two techniques, and a counter-claim, don't force it into PEEL. Let the argument breathe.

## Beyond PEEL — when you're ready

Once PEEL is automatic — once you stop noticing the structure and just write — these are the natural next steps:

- **PETER** — Point, Evidence, Technique, Explain, Reader. Adds explicit room for technique and reader response. Useful for AO2-heavy questions.
- **ICE** — Introduce, Cite, Explain. Less rigid, more flow. Better for comparisons and longer arguments where multiple quotations need to braid together.
- **Thesis-led paragraphing.** No acronym. Each paragraph develops a sub-claim of your thesis; quotations and analysis serve the claim, not the structure.

But here is the honest verdict for most GCSE candidates: **PEEL well > PETER badly**. Don't chase the more sophisticated structure if you haven't mastered the simple one. A clean PEEL paragraph with a sharp Point, embedded short quote, three-layer Explain, and developing Link will out-score a clumsy PETER paragraph every time.

Get the foundation automatic. Then, slowly, the structure will dissolve and you'll just be writing arguments.
