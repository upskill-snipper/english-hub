Most students plan for two minutes and then write for forty-three. The result is predictable: a first paragraph that is sharp, a second that drifts, a third that contradicts the first, and a conclusion that scrambles to tie a knot in something that was never a single thread. Top students do something different. They plan for five to seven minutes and write for thirty-eight. The total time spent is identical, but the essay that comes out is unrecognisably better. A better plan is the single largest lever you can pull on a GCSE English essay, and it is the one nobody teaches you to use properly.

This article walks through a thirty-minute planning and writing routine — five minutes of planning, twenty-five of writing, with checking time built in — and a five-step micro-plan that fits inside the first five minutes. If you take nothing else away, take this: the plan is not optional, and the plan is not a list of quotes.

## Why thirty minutes for one essay

Most GCSE English Literature papers (AQA Paper 1, AQA Paper 2, Edexcel Paper 1, Edexcel Paper 2) allocate roughly forty-five minutes per essay-style question. The split that produces the highest marks looks like this:

- Five minutes: plan
- Thirty-five minutes: write
- Five minutes: check and edit

This article is about the first five minutes. Get those right and the next thirty-five almost write themselves; get them wrong and you spend the next thirty-five fighting your own essay.

The plan you make in five minutes does four jobs. It locks your argument so you don't drift. It pre-selects your evidence so you don't hunt for quotes mid-paragraph. It forces you to pick a counter-argument, which is the marker of Grade 8 and 9 work. And it gives you a finish line so your conclusion is decided before your hand cramps.

## Step 1 (60 seconds): read the question three times

The single biggest reason students lose marks is not misremembering quotes — it is misreading the question. Read the question three times, and read it differently each time.

- First read: comprehension. What is it literally asking? Underline the named character, theme, or extract.
- Second read: pick out two or three key terms. If the question says "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth's guilt?", the terms are *present*, *Macbeth's guilt*, and the implicit *how*. *How* means method, which means language, structure, and form.
- Third read: spot the implicit assessment objective. AQA's mark scheme rewards AO1 (response to the text), AO2 (analysis of language, form, structure), and AO3 (context). A question phrased "How does Shakespeare present..." is a heavy AO2 question. A question phrased "To what extent..." is asking you to argue both sides — that is implicit AO1.

Sixty seconds. Done.

## Step 2 (60 seconds): write the thesis at the top of your paper

This is the move that separates Grade 6 essays from Grade 9 essays. Write your thesis at the very top of the paper before you write anything else.

Your thesis is one sentence. It is an argument, not a topic. "This essay will discuss guilt in Macbeth" is not a thesis — it is a description of what you are about to do, which the marker can already see. A thesis takes a position.

Worked example. Question: *How does Shakespeare present guilt in Macbeth?*

- Topic statement (weak): "Shakespeare presents guilt in many ways throughout the play."
- Thesis (strong): "Macbeth's guilt is presented as physical and inescapable, while Lady Macbeth's is psychological and isolating — and the contrast tracks the play's larger argument about what unchecked ambition does to the body and the mind."

That second version commits to a position, builds a comparison into the structure, and signals that you will land on a contextual reading. You can write a Grade 9 essay from that sentence. You cannot write one from "many ways."

## Step 3 (90 seconds): map four to five evidence points

Now bullet four to five evidence points down the margin. Each one is a quote (or a precise reference if you cannot remember the wording exactly) plus the paragraph it will go in.

Continuing the Macbeth example:

- P1: "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?" — physical guilt, hyperbole, hand imagery
- P2: "Out, damned spot!" — Lady Macbeth's psychological guilt, sleepwalking, repetition
- P3: "I am in blood / Stepp'd in so far" — Macbeth's progression from guilt to numbness
- P4: "These deeds must not be thought / After these ways; so, it will make us mad" — Lady Macbeth's early denial

Notice what is missing: any quote that doesn't serve the thesis. You don't include "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" just because you know it. The plan is already filtering for thesis-relevance, which is what the writing stage will struggle to do under time pressure.

## Step 4 (60 seconds): pick your counter-argument

Bullet a counter-argument. One paragraph in your essay should begin with a phrase like "Some might argue..." or "An alternative reading suggests..." and complicate your thesis. This is the move that lifts a competent Grade 7 essay into Grade 8 or 9 territory.

For our Macbeth example: "Some might argue that Macbeth's guilt does not, in fact, last — by Act 5 he has hardened into 'I have almost forgot the taste of fears,' which complicates the reading of guilt as inescapable. However, the very act of forgetting fear is itself the moral collapse the play warns against — guilt has not vanished; it has consumed the man who once felt it."

Counter-argument paragraphs are how you show the marker that you can hold two ideas at once. Without one, your essay caps at Grade 7 no matter how well-evidenced it is.

## Step 5 (30 seconds): note your closing synthesis

One line. Where does the essay land? Not a summary of what you've already said — a synthesis, a final sentence-shaped destination. For our example: *Shakespeare uses the contrast between Macbeth's bodily guilt and Lady Macbeth's mental guilt to argue that ambition unmoored from conscience destroys the whole human — body and mind together.*

Five seconds to write, three minutes saved at the end of the essay when you are not panicking about how to conclude.

## A worked example — full five-minute plan

Here is the full plan for an AQA-style Macbeth question on the supernatural, written exactly as it would appear on rough paper:

**Question:** How does Shakespeare present the supernatural in *Macbeth*?

**Thesis:** Shakespeare presents the supernatural as morally ambiguous — not as the cause of Macbeth's downfall but as an external mirror of his own already-formed ambition, which complicates the Jacobean expectation that witches simply corrupt the innocent.

**Evidence:**

- P1: "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — moral inversion, paradox, opens the play
- P2: "Stars, hide your fires; / Let not light see my black and deep desires" — Macbeth's ambition pre-dates the prophecy
- P3: "Is this a dagger which I see before me?" — supernatural as projection of internal state
- P4: "By the pricking of my thumbs, / Something wicked this way comes" — supernatural recognises Macbeth as the wickedness, not its victim

**Counter:** Jacobean audiences would read the witches as objectively evil agents (King James's *Daemonologie*, 1597). However, Shakespeare's structural choice to give Macbeth ambition before he meets the witches subverts that reading.

**Synthesis:** Shakespeare uses the supernatural not as cause but as catalyst — the witches reveal what is already there.

That is roughly two hundred words. It took five minutes. The essay that comes out of it will be a Grade 9 essay because every paragraph already has a thesis-aligned quote, a counter-argument is built in, and the conclusion is pre-decided.

## What this plan is NOT

A few things this plan is deliberately not doing.

It is not a full PEEL paragraph rehearsal. Do not write out your point-evidence-explain-link in the plan; write the bullets and let your hand work it out in the actual essay. Rehearsing paragraphs in the plan stage is the single biggest cause of running out of time.

It is not a quote list. By the day of the exam, you should already know your fifteen to twenty key quotes for each text. The plan is for selecting which of those quotes serves this thesis, not for remembering quotes you should already have in long-term memory.

And it is not optional. Students who skip the plan write essays that drift. Students who plan write essays that argue.

## Common planning mistakes

Three mistakes show up again and again.

Spending ten minutes planning. The plan is a tool, not the essay. Ten-minute plans overcook the structure and leave you twenty-five minutes to write — which is not enough for a full GCSE essay. Five minutes is the cap.

Skipping the counter-argument. Without a paragraph that complicates your thesis, the marker has no evidence that you can hold two readings. AO1 caps at Grade 7. You will not break into Grade 8 or 9 without one.

Writing the plan in prose. Plans are bullets. Full sentences in the plan are wasted ink and wasted seconds. The only full sentence in your plan is the thesis itself.
