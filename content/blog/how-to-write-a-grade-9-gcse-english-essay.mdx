Most students who walk out of a GCSE English Literature exam thinking they did well actually wrote a Grade 6 or 7 essay. The work is competent, the quotes are accurate, the techniques are spotted. And yet it caps. The reason is almost always the same: the essay summarises the text and bolts analysis on top. Grade 9 essays do not do this. Grade 9 essays argue.

This is the difference. A Grade 7 essay tells you what the writer does. A Grade 9 essay tells you what the writer does, why it matters, and why a less alert reader would have missed it. Across all four major boards — AQA, Edexcel, OCR and Eduqas — the top band rewards the same thing: a "conceptualised" or "critical" response. That is exam-board language for an argument. If you want to know how to write a Grade 9 English essay, the move you have to make is from describer to arguer.

This guide walks through the structure that does that, with worked examples on Macbeth.

## The thesis is the spine

Everything stands on the thesis. A thesis is one sentence stating your argument — not the theme, not the question, not a vague gesture. If your thesis could appear in the GCSE specification's content list, it is too broad. It needs to be specific enough that someone could disagree with it.

Bad: "Macbeth is a play about ambition."

That is a topic, not a thesis. Every essay on Macbeth could begin with that sentence, which means it tells the examiner nothing about how you think.

Good: "Shakespeare presents Macbeth's ambition as a willed self-destruction; even when supernatural forces offer him an exit, he consistently chooses the corrupting path."

Notice the moving parts. There is a precise verb ("presents"). There is a claim about what the writer does ("willed self-destruction"). There is a counter-pressure baked in ("even when supernatural forces offer him an exit") and a resolution ("he consistently chooses"). That sentence is doing real work. It commits you to an argument and it tells the examiner where the essay is going to go.

Once you have a thesis like that, every paragraph has a job: prove it. Not illustrate it, not orbit around it — prove it. If a paragraph does not advance the thesis, it should not be in the essay.

## Paragraph structure — argument first

Most essay-writing guides tell you to start a paragraph with a topic sentence. That is true, but it misses the harder point: the topic sentence has to be a mini-argument, not a label.

A label looks like: "Shakespeare uses imagery of darkness in Act 2."

A mini-argument looks like: "Shakespeare's darkness imagery in Act 2 functions less as a moral judgement on Macbeth than as a sign that his moral imagination has already collapsed."

That second sentence makes a claim. The rest of the paragraph then proves it. Inside the paragraph, you want this rhythm:

1. **Topic sentence** — the mini-argument that supports the thesis.
2. **Evidence** — one or two short, exact quotations. Not four. Not a paragraph-long quotation. Short and load-bearing.
3. **Layered analysis** — three layers on each quote. Layer 1 is what is happening at word level (a verb, a metaphor, a sound). Layer 2 is what that does to the reader and what it says about character or theme. Layer 3 is the wider effect — the structural, contextual or critical move the writer is making.
4. **Link back** — return to the thesis, but extend it. Do not just repeat the thesis sentence. Show what this paragraph has added.

The link-back is where Grade 7 essays bleed marks. They circle back with a sentence that essentially says "this proves Macbeth is ambitious." The Grade 9 link-back says "this complicates the idea that ambition is simply a flaw — it begins to look like an active, conscious decision Macbeth keeps making."

## The 5-paragraph Grade 9 essay

You do not need a complicated structure. You need a tight one. For a 45-minute literature essay, five paragraphs is plenty.

**Introduction (60 to 90 words).** Open with your thesis. Then give three lines of signposting — what each body paragraph will argue, in order. Do not summarise the text. Do not restate the question. The examiner has the question in front of them.

**Paragraph 1 — strongest evidence.** Lead with the most powerful quotation and the most direct proof of your thesis. Top-load. Examiners read a lot of essays; the strongest paragraph should not be hiding in fourth place.

**Paragraph 2 — complicating evidence.** This is where Grade 9 essays separate from Grade 7. Take a moment in the text that seems to push *against* your thesis, and show how, on closer reading, it actually deepens it. This is the sound of a thinking student.

**Paragraph 3 — counter-argument and rebuttal.** Acknowledge a credible alternative reading. Do not strawman it. Then explain why your reading still holds, or holds more fully. This signals "conceptualised response" to every examiner in the country.

**Conclusion (50 to 80 words).** Synthesise. Do not list what you said. Pull the threads together into a sharper version of your thesis — one that has been earned by the body paragraphs.

## Worked example — Macbeth on ambition

Here is a model intro to a question on how Shakespeare presents ambition in *Macbeth*.

> Shakespeare presents Macbeth's ambition not as a single character flaw but as a willed self-destruction — a series of conscious choices made even when escape is available. The witches do not compel; they tempt, and Macbeth supplies the violence. Lady Macbeth catalyses, but it is Macbeth who escalates after she falters. By the final act, ambition has hollowed him into a man who fights because he can no longer feel.

That is 84 words. It commits to an argument, it forecasts three paragraphs (witches as temptation rather than compulsion; Macbeth as escalator rather than follower; ambition's hollowing), and it does not waste a syllable retelling the plot.

Now a topic sentence for paragraph 2 — the complicating paragraph.

> Critics often read Lady Macbeth's "unsex me here" speech as the engine of her husband's collapse, but Macbeth's own soliloquy moments later — "I have no spur / To prick the sides of my intent, but only / Vaulting ambition" — quietly admits authorship. He knows there is no external "spur"; the verb "vaulting" gives the ambition agency that no witch and no wife has supplied. The horse is his.

That sentence — "The horse is his" — is the kind of compressed, image-aware writing examiners read three times. It earns the paragraph.

And finally, a model conclusion.

> Macbeth's tragedy is not that ambition overtook him but that he kept choosing it. Each scene offers him a door — Banquo's loyalty, Duncan's gratitude, his own conscience — and each scene he closes it. Shakespeare's most disturbing claim is not that ambition corrupts the weak but that, given a willing host, it corrupts the strong with their full and conscious consent.

Sixty-three words. It does not summarise. It sharpens.

## What you do NOT do

Grade 9 essays are defined as much by what they refuse to do as by what they do.

- **Do not list techniques without effect.** "Shakespeare uses sibilance, alliteration, iambic pentameter and imagery." This is feature-spotting and it stops being rewarded after Year 8. A single technique, fully analysed, beats five technique labels.
- **Do not summarise plot.** The examiner has read the play. Probably this term. Probably yesterday.
- **Do not drop quotes without analysing them.** A quotation that is followed only by "this shows ambition" is not evidence — it is decoration.
- **Do not write "this shows" repeatedly.** Replace it with active verbs. "Shakespeare engineers", "the line forces us to", "the structure refuses". Show, do not tell.
- **Do not repeat the question in your introduction.** It wastes 20 words and announces that you are stalling.

## Time management — 45 minutes per essay

A 45-minute literature essay needs three blocks.

**Five minutes: plan.** Write your thesis at the top of the page. Underneath, jot the topic sentence for each paragraph and the quotation you will lead with. If you cannot generate three distinct mini-arguments in five minutes, your thesis is wrong — fix the thesis, do not push on regardless.

**Thirty-five minutes: write.** Roughly seven minutes per paragraph. If you find yourself writing a sixth paragraph because you "forgot" to put something in earlier, stop. The essay is finished when the thesis is proved, not when the page is full.

**Five minutes: reread.** You are looking for one thing — does the thesis recur? Open every paragraph and check the topic sentence still answers to the thesis. If it has drifted, write one extra sentence at the end of that paragraph linking back. That sentence alone is worth a band.

## Common mistakes that cap you at Grade 7

Three diagnostic patterns explain almost every essay that lands at Grade 6 or 7 instead of 8 or 9.

**Feature-spotting.** Identifying a metaphor is not analysis. Explaining why Shakespeare chose *that* metaphor — and not the obvious alternative — is. The trick is to ask what the writer rejected. If the answer is "any other word would do", you are looking at the wrong line.

**Quote-soup.** Four short quotations in a paragraph, none of them analysed properly. Examiners describe this as "tagged" evidence. Cut your quotations in half and double the analysis on each.

**Generic context.** "Shakespeare wrote in 1606 when James I had just become king and was interested in witchcraft." That sentence appears in roughly half the country's Macbeth essays. If your context could be lifted out and pasted into a different essay, it is not earning marks. Context only counts when it is *threaded* into the analysis — when it changes how the line lands.

**No counter-argument.** A Grade 9 essay shows the examiner that you have considered the obvious alternative reading and decided your reading is stronger. A Grade 7 essay does not seem to know there is an alternative. The fix is small and structural: one paragraph, mid-essay, that begins "It might be argued that…" and then dismantles itself in service of your thesis. That single move changes the band you sit in.

A fifth pattern is worth flagging because it is invisible to most students: **front-loaded analysis that fades**. The first paragraph is sharp, the second is competent, the third is summary, and the fourth is a sentence-and-a-half. Examiners notice. The fix is the plan. If you decide your three mini-arguments before you start writing, the energy stays distributed across the whole essay rather than spent in the first ten minutes.
