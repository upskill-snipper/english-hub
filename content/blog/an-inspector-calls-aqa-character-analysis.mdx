Character analysis in *An Inspector Calls* is not about describing who the Birlings are. It is about proving how Priestley uses them to expose moral failure and social responsibility — and that distinction is everything on your AQA exam paper.

## Why Character Analysis Matters for AQA

Describing a character earns you nothing. Explaining what Priestley *does* with a character — the language choices, the dramatic irony, the stage directions — is where marks live.

**AQA GCSE English Literature (8702)** assesses *An Inspector Calls* across three Assessment Objectives:

- **AO1 (Reading, understanding and responding):** Your personal response, supported by textual evidence
- **AO2 (Language, form and structure):** How Priestley's methods create meaning
- **AO3 (Relationships between texts and contexts):** How 1912 society and Priestley's 1945 viewpoint shape the play

### Character vs. Plot: Why Analysis Beats Summary

Every character in this play is a *vehicle*. Mr Birling is not just a businessman — he is Priestley's portrait of capitalist complacency. Sheila is not just a daughter — she is the play's moral compass in motion. The moment you treat a character as a real person rather than a deliberate construct, you slip from AO2 into plot summary.

The responsibility theme is your lens for everything. Ask yourself: does this character accept responsibility, deny it, or grow towards it? That question structures your analysis and keeps you on the right side of the mark scheme.

### Practical Exercise

Open the play at the first stage direction. Identify three stage directions from the opening scene and explain what each reveals about a character's social position or attitude. Notice how 'massively built' for Mr Birling signals physical dominance — and, by extension, the ideological weight Priestley wants him to carry.

## Inspector Goole: The Catalyst and Moral Voice

No character in the AQA canon is more deliberately constructed than the Inspector. He arrives without warning, dismantles a family in one evening, and disappears — possibly never having existed at all.

### Who Is the Inspector? Ambiguity as a Method

Priestley makes the Inspector's identity deliberately uncertain. Is he a real police inspector? A ghost? A collective conscience? That ambiguity is **AO2** gold. The name 'Goole' echoes 'ghoul' — a supernatural hint embedded in the text that rewards close reading.

His interrogation method is equally deliberate. He questions each character separately, controlling information, never allowing the Birlings to construct a shared defence. This technique exposes individual guilt before the family can close ranks.

### The Final Speech: Collective Responsibility

The Inspector's closing speech is the play's thesis statement. Priestley gives him language of almost biblical force:

> 'We are members of one body. We are responsible for each other.'

**Worked example:**

*Weak answer:* 'The Inspector says they are all responsible for each other.'

*Stronger answer:* 'Priestley uses the metaphor of a shared body — "We are members of one body" — to argue that society is an organism, not a collection of isolated individuals. The first-person plural pronoun "We" is rhetorically inclusive, implicating the audience as well as the Birlings. Written in 1945 and set in 1912, this speech carries the weight of two world wars: Priestley implies that Edwardian indifference to the poor was not just morally wrong but catastrophically dangerous.'

### AO3 Anchor: The Titanic Reference

Context matters here. The play is set in 1912 — the year the Titanic sank. Mr Birling famously declares the ship 'unsinkable, absolutely unsinkable' in Act One. Priestley, writing in 1945, gives his audience the grim satisfaction of knowing Birling is wrong. The Inspector embodies the moral reckoning that Edwardian society ignored — and paid for across two world wars.

## Mr and Mrs Birling: Complicity and Denial

If the Inspector is Priestley's voice, the Birling parents are his targets. They represent what he believed was wrong with pre-war Britain: class snobbery, capitalist self-interest, and a wilful blindness to the suffering of those below them.

### Mr Birling's Business Logic vs. Moral Logic

Mr Birling's language is relentlessly self-referential. Count the first-person pronouns in his opening speeches — 'I', 'my business', 'my position' — and you see a man whose moral universe extends no further than his own interests.

His line 'A man has to look after his own' is a direct ideological counter to the Inspector's 'We are responsible for each other.' Priestley sets these two worldviews in explicit opposition, and the dramatic structure of the play is the argument between them.

### Mrs Birling's Class Snobbery and Hypocrisy

Mrs Birling is, if anything, colder than her husband. Her dismissal of Eva Smith's appeal to the Brumley Women's Charity — on the grounds that Eva had the audacity to use the Birling name — is presented as both snobbery and a catastrophic moral failure.

Her language is formal, clipped, and dismissive. Where Mr Birling blusters, Mrs Birling simply refuses. 'I accept no blame for it at all' is not denial born of confusion — it is denial as ideology.

### How Their Dialogue Reveals Ideology

The contrast between parent and child language is one of Priestley's most effective structural choices. The parents' sentences are declarative and closed; the younger characters increasingly ask questions. Questions signal doubt, growth, and moral openness — declarations signal the opposite.

## Sheila and Eric: Conscience and Redemption

Priestley does not leave the audience without hope. Sheila and Eric represent the possibility that the next generation might do better — if they choose to.

### Sheila's Transformation: From Privilege to Moral Agency

Sheila begins the play as a product of her class: pleased with her engagement, comfortable in her privilege. Her role in Eva Smith's dismissal from Milwards — getting her sacked out of petty jealousy — is presented without excuse.

What matters is what happens next. Sheila listens. She asks questions. She refuses to let her parents minimise what has happened. Her language shifts from imperatives (she instructs the manager to remove Eva from the shop — [verify exact wording against your edition]) to self-accusation ('I'm ashamed of myself'), and that shift is **AO2** evidence of Priestley's dramatic method at work.

### Eric's Confession and Vulnerability

Eric's arc is darker. His relationship with Eva Smith involved exploitation as well as, eventually, a kind of care. His confession — delivered against his parents' barely concealed horror — is the play's most emotionally raw moment.

Priestley uses Eric's vulnerability deliberately. He is not a villain; he is a young man shaped by a family that never taught him accountability. His breakdown on stage is an indictment of his parents as much as of himself.

### Generational Divide as Priestley's Argument

The generational split is the play's structural spine:

- **Parents:** deny, minimise, return to business as usual
- **Children:** accept guilt, refuse to move on, demand change

Priestley's message is clear. Social change requires the willingness to feel uncomfortable — and the older generation has insulated itself too thoroughly to manage it.

## Gerald Croft: The Outsider's Complicity

Gerald occupies an uneasy middle ground. He is not a Birling, but he is complicit. He is charming, but his charm is the mask of a man who has never had to examine his own behaviour.

### Gerald's 'Affair' and His Self-Deception

Gerald's relationship with Eva Smith — whom he knew as Daisy Renton — is presented as, in his own mind, an act of kindness. He gave her somewhere to live. He was fond of her. He ended it gently.

His line 'I was at least honest with her' is a masterclass in self-justification. The qualifier 'at least' does all the work — it concedes that honesty was the *minimum* he offered, not the maximum. The irony is that his 'honesty' consisted of abandoning her when she became inconvenient.

### Why He Retreats at the End

Gerald is the character who discovers the Inspector may not be real — and uses that discovery to suggest the whole evening can be forgotten. His retreat into denial at the play's close aligns him, ultimately, with the parents rather than the children.

That alignment is **AO3** context: Gerald represents the upper-middle-class man who benefits from exploitation but will always find a reason why accountability does not apply to him.

## Eva Smith: The Absent Presence

Eva Smith never appears on stage. She is described, remembered, and mourned — but always through the distorting lens of the characters who wronged her.

### Why Priestley Keeps Her Off-Stage

By keeping Eva absent, Priestley forces the audience to construct her from fragments — and in doing so, to notice how each character's account reveals more about themselves than about her.

Mr Birling remembers a troublemaker. Mrs Birling remembers an impudent girl who used the wrong name. Gerald remembers a grateful recipient of his attention. Sheila remembers a pretty girl she was jealous of. Each description is a self-portrait.

### The Power of Her Absence

Eva's absence is an **AO3** method with a specific contextual resonance. Working-class women in 1912 were structurally invisible — denied political representation, economically dependent, easily discarded. Priestley literalises that invisibility on stage, then forces his audience to reckon with it.

## How to Structure a Character Essay for AQA

A strong character essay has a clear architecture. Here is the template that maps directly onto the mark scheme.

### Opening Paragraph: Character and Writer's Intention

Do not begin with biography. Begin with purpose. For example: 'Priestley presents Mr Birling as a vehicle for his critique of capitalist self-interest, using his language and dramatic function to expose the moral bankruptcy of Edwardian business culture.'

### The Evidence Sandwich

Every body paragraph should follow this structure:

1. **Point** — what Priestley is doing with this character
2. **Quote** — a specific, brief textual example
3. **Analyse** — the language, form, or structural choice (AO2)
4. **Context or theme** — link to 1912 society or the responsibility theme (AO3)

### Using Context Without Derailing

Context is a tool, not a detour. One sentence of context per paragraph is usually sufficient. 'In 1912, working-class women had no legal protection against dismissal' earns AO3 credit; three sentences of Edwardian social history does not.

### Closing: Why This Character Matters

Your conclusion should answer the question: what would be lost from Priestley's argument if this character did not exist? That question forces you to think about dramatic function rather than plot.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Examiners see the same mistakes on every paper. Here are the most common — and the fixes:

- **Treating characters as real people:** Always write 'Priestley presents…' not 'Sheila feels…'
- **Ignoring stage directions:** Stage directions are **AO2** evidence. 'Massively built' is a method, not a description.
- **Context without analysis:** Do not describe 1912 — use it to explain *why* Priestley makes a specific choice.
- **Plot summary:** If your sentence could appear in a synopsis, cut it.
- **Forgetting the responsibility theme:** Every paragraph should loop back to what the character reveals about collective responsibility.

**Worked example:**

*Weak:* 'Sheila is kind.'

*Stronger:* 'Priestley presents Sheila as morally awakened through her dialogue shift from imperatives to questions, signalling her rejection of her parents' class ideology and embodying his argument that genuine social change requires active self-examination.'

## Practical Exercise

Choose one character — the Inspector, Mr Birling, or Sheila. Write a 250-word paragraph analysing how Priestley uses their language and behaviour to reveal his message about social responsibility. Include a direct quote, an analysis of a specific language or structural method (AO2), and a link to 1912 context or the play's themes (AO3). When you have finished, check your paragraph against the evidence sandwich template above and identify which AOs you have covered — and which you have not.
