There is a pattern most English teachers will recognise. A student opens the AQA Power and Conflict anthology in February, flicks through it, lands on a poem like Tissue or Exposure, frowns, and quietly decides to "focus on the easier ones first". Six weeks later, when the exam looms, those harder poems are still untouched — and the student walks into Paper 2 with a comparison toolkit that can only go so deep.

That instinct is the wrong way round. The harder poems in the cluster are not the ones to avoid. They are the ones where the marks live. The poems that resist quick reading are the ones examiners reach for when separating Grade 6 essays from Grade 8 and 9 essays, because they reward students who can hold complexity, sustain a thread of analysis, and find unexpected pairings.

This piece ranks all 15 poems in the cluster by exam difficulty and shows you what to do at each tier — what to lock in, what to push further, and which pairs and trios reward the deepest thinking.

## How "difficulty" is defined here

The ranking is a working judgement, not a league table. It is built from three things you can measure when you sit with a poem:

- **Linguistic complexity.** Older language, dense imagery, unusual syntax, archaic or dialect forms — anything that slows the reading down on a first pass.
- **Thematic abstraction.** How easy is the central idea to grasp? A clear narrative is easier to hold than a layered conceit.
- **Comparison difficulty.** How well does it pair with other poems in the cluster? Some poems offer obvious thematic neighbours; others are harder to slot into a comparison spine without strain.

A poem can be linguistically simple but thematically demanding (Tissue), or linguistically dense but emotionally direct (Exposure). The tiers below try to balance all three.

## Tier 1 — Entry-points (easiest)

These four poems do most of the work for you on a first reading. The image is vivid, the speaker's situation is clear, and the central tension reveals itself without much excavation. They are the right place to build confidence — but they are not the place to stop.

**Bayonet Charge** — Hughes drops you mid-action. The verbs are visceral ("stumbling", "running", "lugged") and the soldier's mental collapse — from "patriotic tear" to "rolled like a flame" — moves in three stanzas. The narrative carries the meaning.
- Anchor quote: *"In bewilderment then he almost stopped"*
- Revision tip: track the shift from abstract patriotism to bodily panic across the three stanzas — examiners reward students who chart change rather than list features.

**Remains** — Armitage gives you a modern soldier's voice. The repetition of "probably armed, possibly not" lodges in the memory because the doubt is the point. The PTSD frame is explicit ("his bloody life in my bloody hands").
- Anchor quote: *"his bloody life in my bloody hands"*
- Revision tip: notice the colloquial register — "legs it", "tosses his guts" — and link it to the speaker's failed attempt to reduce the experience.

**Charge of the Light Brigade** — Tennyson's anthem rhythm makes this one of the easiest poems to memorise and quote. The dactylic gallop carries you through, and the famous line offers a ready-made critique.
- Anchor quote: *"Theirs not to reason why, / Theirs but to do and die"*
- Revision tip: do not let the rhythm flatten your reading. Tennyson is celebrating bravery and questioning command at once — hold both.

**War Photographer** — Duffy's central irony (the photographer's careful work versus the reader's brief glance) is signposted. The four-stanza structure makes it easy to track.
- Anchor quote: *"In his dark room he is finally alone"*
- Revision tip: read the closing lines as a critique of the audience, not the photographer — "they do not care" widens the lens beyond the speaker.

## Tier 2 — Middle ground

These five sit comfortably in the middle. Each rewards a careful reread but does not punish a quick one.

**London** — Blake is short and dense. The "mind-forg'd manacles" line is famous because it does so much work — internalised oppression, industrial imagery, political anger — in three words.
- Anchor quote: *"the mind-forg'd manacles I hear"*
- Revision tip: notice the four institutions Blake names — Church, Palace, marriage, soldier — and ask what each is doing to the city.

**Ozymandias** — short, ironic, and the story does most of the work. The framing (a traveller telling the speaker telling us) creates distance, and the inscription's irony is plain on first read.
- Anchor quote: *"Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!"*
- Revision tip: do not stop at the irony of the inscription — push to the sand itself, "boundless and bare", which outlasts every claim.

**Storm on the Island** — Heaney's extended metaphor is accessible. The shift from "we are prepared" to "we are bombarded" mirrors a shift from confidence to fear, and the political reading (Stormont, the Troubles) is a useful second layer.
- Anchor quote: *"Strange, it is a huge nothing that we fear"*
- Revision tip: the title splits into "Storm on" and "Stormont" — a conceit worth knowing, but do not lean on it as the whole reading.

**Poppies** — Weir's mother-voice is emotionally direct. The domestic detail (the blazer, the cat hairs) anchors the grief.
- Anchor quote: *"the world overflowing / like a treasure chest"*
- Revision tip: track how the speaker's body keeps doing tasks ("I pinned one onto your lapel") while her thoughts drift to childhood — the gap between hands and mind is the poem.

**The Emigrée** — Rumens is the most demanding poem in this tier because the dual voice (memory versus present, childhood city versus adult exile) does not resolve.
- Anchor quote: *"There once was a country... I left it as a child"*
- Revision tip: notice the colour "sunlight" runs through the poem like a thread — the city is remembered as a quality of light, not a place.

## Tier 3 — Demanding

These four poems demand close reading. The voice is layered, the syntax is complex, or the situation is harder to summarise. Spend more time here.

**My Last Duchess** — Browning's dramatic monologue is a masterclass in voice. The Duke incriminates himself line by line, and the iambic pentameter is held in heroic couplets that sound polite while the content turns chilling.
- Anchor quote: *"I gave commands; / Then all smiles stopped together"*
- Revision tip: read it aloud with the enjambment — Browning's rhythm hides the rhymes so the Duke sounds conversational. The form is doing political work.

**The Prelude (extract)** — Wordsworth's blank verse is harder to navigate than its narrative might suggest. The shift from confidence ("an act of stealth / And troubled pleasure") to terror ("a huge peak, black and huge") happens through syntax, not statement.
- Anchor quote: *"a huge peak, black and huge"*
- Revision tip: the sublime is the key concept here — landscape that humbles the speaker. Track how the boat journey reverses the speaker's sense of mastery.

**Kamikaze** — Garland's dual narrative voice (the daughter telling, the pilot's daughter remembering) creates layered guilt. The pilot is alive but socially dead, and the family's silence is the real subject.
- Anchor quote: *"he must have wondered / which had been the better way to die"*
- Revision tip: notice the third-person frame and the embedded first-person memory — the daughter cannot tell the story without her mother's voice intruding.

**Exposure** — Owen's modernist syntax is the obstacle. The repeated "but nothing happens" is structural, and the imagery (the merciless iced east winds, the dawn "massing in the east her melancholy army") refuses easy paraphrase.
- Anchor quote: *"But nothing happens"*
- Revision tip: weather is the enemy here, not the Germans. The cold is doing the killing, and the soldiers' theological doubts ("Is it that we are dying?") sit at the centre of the poem.

## Tier 4 — Grade 9 fuel (hardest, but pay biggest)

These two poems are where the highest grades are won. They are not the most popular choices in the exam, which is precisely why they reward the students who put the work in.

**Tissue** — Dharker's central conceit is abstract: paper as power, power as paper. The poem moves through Korans, maps, receipts, building plans, and skin without settling. Each metaphor expands the previous one.
- Anchor quote: *"Paper that lets the light / shine through"*
- Revision tip: do not try to find a single meaning. The poem's argument is that power held lightly — paper, breath, skin — outlasts power held in stone. Hold the layers.

**Checking Out Me History** — Agard's phonetic spelling and polyglossia (Caribbean Creole, Standard English, references that span Toussaint L'Ouverture, Mary Seacole, Nanny de Maroon) demand an integration of historical and literary readings. The form itself argues against the curriculum it critiques.
- Anchor quote: *"But now I checking out me own history / I carving out me identity"*
- Revision tip: the indented italicised stanzas (the Caribbean histories) sit visually apart from the British nursery-rhyme stanzas. The page is doing the argument.

Both poems reward students who can hold complexity. They are also the easiest path to Grade 9 territory in cross-text comparison, because most candidates will not have spent enough time with them.

## How to use the ranking

Two practical points.

First, do not ignore the harder ones. The temptation in late revision is to default to the poems you already know — to write the Bayonet Charge essay you have written four times. Resist it. Get a baseline understanding of all 15, then deep-dive on Tissue and Checking Out Me History. The marginal hour spent on a Tier 4 poem buys more than the marginal hour spent re-reading London.

Second, reverse your revision schedule. Most students revise in alphabetical or anthology order, which means they hit the hardest poems when they are tired. Flip it. Take the Tier 4 poems on a Saturday morning when you are fresh. Take the Tier 1 poems in the gaps between other subjects. Difficulty should set the order, not the table of contents.

## Comparison spines that work across tiers

Comparison is where Paper 2 is won or lost, and the strongest comparisons cross tiers — pairing an entry-point poem with a demanding one forces a richer argument. Three spines that work:

- **Power and decay.** *Ozymandias* (Tier 2) with *Tissue* (Tier 4). One stages decay through stone and time; the other stages it through paper and light. The contrast in materials is the contrast in worldviews.
- **Voice and silencing.** *Checking Out Me History* (Tier 4) with *The Emigrée* (Tier 2). Both poems push back against an imposed identity, but Agard reclaims through form while Rumens reclaims through memory.
- **War and humanity.** *Bayonet Charge* (Tier 1) with *Exposure* (Tier 3) with *Remains* (Tier 1). Three different distances from violence — mid-action, waiting, aftermath — and three different registers. A trio like this lets you sustain comparison across the whole essay.
