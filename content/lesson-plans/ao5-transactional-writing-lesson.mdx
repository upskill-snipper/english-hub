## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson prepares Year 11 students for the transactional writing task on AQA English Language Paper 2, where AO5 assesses content and organisation. Pupils learn to recognise the form a question demands — letter, article or speech — and to shape register, structure and persuasive choices around audience and purpose.

## Starter (10 minutes): What Makes a Good Letter to the Editor?

Open with a brainstorm. Display the prompt, "What makes a good letter to the editor?" Students work in pairs for three minutes listing features they would expect: a clear opening that names the issue, a respectful but firm tone, evidence or example, a call to action. Take feedback and group responses on the board under three headings — form, voice, structure — which will frame the rest of the lesson.

## Main (40 minutes): Form, Register and Structure

### Reading the Question

Show two sample prompts side by side. The first reads, "Write a letter to your local council arguing for or against the closure of a community library." The second reads, "Write an article for your school magazine about the value of reading for pleasure." Students annotate each: who is the audience, what is the purpose, and which form is required? Surface the rule that the question always specifies form, audience and purpose — and a strong response answers all three.

### Register and Form

Introduce three forms briefly. Letters open with a salutation and close with a sign-off; they tend toward formal register when addressed to authority. Articles use a headline and standfirst, and they vary in tone depending on the publication. Speeches use direct address — "you," "we," "imagine" — and rhetorical patterning to hold a live audience. Students match short extracts to each form to embed the distinctions.

### Worked Example

Walk through a five-paragraph plan for the council letter prompt. The opening paragraph names the issue, states the writer's position and signals the argument to come. Three supporting paragraphs each take one reason — community value, educational impact, financial counter-argument — and develop it with example, evidence and persuasive technique. The conclusion restates the position and ends with a clear call to action. Read aloud a teacher-modelled opening paragraph and ask students to identify the rhetorical devices used.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Drafting an Opening

Students choose one of the displayed prompts and draft an opening paragraph in five minutes. They must establish voice, audience and purpose within four sentences. Two volunteers read aloud; the class identifies one strength and one improvement using the success criteria from the starter.

## Differentiation

Foundation pupils receive a register checklist with sentence starters for formal openings and signposting phrases. Higher pupils receive a rhetorical-device extension card asking them to incorporate a tricolon, an antithesis and a direct address into their opening.
