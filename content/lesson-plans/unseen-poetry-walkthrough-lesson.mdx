## Lesson overview

This lesson gives Year 11 students a repeatable routine for tackling any unseen poem under timed conditions. The five-pass approach (form, voice, image, structure, shift) replaces the panic of a blank page with a sequence students can rehearse. Pair this lesson with the [unseen poetry revision hub](/revision/poetry/unseen-poetry) and the companion guide on [how to read an unseen poem in 8 minutes](/blog/how-to-read-an-unseen-poem-in-8-minutes).

## Starter (10 minutes): Rank the difficulty

Display the opening four lines of three canonical public-domain poems on the board:

1. William Blake, "The Tyger" — "Tyger Tyger, burning bright, / In the forests of the night..."
2. Christina Rossetti, "Remember" — "Remember me when I am gone away, / Gone far away into the silent land..."
3. Gerard Manley Hopkins, "Pied Beauty" — "Glory be to God for dappled things..."

Ask students to rank these openings by perceived difficulty and justify their top choice in one sentence. Take three contributions. Most will rank Hopkins hardest because of the unfamiliar diction. Use this to make the point that difficulty often comes from voice, not subject — the routine fixes this.

## Main (40 minutes): Teach the 5-pass routine

Introduce the five passes and what each one asks:

- **Form** — what kind of poem is this? Sonnet, free verse, ballad, dramatic monologue?
- **Voice** — who is speaking, to whom, and in what tone?
- **Image** — which one image carries the most weight?
- **Structure** — how is the poem shaped on the page and across stanzas?
- **Shift** — where does the poem turn? Volta, change in tense, a "but" or "yet"?

Apply the routine to a sample unseen (project a public-domain poem of your choice — Rossetti's "Remember" works well). Annotate the board live, one pass at a time. Pause after the **shift** pass and ask students to identify the turn. In "Remember", it lands on "Yet if you should forget me for a while".

### Whole-class modelling

Together, build one analytical paragraph. Establish the argument first ("Rossetti's volta reframes remembrance as a generous act rather than a duty"), then choose the evidence, then layer the analysis. Write the paragraph on the board sentence by sentence, asking students to suggest the next move.

### Common pitfalls to name explicitly

- **Feature-spotting**: listing techniques without an argument.
- **Missing the shift**: writing two paragraphs about the opening tone and ignoring the turn.
- **Treating the poem as a puzzle**: hunting for a hidden meaning instead of reading what's on the page.

### Worked Grade 9 paragraph

Display this model paragraph and unpack what makes it Grade 9:

> Rossetti reconstructs remembrance as an act of generosity rather than obligation. The volta — "Yet if you should forget me for a while / And afterwards remember, do not grieve" — pivots the sonnet's emotional logic, releasing the addressee from guilt. The conditional "if" is doing the heavy lifting: the speaker concedes forgetting as inevitable, and in doing so transforms remembrance from a debt into a choice.

The paragraph wins because it leads with argument, treats the shift as the spine, and reads what the conditional does rather than naming it.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Timed practice

Hand out a second unseen (a different public-domain poem). Students write for five minutes on the central shift. Take two readings aloud.

## Differentiation

- **Foundation**: provide a technique-spotting card (volta, enjambment, anaphora) with one-line definitions and an example for each.
- **Higher**: extension question on meter and sound — how does the rhythm reinforce the volta?
