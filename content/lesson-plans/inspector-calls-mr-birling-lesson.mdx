## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson introduces Year 10 students to Arthur Birling as Priestley's most exposed political target. By the end, pupils should understand that the gap between 1912 setting and 1945 audience is not a coincidence of staging but the play's central rhetorical engine.

## Starter (10 minutes): The Iceberg Speech

Project the lines in which Birling assures the family the Titanic is "absolutely unsinkable" and dismisses the prospect of war as "fiddlesticks." Without naming the device, ask students: knowing what we know about 1912, what does Priestley want a 1945 audience to feel here? Take three responses on the board under the headings "Smug," "Wrong," "Dangerous." Reveal the term dramatic irony only after pupils have done the inference work themselves. Frame the question that will guide the lesson: why begin a play with a character whose first job is to be wrong?

## Main (40 minutes): Birling and the Architecture of Capitalist Self-Interest

### Close Reading Birling's Act 1 Monologue

In pairs, students annotate the long Act 1 speech in which Birling lectures Eric and Gerald on social philosophy. Direct their attention first to the line, "a man has to make his own way," and the dismissive phrasing around "community and all that nonsense." Pupils mark every first-person pronoun and every reference to time ("the way some of these cranks talk and write now"). The pattern they should surface: Birling's worldview is individualist, future-blind and contemptuous of collectivism — exactly the politics Priestley has staged 1945 Britain to reject.

### 1912 versus 1945: Dramatic Irony as Political Argument

On a split board, students populate two columns. Column one: what Birling claims about 1912 — Titanic safe, war impossible, prosperity guaranteed. Column two: what a 1945 audience knew — two world wars, Titanic sunk, 1926 General Strike, Great Depression. The visual gap between columns is the irony. Discuss: every prediction Birling makes is chosen by Priestley because the audience already knows it failed. Birling is not just mistaken; he is a vehicle for retrospective judgement.

### Birling as Personification of Capitalism

Move to the line on "lower costs and higher prices" and the reference to "cheap labour." Ask students whether Birling speaks here as a man or as a position. Introduce personification of ideology: Priestley does not write Birling as a rounded individual but as capitalism wearing a dinner jacket. Pupils annotate evidence that Birling treats workers as units of cost, not human beings.

### Worked Grade 9 Paragraph

Model on the board a paragraph beginning, "Priestley weaponises dramatic irony to discredit capitalist confidence before it can speak unchallenged." Demonstrate how to embed Birling's "absolutely unsinkable" alongside 1945 context, and how to argue that Birling's wrongness is structurally engineered, not characterological. Pupils annotate the model paragraph with the moves you have made.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Exit Ticket

Students answer in two or three sentences: how does Priestley use Birling to make a political argument? Collect on slips. Strong responses will name dramatic irony, the 1912/1945 gap and the personification of capitalist self-interest.

## Differentiation

- Foundation: a 1912 context glossary covering Titanic, suffragette movement, pre-war optimism and class hierarchy, plus sentence stems ("Priestley shows Birling is wrong about... which makes the audience feel...").
- Higher: extension reading on capitalism as an economic theory and Priestley's socialism, with the prompt: is Birling a person or a thesis?
