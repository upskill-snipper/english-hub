## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson invites Year 10 to read Charles Causley's "Eden Rock" as a poem that holds two truths at once: a remembered family picnic and a quiet vision of the threshold between life and death. The lesson trains pupils to slow down on register, form, and the moments that surprise them.

## Starter (10 minutes): Paired Reading and Surprise-Marks

In pairs, students read the poem aloud, taking turns stanza by stanza. As they listen, each pupil pencils a small star next to any moment that surprised them — a phrase, an image, a tonal shift, or a word that did not behave as expected. After two read-throughs, pairs compare stars and pick the single moment that felt strangest. Three pairs feed back to the class. Most groups will land on "Somewhere beyond the Eden Rock," "I had not thought it would be so easy," or the speaker's stillness in the final line.

## Main (40 minutes): Close Reading and Worked Paragraph

### Pastoral Imagery and Archaic Register

Project the picnic stanzas. Pupils annotate "the same suit of Genuine Irish Tweed," "the patterned stones," "the sky whitens as if lit by three suns." Discuss how Causley reaches for a pastoral palette — grass, stream, sky, light — and a slightly archaic register ("Genuine Irish Tweed," "drawn from an old H.P. Sauce-bottle") that places the parents in a remembered, almost mythic past. The world feels gently lit, not gloomy.

### The Dead Parents

Reveal the biographical context for the Higher group: Causley's parents died long before the poem was written. The picnic is therefore a vision, not a memory of a single afternoon. Ask pupils to track the parents' agency across the poem — they "beckon," they "suggest," they wait. The dead are not passive; they invite.

### Form and Tone

Establish that the poem is free-verse, with no fixed metre, but the closing lines pull toward a couplet-like resolution that feels formally settled. Tone is tender, elegiac, ambiguous: is this a literal scene, an imagined reunion, or the speaker's own dying moment? Pupils argue all three readings.

### Worked Grade 9 Paragraph

Model a paragraph on "I had not thought it would be so easy." Open with a thesis on Causley's quiet refusal of grief; embed the line; analyse the modal "would" as the speaker testing an expectation against reality; read "easy" as both relief and gentle shock; situate the line against the form's late-arriving resolution; close with what the reader feels — invited, not frightened.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Pair-Share

Pupils discuss in pairs: what does Causley want the reader to feel about death? Take three responses; press each pair to point to one word in the poem that justifies their answer.

## Differentiation

- Foundation: glossary card defining "pastoral," "elegiac," "register," "couplet," "free-verse."
- Higher: biographical context handout on Causley's Cornwall, his late parents and the autobiographical pressure behind the poem.
