## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson invites Year 10 students to investigate how Shakespeare establishes the moral architecture of Macbeth in a single act. Pupils move from the eerie ambiguity of the witches to the steely persuasion of Lady Macbeth, finishing with the soliloquy that exposes the tragic hero's hesitation.

## Starter (10 minutes): Prophecies and Self-Fulfilling Beliefs

Begin with a hook: a short anecdote about a horoscope or a confident prediction that came true only because the believer made it true. In pairs, students discuss the prompt, "Do prophecies make things happen, or only describe them?" Take two contributions, then surface the term self-fulfilling belief on the board. Display the witches' chant, "Fair is foul, and foul is fair," and ask students to predict how this paradox might shape the play they are about to study.

## Main (40 minutes): Close Reading Across Three Pivotal Scenes

### 1.3 — The Witches and Banquo's Warning

Pupils read the prophecy passage in mixed-ability pairs. Annotate the line, "All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be king hereafter!" focusing on the future modal "shalt" and the imperative "hail." Compare with Banquo's caution that "instruments of darkness tell us truths" to win us "with honest trifles." Question: who interprets the prophecy responsibly, and who does not?

### 1.5 — Lady Macbeth's Invocation

Project the soliloquy beginning, "Come, you spirits that tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here." Students annotate verbs of removal ("unsex," "stop up," "take my milk for gall") and consider the imperative mood. Run a short whole-class discussion: is this rejection of femininity empowering, frightening, or both?

### 1.7 — "If it were done when 'tis done"

Distribute the soliloquy on the central panel of a tracking grid. Students underline conditional language and identify the metaphor, "Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself, And falls on th' other." This sets up the group task.

### Group Work — Tracking Macbeth's Wavering

In groups of four, students complete a wavering chart with three columns: Tempted, Resisting, Decided. They place quotations from 1.3, 1.5 and 1.7 into the appropriate column and justify each choice in one sentence.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Exit Ticket

Students write a Grade 7 paragraph in response to: "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth's hesitation in Act 1?" Insist on a clear topic sentence, two embedded quotations and one comment on dramatic effect. Collect on slips for retrieval next lesson.

## Differentiation

- Foundation: scaffolded quote sheets with line references, glossed vocabulary and a sentence-starter bank ("Shakespeare presents... through the use of...").
- Higher: extension prompt on Lady Macbeth's gender critique, asking whether her invocation indicts Jacobean expectations of women or merely weaponises them.
