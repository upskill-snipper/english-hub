## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson opens GCSE Literature by giving Year 10 a stable, transferable model for AO2: identify a feature, explain its effect, then explain the writer's purpose. The lesson trusts pupils to spot quality before they produce it, then equips them with the terminology and structure to lift their own writing.

## Starter (10 minutes): Rank the Paragraphs

Display two paragraphs side-by-side, both responding to "Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood clean from my hand?" Paragraph A sits at Grade 5: identifies the question, retells the meaning, gestures at "Macbeth feels guilty." Paragraph B sits at Grade 8: names the rhetorical question, reads "all great Neptune's ocean" as hyperbole, links to the Jacobean fear of regicide, and finishes on Shakespeare's purpose. Pupils rank them in pairs and write one sentence on what makes B stronger. Take three voices.

## Main (40 minutes): The Three-Layer Model

### Teach the Layers

On the board, write three columns: Identify — Effect — Purpose. Layer 1 is the feature: metaphor, plosive, structural turn, dramatic irony. Layer 2 is the effect on the reader or stage audience. Layer 3 is the writer's purpose: what idea, theme or argument the writer wants to land. Stress that Layer 3 is what separates competent from confident analysis.

### Apply to Macbeth

Pupils apply the model to "Is this a dagger which I see before me?" Identify: rhetorical question and visual hallucination. Effect: pulls the audience into Macbeth's destabilised perception. Purpose: Shakespeare dramatises how guilt arrives before the act, not after. Take feedback and tighten the wording on the board.

### Apply to Power and Conflict

Move to a quotation from the cluster — "My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings." Identify: declarative, allusion, hyperbole. Effect: reader sees self-aggrandising voice swallowed by the desert. Purpose: Shelley argues that political power decays while art outlasts the powerful.

### Tier 1, 2 and 3 Terminology

Brief lesson on tiers: Tier 1 is everyday vocabulary (sad, big), Tier 2 is academic across subjects (analyse, structure, evidence), Tier 3 is subject-specific (caesura, sibilance, dramatic irony, hamartia). Distribute a Tier 3 reference card for the Higher group.

### Whole-Class Modelling

On the visualiser, write a Grade 8 paragraph on "Out, damned spot" using all three layers. Identify the imperative and the noun "spot" as guilt made physical. Read the effect: a sleepwalking Lady Macbeth performing the murder she once dismissed. State Shakespeare's purpose: that suppressed conscience returns through the body when the mind refuses it. Pupils annotate the model paragraph against the three-layer model.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Rewrite the Starter

Pupils retrieve their starter paragraph and rewrite it using the three-layer model and at least two pieces of Tier 3 terminology. Three volunteers read the new version against the old.

## Differentiation

- Foundation: paragraph frame with sentence starters for each layer ("The writer uses... This makes the reader... The writer's purpose is to...").
- Higher: Tier 3 terminology card extended with structural and metrical terms for application to unseen poetry.
