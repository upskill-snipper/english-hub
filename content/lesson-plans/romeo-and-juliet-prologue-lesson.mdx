## Lesson overview

This 60-minute Year 10 lesson treats the prologue not as scene-setting decoration but as a 14-line poem that programs the entire tragedy. Students move from prediction to formal analysis to creative comparison, finishing with a focused exit ticket on dramatic irony.

## Starter (10 minutes): Predict the ending

Display only the prologue (no title, no character list). Give pupils five minutes to read it in pairs and answer one question: *Based on these 14 lines alone, what do you predict will happen in the play?* Take four or five responses on the board. Most pupils will spot the deaths and the family feud — confirm that Shakespeare has, unusually, told us the ending before the play begins. Hold the question that will frame the lesson: *why?*

## Main (40 minutes): Close reading the sonnet

**Form (10 minutes).** Establish that the prologue is a Shakespearean sonnet — 14 lines, three quatrains and a rhyming couplet, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG. Mark the rhyme scheme together on a printed copy. Tap out the iambic pentameter on the opening line, *"Two households, both alike in dignity,"* showing five stressed beats. Ask: why use the form most associated with love poetry to introduce a tragedy? Draw out the irony — the form promises romance, the content promises death.

**Foreshadowing (15 minutes).** Annotate the key tragic signals together. Focus on *"star-cross'd lovers"* and *"fearful passage of their death-mark'd love."* Pupils identify the imagery of fate (stars, marking) and the way the lovers are doomed before they appear. Add *"From forth the fatal loins of these two foes"* — note "fatal" and the way family identity is itself the source of the tragedy. Build a class table of every fate or death word in the prologue; the density alone makes the argument.

**Group task — modern English rewrite (15 minutes).** In groups of three, pupils rewrite the prologue in plain modern English, keeping the meaning but losing the verse. Each group reads their version aloud, then compares effect with Shakespeare's original. Lead the comparison: the modern version delivers information; the sonnet delivers atmosphere, ritual and inevitability. The form is doing work the prose cannot.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Exit ticket

On a slip of paper, pupils answer in three to four sentences: *What does Shakespeare gain by telling us the ending up front?* Strong answers will mention dramatic irony, audience focus shifting from "what happens" to "how it happens," tragic inevitability, and the way every hopeful moment in the play is shadowed by the prologue's promise.

## Differentiation

- **Foundation support:** A line-by-line annotated guide pairing each line of the prologue with a plain-English gloss, and a sentence-stem bank ("Shakespeare suggests... by using the word...").
- **Higher extension:** Pupils scan the full 14 lines for iambic pentameter, marking stresses and identifying any deliberate metrical disruption. They write a short paragraph on what the rhythm contributes to the prologue's tone and authority.
