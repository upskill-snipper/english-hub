## Lesson overview

This workshop fixes the most common Year 11 essay problem: paragraphs that describe instead of argue. Students learn a thesis-led structure — argument first, evidence second, analysis third, link fourth — and see why a counter-argument paragraph is the Grade 9 marker. Pair with the [essay structure resource](/resources/exam-technique/essay-structure) and the companion guides on [how to write a Grade 9 GCSE English essay](/blog/how-to-write-a-grade-9-gcse-english-essay) and [how to PEEL a paragraph](/blog/how-to-peel-a-paragraph).

## Starter (10 minutes): Rank the openings

Display three paragraph openings on the board:

1. "Shakespeare uses a lot of imagery in this scene to show that Macbeth is ambitious."
2. "In Act 1, Scene 7, Macbeth gives a soliloquy where he thinks about killing Duncan."
3. "Macbeth's ambition is not awakened by the witches but exposed by them — the soliloquy in Act 1, Scene 7 reveals a will already at work."

Ask students to rank these from weakest to strongest and justify their top choice. Most will pick (3). Surface why: it makes a claim. (1) names a technique, (2) describes the plot. Frame the lesson: today we move from topic openings to argument openings.

## Main (40 minutes): Thesis-led structure

Teach the four-move paragraph:

- **Argument** — a claim the rest of the paragraph proves.
- **Evidence** — a short, well-chosen quotation.
- **Analysis** — what the language does, not what it is.
- **Link** — back to the thesis, forward to the next argument.

### Whole-class modelling

Build a 200-word paragraph live on the question: "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth's ambition in Act 1, Scene 7?" Start with the argument sentence, then ask students to suggest the quotation, then write analysis in real time. Stop and revise on the board so students see the editing.

### The counter-argument paragraph

Explain why this is the Grade 9 marker. Most students argue one side. Top-band students concede the opposite reading and then resolve it. Model a counter-argument move:

> A reader could argue that Macbeth's "vaulting ambition" is the cause of the murder. But the soliloquy disproves this: he lists every reason not to kill Duncan and still proceeds. Ambition is the symptom, not the cause — the cause is something darker.

Name what's happening: the counter-argument is conceded, then dismantled, leaving the original thesis stronger.

### Worked example: full five-paragraph plan on Macbeth

Question: "How does Shakespeare present Macbeth as a tragic hero?"

1. **Thesis paragraph**: Macbeth is a tragic hero because he sees his own destruction clearly and chooses it anyway.
2. **Evidence one**: the Act 1, Scene 7 soliloquy — knowledge of consequence.
3. **Evidence two**: the dagger speech — the moment of choice.
4. **Counter-argument**: the witches as external compulsion, then dismantled.
5. **Conclusion**: tragic structure relies on agency; Shakespeare gives Macbeth agency at every turn.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Thesis sentence plus four bullets

Students write a thesis sentence in response to a fresh question, followed by four evidence bullets they could use to defend it. Take two readings aloud and stress-test the thesis with one challenge each.

## Differentiation

- **Foundation**: provide a PEEL frame (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link) with sentence starters for each move.
- **Higher**: extension to PETER (Point, Evidence, Technique, Explanation, Reader) plus a critical-lens task — argue the same thesis from a feminist or Marxist reading.
