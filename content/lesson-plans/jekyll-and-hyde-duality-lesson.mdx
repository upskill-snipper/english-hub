This lesson plan supports a single 60-minute Year 10 GCSE session on duality in Robert Louis Stevenson's *The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde*. It assumes students have read at least up to chapter 9 ("Dr Lanyon's Narrative") and are comfortable annotating short passages. It pairs with the longer study guide at [/revision/texts/jekyll-and-hyde](/revision/texts/jekyll-and-hyde) and the essay-focused support at [/analysis/jekyll-hyde](/analysis/jekyll-hyde).

## Starter (10 minutes)

Project a two-column grid headed "Jekyll" and "Hyde". Ask students, in pairs, to fill each column with everything they associate with the figure: physical description, language Stevenson uses, settings, social role, moral reputation. Take feedback for three minutes and circle any pair of opposites that emerges across the columns (large/dwarfish, fluent/inarticulate, Cavendish Square/Soho). The board image should already begin to suggest that the novella is structured around binaries.

## Main (40 minutes)

Spend the first fifteen minutes on a close reading of the transformation scene from chapter 9, "Dr Lanyon's Narrative". Read aloud from "He put the glass to his lips, and drank at one gulp" through Lanyon's collapse. Students annotate for three things in three colours: verbs of physical change, references to the body, references to the soul or mind. Draw out how Stevenson interleaves the physiological and the metaphysical so the doubling is at once medical and theological.

Move next to gothic doubling at the level of sentence and structure. Frame Stevenson's technique with three trackable strands:

- **Respectability vs repression:** Jekyll's status as a "large, well-made, smooth-faced man of fifty" set against Hyde, who gives "an impression of deformity without any nameable malformation".
- **Science vs religion:** Lanyon's reaction that what he saw "would stagger the unbelief of Satan" places the experiment in religious as well as scientific frames.
- **Day vs night:** the recurring movement between bright respectable streets and the "chocolate-coloured pall" of Soho fog.

Spend the final fifteen minutes on a worked Grade 9 paragraph on the line, "Man is not truly one, but truly two." Model on the board: open with a precise thesis about Stevenson's use of the aphorism to compress Victorian anxiety; embed the quotation; analyse the parallel structure ("not truly one, but truly two") as a grammatical enactment of doubling; widen to the chapter's confessional form; close on context. Students then redraft their own paragraph in their books.

## Plenary (10 minutes)

Show two short prompts side by side: a Darwin quotation on descent and a verse on the divided self from Romans 7. Ask students to write two sentences linking Stevenson's duality to Victorian Darwinism and to Christian morality. Take three responses; flag that AO3 marks reward this kind of contextual layering.

## Differentiation

For Foundation, provide a printed visual chart of the Jekyll/Hyde binary with sentence stems for the worked paragraph. For Higher, frame duality through a brief psychoanalytic lens (id, ego, super-ego) and ask students to argue whether Stevenson anticipates Freud or stays inside Calvinist categories.
