## Lesson Overview

Mock results day often produces two unhelpful reactions: relief or panic. This 60-minute lesson replaces both with a methodical debrief. Pupils stop asking "what did I get?" and start asking "where did the marks come from, where did they go, and what changes from Monday?"

## Starter (10 minutes): How Mocks Felt

Give pupils 30 seconds to write freely on the prompt: "How did the mocks feel?" Anything goes — emotional, practical, scattered. Take a few volunteer responses. The aim is to surface that the feeling of an exam is rarely a reliable guide to performance. A pupil who panicked may have scored well; a pupil who felt confident may have under-performed. This naming step clears the room of vague anxiety so the rest of the lesson can stay analytical.

## Main (40 minutes): The Debrief Framework

Walk pupils through three diagnostic stages.

### Stage 1: Paper Review

Hand back marked papers. Pupils answer two questions on a worksheet: where did marks come from, and where were marks lost? They list specific question numbers, not vague impressions. A sentence like "I lost marks on Question 3" is fine; "I felt rubbish in Section B" is not.

### Stage 2: Pattern Check

Pupils look across the paper for repeating issues. Did they consistently lose marks on AO2 language analysis? Did time pressure crush the final question? Did they paraphrase quotations rather than analyse them? One repeating pattern is enough — chasing five problems at once dilutes any revision plan.

### Stage 3: Action Planning

Each pattern becomes one concrete, time-bound action. Not "get better at language analysis" but "complete two language analysis paragraphs every Monday and Thursday for the next four weeks."

### Self-Marking Against the AO Chart

Project the Assessment Objective grid and walk pupils through one of their own answers. They self-mark against the AO criteria, noting where their evidence does and does not meet each band descriptor. The aim is not to argue with the teacher's mark but to internalise what each AO actually rewards.

### Pair-Share: Identical Question, Different Score

Pair pupils whose scores differed on the same question. They read each other's responses and identify, in a single sentence, what differed. This is consistently the most powerful five minutes of the lesson — pupils see, not hear, what a higher-band response looks like.

### Worked Example

Project a sample mock paper with annotated commentary written in the style of an experienced teacher: where the response hits the AOs, where it slips, what a Grade 7 version of the same paragraph would do differently. Pupils annotate their own paper with one comparable note.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Three Actions for Six Weeks

Pupils write three specific revision actions for the next six weeks. Each action must name a topic, a frequency and a measurable outcome. Direct pupils to the [exam technique resources](/resources/exam-technique) for paper-by-paper guidance and to the [grade-drop analysis](/blog/why-students-drop-a-grade-in-mocks) for the most common mock-to-real shifts.

## Differentiation

Foundation pupils receive a structured feedback frame with sentence starters guiding them through the three stages. Higher-tier pupils complete a mark-scheme deep-read, comparing two band descriptors line-by-line and identifying the precise lexical shift that separates the bands.
