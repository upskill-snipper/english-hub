## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson asks Year 11 to think thematically rather than scene-by-scene. Pupils stake a position on whether Macbeth is "a victim of fate," then test that claim against the textual evidence of ambition collapsing the protagonist over five acts.

## Starter (10 minutes): 4-Corners Debate

Label the four corners of the room: Strongly Agree, Agree, Disagree, Strongly Disagree. Read aloud, "Macbeth is a victim of fate." Students walk to the corner that matches their view and discuss in clusters for two minutes. Take one voice from each corner, then ask whether anyone has shifted. Record the spread on the board for the plenary callback.

## Main (40 minutes): Thematic Tracking and Worked Exemplar

### Act 1 — The Seed of Ambition

Project the line, "Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself, And falls on th' other." Annotate "vaulting" as athletic and aspirational, then turn the metaphor: ambition is also gravity, the inevitable fall already foreseen. Pair this with Lady Macbeth's, "Art not without ambition, but without The illness should attend it," noting how she diagnoses Macbeth as morally squeamish.

### Act 3 — Ambition Becomes Method

Move to Macbeth's, "To be thus is nothing, But to be safely thus." Students discuss the shift from desire to maintenance: ambition is no longer about climbing but about defending the summit. Compare with Lady Macbeth's diminishing presence — she is now reactive, asking, "Nought's had, all's spent."

### Act 5 — Ambition's Wreckage

Examine, "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow, Creeps in this petty pace from day to day." Lady Macbeth's "Out, damned spot" sleepwalking sits alongside. Students complete a comparative table: how has each character's relationship to ambition changed from Act 1?

### Worked Exemplar — Grade 9 Paragraph

Display a model paragraph on "vaulting ambition" that opens with a thesis about tragic inevitability, embeds the quotation precisely, analyses "vaulting" as both verb and adjective, contextualises with Jacobean attitudes to regicide and divine right, and concludes by linking back to the thesis. Pupils annotate the model with What A Good One Looks Like criteria, then attempt a parallel paragraph on a different ambition quotation.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Five-Minute Essay Plan

Display the question: "How does Shakespeare present ambition as a destructive force in Macbeth?" Students produce a five-minute plan: thesis, three body paragraphs (one per act), and a conclusion sentence. Collect three plans and read aloud for whole-class feedback.

## Differentiation

- Foundation: language stems for analytical sentences ("The verb 'vaulting' suggests... which implies... linking to the theme of...").
- Higher: counter-argument prompts asking pupils to defend the position that ambition is presented as morally neutral, with the witches or Lady Macbeth, not ambition itself, as the corrupting agent.
