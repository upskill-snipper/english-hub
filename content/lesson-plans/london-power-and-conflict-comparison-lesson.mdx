## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson pairs William Blake's "London" with Percy Bysshe Shelley's "Ozymandias" to build comparative analytical writing. Students close-read Blake first, then bridge to Shelley, ending with a worked Grade 9 comparison and a single-sentence thesis.

## Starter (10 minutes): Mark the Surprise

Read "London" aloud, slowly and once. Students annotate the words that surprised them — the noun, verb or image they did not expect. Likely candidates: "charter'd", "marks", "manacles", "blights", "hearse". Quick share-back. This builds vocabulary attention before any framework lands.

## Main (40 minutes): Close-Read and Compare

**London close-read (20 minutes).** Begin with the repeated "charter'd" in the opening couplet — even the Thames is owned. Move to "the mind-forg'd manacles I hear", the line that re-routes blame from the institutions to the internalised acceptance of them. The chimney-sweeper's cry "appals" the Church; the soldier's "sigh / Runs in blood down Palace walls"; the harlot's curse "blights with plagues the Marriage hearse". Each stanza pairs an institution with its victim.

Now turn to form. The poem moves in regular ABAB quatrains in iambic tetrameter — an orderly, almost nursery-rhyme rhythm. That regularity is ironic: the surface order of the verse contradicts the chaos and suffering it catalogues. Students mark the rhyme scheme and identify two moments where rhythmic regularity makes the content feel worse, not better.

**Comparison bridge (10 minutes).** Set up the comparison axes on the board: *target of critique*, *form*, *speaker position*, *resolution*. Blake's speaker walks among the suffering ("I wander thro' each charter'd street"); Shelley's speaker is twice removed via the traveller. Blake critiques living institutional power — Church, Crown, marriage. Shelley critiques fallen tyrannical power. Both deploy controlled form (regular quatrains; subverted sonnet) to underline their argument.

**Worked Grade 9 comparison paragraph (10 minutes).** Model a 200-word paragraph on the board comparing how each poet uses form to amplify critique. Open with a comparative thesis. Embed "mind-forg'd manacles" against "Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!". Note that Blake's regularity traps the reader inside the suffering, while Shelley's broken sonnet enacts the collapse it describes. Close on the political moment — Blake in 1794 after the French Revolution; Shelley in 1818 in radical exile.

## Plenary (10 minutes): One-Sentence Comparative Thesis

Students write a single sentence comparing how Blake and Shelley critique unjust power. The sentence must name both poets, both forms and one shared idea. Read three aloud. Collect the rest as exit tickets.

## Differentiation

Foundation learners receive a thematic word-bank: *institution, tyranny, decay, suffering, restraint, irony, regularity, fragmentation*. Higher learners receive a political-historical context sheet covering Blake's London in the 1790s, the Pitt government's repression, Shelley's radicalism and the Peterloo period.
