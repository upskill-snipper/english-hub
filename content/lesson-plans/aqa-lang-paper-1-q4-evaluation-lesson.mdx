## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute lesson tackles the question students most often misjudge on AQA English Language Paper 1: Question 4. Worth 20 marks, Q4 asks for evaluation of a viewpoint about a section of the source text, and pupils who treat it as another analysis question lose marks they could otherwise secure. The lesson teaches them to read the stem precisely, choose a position, and defend it with selected evidence.

## Starter (10 minutes): Unpacking the Stem

Display a typical Q4 stem on the board, framed as a statement followed by, "To what extent do you agree?" Students annotate in pairs for five minutes. What is the statement claiming? Which part of the source does it refer to? Is the claim absolute, partial or comparative? Take feedback and emphasise that the verb "evaluate" sits inside the words "to what extent." The question is asking them to weigh and judge, not simply to explain.

## Main (40 minutes): Position, Structure and Evidence

### Q4 Versus Q2

Begin by contrasting Q2 and Q4 directly. Q2 asks how the writer uses language for effect — pure analysis. Q4 quotes a reader's interpretation and asks the student to judge it. The skill on test is critical evaluation: agreement, qualification or refutation, supported by evidence and analysis. Make the distinction explicit on the board so students do not default to analytical autopilot.

### Choosing a Position

Three positions are available. Full agreement works when the statement holds across the whole section. Partial agreement — the most flexible option — works when the statement holds in some respects but not others, allowing pupils to nuance their reading. Disagreement is the boldest option and demands strong counter-evidence. Stress that the most defensible position, not the most ambitious, scores highest.

### Structure

Walk through a recommended four-paragraph shape. An opening thesis names the position and previews the supporting points. Two body paragraphs each take one aspect of the statement, quote evidence, analyse the writer's method, and judge the extent of agreement. A short concluding paragraph reinforces the overall verdict.

### Grade 9 Worked Paragraph

Read aloud a teacher-modelled Grade 9 paragraph on a sample Q4. Annotate it together: the thesis verb signals judgement; the embedded quotation is short and precise; the analysis links method to effect; the closing sentence returns to the statement to confirm extent of agreement. Pupils colour-code these moves in their own copy.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Thesis Sentence

Students write a single thesis sentence in response to a fresh Q4 stem displayed on the board. The sentence must name the position, signal scope and gesture toward the evidence to come — all in one cohesive sentence. Two volunteers share; the class identifies whether each thesis is defensible.

## Differentiation

Foundation pupils receive an argument-building scaffold with sentence stems for each paragraph move. Higher pupils receive a counter-argument extension asking them to acknowledge an opposing reading before defending their own position, modelling the qualification a top-band response makes.
