## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute entry lesson onboards Year 10 students to the Pearson Edexcel IGCSE 4ET1 anthology. By the end, students should know what the anthology contains, why it matters in Paper 2, and how to approach a comparative response with a five-step routine. The lesson is designed as the first proper anthology session of the course — not a drilling session, but a map of the territory.

## Starter (10 minutes): Predict the Themes

Project the anthology table of contents on the board (titles only — no analysis yet). Students work in pairs and predict which themes will recur across the collection: family, identity, conflict, place, voice, time, power. They write their top three predictions on a sticky note and post them on the wall. Keep the sticky notes up — at the end of the unit, students will revisit and check which predictions held. This primes them to read the anthology as a curated argument, not a random anthology.

## Main (40 minutes): Structure, Role and Strategy

**Anthology structure (10 minutes).** Walk students through the architecture of the anthology — it pairs poetry with prose extracts, and the editors have grouped texts so that students can compare across forms, not just within them. Stress that the anthology is not a list of poems to memorise; it is a comparison toolkit. For canonical poem and prose titles, refer students to the [Edexcel English Language anthology hub](/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology) rather than relying on memory.

**Exam role (5 minutes).** Paper 2 of the 4ET1 specification asks students to compare anthology texts (and, in some questions, an anthology text against an unseen extract). The mark scheme rewards sustained comparison, integrated quotation, and a clear line of argument. Students who treat the answer as two summaries glued together cap out at the middle bands.

**Walk through one sample poem (10 minutes).** Pick one poem from the canonical list on the [anthology hub page](/igcse/edexcel-lang/anthology) and model an annotation — title, voice, one structural feature, two language choices, one contextual hook. Students need to see one done well before they compare.

**Comparison strategy — the 5-step routine (15 minutes).**

1. **Read both texts straight through.** No annotation. Just listen for the dominant voice and the central image.
2. **List similarities.** Three to five — at the level of theme, voice, structure, or imagery.
3. **List differences.** The same number, mirrored against the similarities. Avoid trivial contrasts.
4. **Choose two anchor points.** These are the comparative hinges your essay will pivot on. Without anchor points, the response drifts.
5. **Write a thesis.** One sentence that names both anchor points and signals the argument.

Demonstrate the routine on the board using the sample poem and a short unseen extract. Students should see all five steps land in under ten minutes — this is a tool, not a treatise.

## Plenary (10 minutes): One-Sentence Thesis

Project a sample comparison prompt pairing the modelled poem with a brief unseen extract. Students write a single-sentence comparative thesis using their two anchor points. Collect on exit. Mark for: are there two anchor points named, and is there a clear argument rather than a list?

## Differentiation

Foundation learners receive a one-page anthology overview sheet listing the canonical groupings, key themes per cluster, and a comparison sentence-stem bank. Higher learners receive a comparative criticism extension — a short critical extract on the politics of anthologising, with three discussion prompts about whose voices the editors have foregrounded.
