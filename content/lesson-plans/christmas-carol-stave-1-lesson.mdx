This lesson plan supports a single 60-minute Year 10 GCSE session on Stave 1 of Charles Dickens' *A Christmas Carol*. It assumes students have read Stave 1 once and have a clean copy in front of them for annotation. It pairs with the longer study guide at [/revision/texts/a-christmas-carol](/revision/texts/a-christmas-carol) and the essay-focused support at [/analysis/christmas-carol](/analysis/christmas-carol).

## Starter (10 minutes)

Write "SCROOGE" in the centre of the board and ask students to call out everything they associate with the name: stinginess, "Bah! Humbug!", Christmas, ghosts, Tiny Tim, redemption. Map the responses around the word in two clusters, one for traits drawn from popular culture and one for traits they remember from the text itself. The aim is to surface how thoroughly Scrooge has entered cultural shorthand and to set up a question for the lesson: how does Dickens actually build the figure on the page in the first few thousand words?

## Main (40 minutes)

Spend the first fifteen minutes on a close reading of the opening pages of Stave 1. Move sequentially through four micro-units: Marley's death, the counting house, Fred's visit, and the charity collectors. For each, students underline one phrase per sentence that does narrative work and write a two-word label in the margin (for example, "establishes finality", "cold imagery", "rejection of family", "rejection of poor").

Next, build a board grid tracking Dickens' techniques in three strands:

- **Asyndeton in the opening:** "Marley was dead: to begin with. There is no doubt whatever about that." Then follow into "Old Marley was as dead as a door-nail." Discuss how the clipped, listed clauses force the reader to accept the fact before the story can move.
- **Semantic field of cold:** "The cold within him froze his old features, nipped his pointed nose, shrivelled his cheek, stiffened his gait." Track every cold-coded verb and adjective across the first six pages.
- **Semantic field of money:** "squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching, covetous old sinner." The participial list compresses Scrooge's economic identity into a moral verdict.

Spend the final fifteen minutes on a worked Grade 9 paragraph on Marley's line, "I wear the chain I forged in life." Model on the board: open with a thesis on Dickens' use of the chain as an externalised conscience; embed the quotation; analyse "forged" as both metalwork and moral fabrication; widen to the allegorical structure of the novella; close on Victorian context. Students draft their own paragraph in their books.

## Plenary (10 minutes)

Pose the question: what evidence in Stave 1 already sets up Scrooge's redemption? Take three pieces of evidence on the board: Fred's persistent warmth, Marley's warning, the charity collectors framing a moral choice. Students write a single sentence predicting which character's intervention will most shift Scrooge by Stave 5.

## Differentiation

For Foundation, distribute a pre-reading vocabulary sheet covering "humbug", "covetous", "miser", "workhouse" and "surplus population", with simple definitions and example sentences. For Higher, supply a short Victorian poverty context note covering the 1834 Poor Law, the workhouse system and Dickens' own journalism, and ask students to weave one concrete contextual reference into the worked paragraph for AO3.
