## Lesson Overview

This 60-minute deep-dive treats Percy Bysshe Shelley's "Ozymandias" as a single sustained close-read. Students move from visualising the wreck to dissecting form, voice and the inscription, finishing with a Grade 9 worked paragraph and a sharp written thesis.

## Starter (10 minutes): Sketch the Statue

Before the poem is read, students sketch what they imagine Ozymandias' statue looked like at its height — proportions, posture, expression, setting. They then sketch what remains. The two drawings sit side by side. This visual contrast primes the central image: "Two vast and trunkless legs of stone / Stand in the desert" with the "shattered visage" half-sunk in sand. Students share one detail they expect Shelley to dwell on. This pre-loads attention to fragmentation.

## Main (40 minutes): Close-Reading the Sonnet

Read the poem aloud twice. The first reading is uninterrupted. The second is paused at line breaks for annotation.

**Frame narrative.** Track the layered voice: the poet ("I met a traveller from an antique land") quotes the traveller, who quotes the sculptor ("its sculptor well those passions read"), who in turn quotes Ozymandias ("My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings"). Power's voice is the most distant and the most undermined — Shelley puts the tyrant at the bottom of a stack of intermediaries. Discuss why a Romantic poet would deny the tyrant a direct voice.

**Sonnet form subverted.** "Ozymandias" looks Petrarchan but refuses to behave. The volta does not land cleanly at line 9. The rhyme scheme (ABABACDCEDEFEF) refuses both Petrarchan and Shakespearean conventions, mirroring the broken statue. Students mark every rhyme and label the disruption — form enacts collapse.

**The inscription.** Spend fifteen minutes on "And on the pedestal, these words appear: / 'My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings; / Look on my Works, ye Mighty, and despair!'" The boast was meant to terrify rivals; in context, "Nothing beside remains" makes it terrify the speaker instead. The dramatic irony is total. The "lone and level sands" stretching "far away" answer the boast with silence.

**Worked Grade 9 paragraph.** Model on the board, sentence by sentence: a precise thesis, embedded quotation, zoomed analysis of "Mighty" and "despair", attention to caesura and exclamation, then a contextual lift to Shelley's radical politics in 1818 and the Romantic suspicion of monumental authority. Students copy and annotate the model rather than producing their own draft yet.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Sixty-Word Thesis

Exit ticket. Students write a 60-word thesis answering: *What does Shelley argue about power?* The constraint forces precision — no padding, every word load-bearing. Collect on the way out.

## Differentiation

Foundation learners receive a line-by-line guide marking syntax breaks, rhyme positions and key noun choices. Higher learners receive a one-page Romanticism context sheet covering Shelley's politics, the 1817 sonnet competition with Horace Smith, and the Egyptian Ramesses fragments arriving in the British Museum.
