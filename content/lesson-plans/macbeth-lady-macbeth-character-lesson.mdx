## Lesson overview

This 60-minute Year 11 lesson invites students to study Lady Macbeth as a developing character rather than a fixed villain. Pupils move from active drama to forensic close reading and into critical debate, ending with extended writing pitched at the top of the GCSE mark scheme.

## Starter (10 minutes): Hot-seat — Lady Macbeth on trial

Place one student in role as Lady Macbeth in the chair after Duncan's murder. The rest of the class question her: *Did you act alone? Why did you summon the spirits? Do you regret it?* Encourage in-character answers grounded in Act 1 and Act 2. Rotate two or three volunteers. The activity surfaces prior knowledge, gets pupils speaking in textual register, and frames the central question: who is Lady Macbeth, really?

## Main (40 minutes): Tracing the arc

Split the board into three columns — **Power (Act 1)**, **Unease (Act 3)**, **Collapse (Act 5)** — and lead a guided close reading of one anchor quotation per stage.

**Act 1 — Power.** Examine Lady Macbeth's invocation: *"Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here."* Students annotate the imperative verbs, the supernatural address and the verb "unsex." Ask: what does she believe femininity prevents her from doing? Pair this with *"screw your courage to the sticking-place,"* noting the mechanical, almost violent metaphor she uses to manipulate her husband.

**Act 3 — Unease.** Read her aside in 3.2: *"Nought's had, all's spent, / Where our desire is got without content."* Students identify the shift from imperative to reflective, the antithesis, and the dawning recognition that ambition has not delivered satisfaction. Compare her diminishing stage time with Macbeth's growing autonomy.

**Act 5 — Collapse.** Move to the sleepwalking scene: *"Out, damned spot! out, I say!"* Students annotate the broken prose, the imperatives turned inward, and the obsessive return to the imagined blood. Discuss how Shakespeare structurally mirrors her Act 1 invocation — the same commanding voice, now powerless.

## Discussion: Is Lady Macbeth a feminist character?

Spend ten minutes of the main on a structured debate informed by AS-level critical lenses. Offer two positions:

- **Reading A (proto-feminist):** Lady Macbeth rejects the constraints Jacobean society placed on women and seizes political agency.
- **Reading B (cautionary):** Shakespeare punishes a woman who steps outside her sphere; her madness is the play's moral correction.

Ask pupils to use textual evidence to back a position, then to challenge their own reading. This trains the AO5-style evaluative skill rewarded at the top of the mark scheme.

## Plenary (10 minutes): Grade 9 paragraph

Set the focused task: *How does Shakespeare present Lady Macbeth's unconscious mind in Act 5?* Students write one tightly argued paragraph using the sleepwalking quotation, embedding terminology (imperative, fragmented syntax, repetition) and finishing with a sentence linking back to her Act 1 invocation. Take two examples to read aloud; peer-assess against a Grade 9 descriptor.

## Differentiation

- **Foundation support:** A glossary sheet defining *invocation, imperative, antithesis, prose, soliloquy* and a sentence stem bank for the plenary.
- **Higher extension:** A short extract from gender-criticism scholarship (teacher-selected) and a follow-up question asking pupils to apply or contest the critic's claim.
