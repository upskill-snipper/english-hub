## 2. Voice in one paragraph (the north star)

We sound like a serving English teacher who has marked too many essays to be fooled by jargon. We are calm, plain, and pedagogical before we are persuasive. We explain every feature in terms of the learning it supports — the assessment objective, the mark scheme, the next step a pupil can take. We are honest about limits: we label AI-assisted output, we do not promise grade uplift, and we size our marketing claims to the evidence we can actually point at. When we talk to parents, we use the same voice we would use at a parents' evening. When we talk to pupils, we are the older sibling who got a 9 and actually wants to help — warm, direct, never condescending, never trying to sound younger than we are.

**Elevator test:** if a Year 11 English teacher read this copy over a cup of tea in the staffroom, would she roll her eyes, or would she quietly agree with it? We write for her.

**Confidence:** High. Lifted and reconciled from the `/brand` Voice & Tone section, with the student-facing warmth layered in from the Content Strategy Playbook (moderated — see §7 and §11).

## 4. Grammar and style rules

### 4.1 British English, consistently

- **Spelling:** programme, colour, behaviour, catalogue, practise (verb) / practice (noun), realise, centre, grey.
- **Vocabulary:** revision (not "review"), Year 10/11 (not "10th grade"), secondary school (not "high school"), holiday (not "vacation"), mobile (not "cell"), timetable (not "schedule" for a class).
- **Punctuation:** single quotes for speech, double quotes for quoted speech inside speech. Oxford comma optional — use when it avoids ambiguity, skip when it doesn't. En-dashes for ranges (10–11), em-dashes for asides — like this — with spaces either side.
- **Dates:** "11 May 2026", not "May 11, 2026". "Week commencing 12 May", not "week of 5/12".

### 4.2 Sentence shape

- **Short is better.** Aim for a Flesch reading ease around 55–65 for body copy. We are not dumbing down — we are writing for a tired reader.
- **Active, not passive.** "The AI marks the essay against AO2." Not "Essays are marked against AO2 by the AI."
- **One idea per sentence.** If two ideas want to hold hands, use a full stop.
- **Subordinate clauses are fine** — just not stacked three deep.

### 4.3 Punctuation habits

- **Exclamation marks:** none in product copy, none in marketing body, none in email subject lines. One permitted when a pupil has actually done something worth celebrating ("Your predicted grade just moved to a 7!") and never more than one in a row.
- **All caps:** never in running prose, never in the wordmark. Acceptable for single words inside a dashboard badge ("NEW") or a single word of emphasis inside a student-facing quiz ("CORRECT") — and even then, sparingly.
- **Emoji:** single emoji max per social post, placed at the end of a line, never in the middle of a sentence. Zero emoji in email body copy. Zero in subject lines. If you feel the urge for two, the sentence isn't working.
- **Ellipsis:** only for genuine trailing thought in pupil-facing revision copy ("and that's where Lady Macbeth pivots…"). Never as a soft sell device ("Curious to learn more…").

### 4.4 Numbers, data, and claims

- **Numbers up to nine** in prose: one, two, nine. Numerals for 10 and above.
- **Percentages always as numerals with a % sign**: "20% recurring".
- **Currency:** GBP with "£" and no space: "£3.49/mo", "£29.99/year". Never "GBP 3.49" in user-facing copy.
- **Claims are sized to the evidence.** Pupil counts, ratings, retention numbers, success stories — none of them ship until they are independently verifiable on our side and the founder has explicitly approved the form of words. The `/press` page already uses `StatPlaceholder` components for exactly this reason. Any claim that cannot point to a specific source on file must be removed before send.
- **No promise of grade outcomes.** We do not say "go from a 5 to a 9". We do not promise a grade boost in any form. If we can't verify it, we don't claim it.

### 4.5 Product name discipline

- **Always** "The English Hub" with a capital T, E, and H. "The" is part of the name.
- **Never** "TEH", "EH", or "English Hub" without the article, or any all-caps rendering in running prose.
- **Second reference** in long-form articles may use "the Hub" — lowercase "t", capital "H" — but never in headlines, link text, or standalone product naming.
- **Inside URLs and email addresses** lowercase is fine (`theenglishhub.app`, `press@theenglishhub.app`).
- **Wordmark:** always the full serif lockup exactly as the footer renders it. Do not redraw, re-kern, recolour, or pair with a second typeface.

**Confidence:** High. Lifted directly from the `/brand` page.

## 5. Vocabulary — words we use, words we don't

### 5.1 Ownable phrases (use these; they are us)

- **"Built by a serving UK secondary English teacher."** The authority line. Use first-touch.
- **"Start your anthology."** The homepage hero verb. An ownable, literary, low-stakes imperative. Use in hero blocks, campaign titles, social graphics where space allows.
- **"Against the real mark scheme."** Our AI-feedback differentiator, one-line. Variants: "against the real AO rubric", "against the AQA / Edexcel / OCR / WJEC mark scheme your pupils actually sit."
- ~~"Join thousands of students raising their grade."~~ — **Withdrawn 2026-04-25.** Live homepage copy claims a user count that is not currently verifiable. Do not use this line in outreach, ads, social posts, or partner pitches until the founder has confirmed the underlying number on file. The homepage itself should be reviewed and either updated with a verifiable number or softened.
- **"Let's fix this."** Pupil-facing, warm, direct. Use in revision copy openers.
- **"Here's what your examiner is looking for."** Pupil-facing, in-the-know. Use in model-answer intros and revision explainers.
- **"Your predicted grade just moved."** Dashboard language when a pupil improves. Emotional, earned.

### 5.2 Words we reach for first

`revise · revision · mark scheme · assessment objective · AO1/AO2/AO3/AO4/AO5 · paper · specification · set text · anthology · extract · evidence · argue · analyse · explore · pupil · student · cohort · Year 10 · Year 11 · department · teacher · head of English · grade · predict · model answer · annotated · technique`

### 5.3 Words we try to avoid

- **Edtech-jargon:** "gamified learning", "personalised learning journey", "smart tutor", "learning experience", "cutting-edge".
- **Corporate softwarespeak:** "solutions", "empower", "unlock", "leverage", "best-in-class", "trusted partner".
- **Hype AI vocabulary:** "revolutionary", "game-changing", "AI-powered" (we prefer "AI-marked", "AI feedback", "AI-assisted" — each more specific).
- **Dated teen slang:** "no cap", "bestie", "it's giving", "slay", "lowkey/highkey", "periodt". Also avoid "on fleek"-tier artefacts of any era.
- **Empty superlatives:** "amazing", "incredible", "unbelievable", "super-simple".
- **Vague promises:** "take your grades to the next level", "reach your potential", "be the best version of yourself".

### 5.4 AI-specific vocabulary

We are **deliberately calm** about our AI. Three rules:

1. Always specify what the AI does, not that it exists. Not "AI-powered essay help" — instead "AI feedback with an AO breakdown in under a minute."
2. Always label AI output as such in-product ("Marked by The English Hub AI against the AO rubric — a teacher can override any grade").
3. Never attribute knowledge or feelings to the AI. "The AI decided" / "the AI thinks" / "our AI has read thousands of essays" are all out. "The AI matches your response against the AO rubric" is in.

**Confidence:** High (5.1–5.3 triangulated across `/brand`, live site, and outreach materials). Medium on 5.4 — want founder confirmation that AI labelling language matches what's shown in-product today.

## 6. Sentence and paragraph patterns we reach for

### 6.1 Opening moves

- **Teacher-to-teacher opener (outreach):** "I watched [specific video / read specific post] this weekend and the bit where you [one specific thing they did] is genuinely better than how most departments teach it."
- **Pupil-to-pupil opener (revision):** "Here's the bit of [text / paper] that most Year 11s lose marks on."
- **Parent-to-parent opener (outreach):** "I came across your [specific post / episode / column] — it landed because it's the honest version of a conversation we hear all the time."
- **Institutional opener (schools):** "I'm writing to offer [SCHOOL NAME]'s English department free use of our teacher and classroom tools for the rest of this academic year."

### 6.2 Proof-stacking (the three-bullet rhythm)

When demonstrating the platform, use a three-bullet stack with a consistent shape — **feature → rubric tie-in → payoff**. Example:

> - 470+ structured lessons, mapped to the specification
> - AI essay feedback with AO1/AO2/AO5 breakdown, against the real mark scheme
> - A department dashboard showing which AOs your Year 11s are losing marks on

### 6.3 Closing moves

- **Soft close:** "Reply 'yes' and I'll send the link within the hour."
- **Honest close:** "If it isn't an improvement on what you're already using, bin it."
- **Partnership close:** "Worth a short conversation?"
- **Institutional close:** "Thank you for reading this far."

### 6.4 What good sounds like — a three-tone reference

For the same feature, here's how the register shifts by audience. Use this as a calibration check on any new piece of copy.

**Feature:** AI essay feedback.

- **Pupil-facing (dashboard / TikTok caption):** "Upload a Q5. Get an AO breakdown back in under a minute. Know exactly which bits cost you marks."
- **Teacher-facing (email):** "Pupils submit a Paper 1 Q5; it comes back marked against the real AO rubric, with AO1 / AO2 / AO5 strengths and weaknesses laid out and a Grade 1–9 prediction. Sixty seconds. No marking pile."
- **Parent-facing (blog / podcast):** "Your child writes an essay. The platform marks it against the same mark scheme the examiner will use in May, and shows which parts earned the grade and which didn't. You don't need to know English Literature yourself — the feedback explains it."

Same feature. Same facts. Three registers, all recognisably us.

**Confidence:** High.

**Student-facing TikTok voice — the tension worth calling out.**
The older Content Strategy Playbook (22 March 2026) proposed a Gen Z register with "lowkey", "no cap", "literally". A 2026 Year 11 audience reads "no cap" as a marker of adults-trying-too-hard; they don't use it themselves. Same outcome — relatable, peer, warm — with less risk of cringe. See §11 for the explicit open question.

## 8. Visual voice — colour, type, layout

The written voice and the visual voice must match. A pedagogical, quiet, British sentence on a neon-gradient Instagram graphic does not read as us.

### 8.1 Colour tokens

| Role                     | Hex       | Token       | Usage                                                                                        |
| ------------------------ | --------- | ----------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary — sea-slate teal | `#2DD4A8` | `--primary` | Primary CTAs, brand marks, focus rings.                                                      |
| Accent — Formby clay     | `#E8A382` | `--accent`  | Editorial accents, the italic wordmark, highlight text. **Never the primary button colour.** |
| Ink — pit-ink black      | `#0F1411` | `--ink-900` | Footer, dark surfaces, highest-contrast type.                                                |
| Cream — manuscript cream | `#FBF7F0` | `--ink-50`  | Background in dark contexts, quiet cards.                                                    |
| Muted — slate mist       | `#B5B8B3` | `--ink-400` | Secondary text, dividers, muted labels.                                                      |
| Success — hedge green    | `#16A34A` | `--success` | Completion, pass, positive progress.                                                         |
| Warning — amber          | `#D97706` | `--warning` | Caution, Early Access flags.                                                                 |
| Danger — signal red      | `#DC2626` | `--danger`  | Destructive actions, errors, at-risk markers.                                                |

**Do not** use the clay accent as a CTA colour. **Do not** put the logo on a low-contrast background (keep 4.5:1 minimum).

### 8.2 Type

- **Display:** Source Serif Pro. Headlines, editorial callouts, the wordmark itself.
- **Body:** Inter. Navigation, body copy, interface labels.
- **Pairings to never make:** the wordmark with a third typeface; Source Serif Pro with any other serif in the same piece.
- **Fallback stacks** (when the hosted fonts fail): Georgia, Times New Roman (serif); system-ui, Helvetica (sans). Mark any v0 asset as "v0 — pending font procurement" if the primary families are unavailable.

### 8.3 Layout

- **8-point grid.** All spacing snaps to 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96 pixels.
- **Radius ladder:** 6px chips/inputs, 12px cards, 16px sheets, full-round avatars and pill badges.
- **Focus rings:** 2px solid primary, 2px offset.

### 8.4 Photography + illustration

We have not yet locked a photography style. Until we do:

- **Avoid stock photos of happy diverse teens around a laptop.** They read as stock.
- **Prefer:** product screenshots (crop thoughtfully), annotated text extracts, handwritten-and-marked essays photographed from above, quote typography on cream backgrounds.
- **No illustrations of robots, brains with gears, or hands reaching for light bulbs.**

**Confidence:** High on 8.1–8.3 (from `/brand`). Low on 8.4 — photography direction is an open question for the founder; flagged in §11.

## 9. Claim ethics — what we will and won't say

This is non-negotiable and sits above the voice rules: if a voice rule and a claim rule conflict, the claim rule wins.

- **We do not fabricate stats, quotes, or press mentions.** If we do not have them, we leave the slot empty. The `/press` page uses visible `StatPlaceholder` components for this reason.
- **We do not promise grade outcomes.** Not "go from a 5 to a 9", not "guaranteed grade boost", not "the path to a 9". We can report what we can measure: completion, engagement, time saved, AO coverage. We can share testimonials in the speaker's own voice. We cannot promise what a real exam will return.
- **We do not make comparative claims we cannot defend.** "Better than [competitor]" without a footnoted study is out. "Different from [competitor] because [specific]" is fine.
- **We label AI output.** In-product, in-email, in-social. A pupil or teacher should always know which response came from a human and which came from the AI.
- **We do not fake scarcity.** If "10 Founding School places" is real, we say how many remain. If "limited time" is real, we say when it ends.
- **We do not test dark patterns.** No "confirm-shaming" unsubscribes ("No thanks, I don't care about my grades"), no countdown timers that reset on refresh, no false-default radio buttons.
- **Under-18s get the extra sentence.** Any cold outreach to a creator we believe is under 18 must be flagged to the founder first. Safeguarding is not a voice choice; it is a gate.

**Confidence:** High. Directly from `/brand` don'ts and from compliance-first stance on `/press`.
