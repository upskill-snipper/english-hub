# Corporation Tax Registration — The English Hub Ltd

**Purpose:** Register the new entity for Corporation Tax and ensure the old entity's final CT return is filed cleanly.

---

## New entity: Corporation Tax registration

### How it works

When you incorporate a company at Companies House, HMRC is usually notified automatically and issues a Corporation Tax Unique Taxpayer Reference (UTR) to the registered office within 14 working days.

### What you receive

- A welcome letter from HMRC addressed to the registered office
- The CT UTR (10-digit number)
- A CT41G form (or equivalent) asking for basic information about the company

### What you must do within 3 months of starting to trade

Notify HMRC that the company is "active" (i.e. trading) — this is done via the Government Gateway once you have the CT UTR. The clock starts from the date the company first "comes within the charge to Corporation Tax" — which is typically the Transfer Date when assets start moving.

**Critical:** the 3-month deadline runs from when trading starts, not from incorporation. You can incorporate in Week 1 and be dormant for a few weeks before the asset transfer — that is fine, just register as active within 3 months of the Transfer Date.

### Information needed

- Company name and CRN
- Registered office
- Date the company started trading / became active
- Main business activity
- Name of accountant (if any) and their agent code
- Accounting reference date
- Director contact details

### First CT accounting period

The first CT accounting period runs from the date trading begins to the end of the first set of statutory accounts. If the company was incorporated but dormant before trading started, the period before trading is a separate dormant period (no CT return needed).

Example:
- Incorporated: 1 June
- Dormant from: 1 June to 1 July
- Started trading: 1 July (Transfer Date)
- ARD: 31 March (set via AA01)
- First CT period: 1 July to 31 March (9 months)
- CT600 due: 12 months after end of period = 31 March the following year
- CT payment due: 9 months + 1 day after end of period = 1 January the following year

### Filing CT600

- Filed online via HMRC's Corporation Tax service or by accountant
- Accountant typically handles this as part of annual accounts preparation
- Due: 12 months after end of accounting period
- Payment due: 9 months and 1 day after end of accounting period — this is a **separate deadline** from the filing deadline, and it matters

### Payment

- Online banking (Faster Payments / BACS / CHAPS)
- Direct Debit (must be set up via HMRC Gateway, cannot just pull it)
- Corporate debit card

## Old entity: Corporation Tax cessation

### Final accounting period

The old entity (Upskill Energy Ltd) must file a final CT600 covering the period from the start of its current accounting period to the cessation date (typically the day before the Transfer Date, so the new entity picks up from the Transfer Date itself).

### Final accounts

Final accounts must be prepared to the cessation date — not the original ARD. This "short" final period is filed at Companies House as part of the final annual accounts, and the CT600 matches this period.

### Notify HMRC of cessation

Notify HMRC that the old entity has stopped trading. This can be done:
- Via the Government Gateway
- On the CT600 itself (tick the "final return" box)
- By letter to the CT office

### Pay any final CT

Any CT due on disposal gains from the asset transfer is settled as part of this final return. **The ATA consideration allocation directly affects how much CT is due on the old entity's final disposal.** Coordinate with the accountant before fixing consideration.

### Close the CT UTR

Once the final return is filed and any tax paid, HMRC will flag the old entity's CT UTR as ceased. This usually happens automatically but confirm in writing.

## Disposal of intangibles — what the accountant needs to consider

For a small EdTech business:
- **Goodwill** — if it was acquired originally (e.g. in a previous transaction), there may be intangible fixed asset treatment. Internally-generated goodwill sits outside the intangibles regime.
- **Intellectual property** — depends on whether it was on the balance sheet as an intangible asset.
- **Customer list / databases** — intangible, usually no book value, but disposal is still technically in scope.

The honest position for most founder-owned businesses: there's very little on the balance sheet, so the disposal produces minimal taxable profit, and TOGC + intercompany pricing keeps things clean.

**Don't guess. Get the accountant to model it.**

## Relevant regime — intangible fixed assets (IFAs)

The intangibles regime (Corporation Tax Act 2009, Part 8) deals with goodwill and intangibles acquired **after 2002**. Most restructure transfers between commonly-owned companies fall within it. The regime:
- Allows amortisation of acquired intangibles in some cases (but not goodwill acquired since 2015, mostly)
- Taxes disposals as income (not capital gains)
- Has specific rules for intra-group transfers at tax-neutral value under s.775 CTA 2009 (group relief)

**Group relief on intangibles may apply** if the two companies are in a "group" at the time of transfer. A group for CT purposes generally requires 75% parent-subsidiary relationship. In this restructure, the old entity and the new entity are **not** in a group (they are sister companies under the founder's personal ownership), so group relief does **not** apply.

This means the disposal by the old entity is taxable at full market value in the old entity's final CT return. The new entity gets a corresponding cost base for future depreciation or disposal.

**This is a place where the accountant earns their fee.** If there is meaningful value on the old entity's balance sheet, model the tax before committing to the transfer price.
