## 2. The share-for-share question

A "clean" restructure usually takes one of two forms:

### Option A — Asset transfer (this plan)

The new company is incorporated with fresh shares. The trade, assets, IP and contracts are transferred from the old company to the new company via an Asset Transfer Agreement. Consideration is either:
- **Cash** (the new company pays the old company for the assets — requires funding), or
- **Intercompany loan** (the new company owes the old company for the assets, then the loan is waived or novated when the old company is wound up).

Tax treatment matters and needs accountant sign-off. Usually this route involves:
- VAT TOGC relief to avoid charging 20% VAT on the asset transfer.
- Consideration at market value to avoid HMRC challenging the transfer as artificially priced.
- A final CT return in the old entity picking up the disposal.

### Option B — Share-for-share exchange

The shareholders of Upskill Energy Ltd exchange their shares for shares in a new HoldCo, which then holds Upskill Energy Ltd as a subsidiary. This is typically done with HMRC clearance under s.138 TCGA 1992 to avoid triggering CGT.

This plan does **not** recommend Option B because:
- It doesn't actually clean up the trading entity name — Upskill Energy Ltd still sits underneath.
- It adds a holding company layer the business doesn't need yet.
- It costs more in legal and tax advice than Option A for a small business.

**If the founder wants share-for-share later** (e.g. for exit tax planning), that is a separate future exercise. Get the operating entity clean first.

## 3. Consideration for the asset transfer — worked example

For a small EdTech SaaS business that has not previously been valued, a typical approach is:

1. Commission a simple asset valuation from the accountant (cost: £300–£600 if not included in their standard fee). This establishes:
   - Value of goodwill (often set at 1–2x current ARR for small SaaS)
   - Value of customer list (nominal if already covered in goodwill)
   - Value of IP (code, content, trademarks — often nominal book value)
   - Value of any tangible assets
2. The Asset Transfer Agreement records the consideration — usually structured as an **intercompany loan** from the new company to the old company at the agreed value.
3. When the old company is wound up, the loan is released. This may have tax consequences — get advice.

**Critical:** talk to the accountant **before** committing to a number. Underpricing can trigger a transfer pricing / market-value challenge from HMRC. Overpricing creates cash strain on the new entity.

## 4. Future states (indicative, not planned)

### If a co-founder joins later

| # | Shareholder | Shares | % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Founder | 80 | 80% |
| 2 | Co-founder | 20 | 20% |

Bring in via issue of new shares (dilutes founder to 80%), not transfer. Pair with a Shareholders' Agreement that covers: vesting, drag-along, tag-along, good leaver / bad leaver, pre-emption.

### If an EMI pool is set up for employees

Typical UK practice: carve out **5–15%** of fully-diluted shares into an EMI option pool for key hires. Done via board resolution granting options after HMRC notification for an EMI scheme.

### If investment is taken

Will likely require:
- A second share class (Preferred / Series Seed) with liquidation preference
- Amended articles (replacing Model Articles)
- Investors' Rights Agreement
- Board consent matters

Again — deal with when it happens, don't pre-build.
