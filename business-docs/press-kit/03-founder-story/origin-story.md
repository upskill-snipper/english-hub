## The longer version — the problem that started it

The first version of this story is not about a product. It's about a specific student.

[FOUNDER NAME] was [teaching / tutoring] a Year 11 class at [SCHOOL / PRIVATE TUITION]. There was one student — let's call her [NAME / "M"] — who was predicted a grade 5 in English Language. She was a precise, careful writer. She followed the structure exactly as it had been taught. And she was losing marks, every time, on Question 5 of Paper 1 — the big 40-mark creative writing task.

The reason she was losing marks was visible to anyone who knew the mark scheme: she was describing things when she should have been showing them. "The tree was big" where the mark scheme wanted "The oak rose above the fence like a second roof." She needed to see, specifically, in her own writing, where the gap was. She needed a second draft within the hour.

She wrote her essay on a Tuesday morning. [FOUNDER NAME] marked it on Sunday evening. The feedback went back on Monday. By then she had written two more essays with the same problem.

A private tutor would have marked it in the same evening. Most of her classmates couldn't afford one.

That one mismatch — between how fast students needed feedback and how fast any human teacher in Britain could provide it — became the whole thesis.

## The second insight — generic feedback doesn't work

[FOUNDER NAME]'s second insight came later: the first wave of "AI writing helpers" was not the answer. Generic grammar checkers and chatbots would happily mark a GCSE essay — and produce advice that was, by any examiner's standard, wrong. They would praise the wrong things. They would miss the assessment objective entirely. They would give a grade that didn't line up with any real mark scheme.

The English Hub's breakthrough was refusing to treat essay marking as a generic writing task. Every exam board's mark scheme is different. Every set text has its own language of analysis. Every assessment objective rewards something specific. The AI marker was built on the real documents — the mark schemes, the examiner's reports, the banded descriptors — so that when it gives a student a 7 out of 8 on AO2, the reason maps to the sentence in their essay that actually did or didn't meet the criterion.

That's the bit that took longest to build, and it is what teachers now tell us makes them willing to recommend us.

## The third insight — price is not an afterthought

The third decision was about pricing, and it is the one the founder is most proud of.

At £40 an hour for tutoring, a student using a tutor for one hour a week across the two years of GCSE English would cost a family around £3,300. That is a life-changing sum for most households. The English Hub costs £9.99 a month, or £79.99 for a whole year — 1/50th the cost of private tutoring. And for students on free school meals, we give it away, verified via the school. The pricing is deliberate. Any decision that makes The English Hub more expensive is a decision that excludes the students who most need it.

## What the student actually does, in one sentence

A student opens the app, writes an essay on a set text or a Language paper question, taps "mark my essay", and in under 30 seconds sees their grade, their assessment-objective breakdown, the quotes from their own essay that earned or lost marks, and a rewrite of a single sentence to show what a grade-9 version would look like.

They then do it again. And again. Until the Tuesday-morning essay becomes the Tuesday-evening essay, and the feedback loop closes.
