# The English Hub — Voice and Tone

**Voice** is who we are — it does not change.
**Tone** is how we speak in a given situation — it changes all the time.

This document defines both.

---

## Our voice in three attributes

### 1. Clear

We write the way the best English teachers speak. Short sentences. Active verbs. No throat-clearing. If a 15-year-old cannot follow our writing, we've failed.

- We write at a Year 10 reading level by default (Flesch reading ease 60+).
- We remove every word that isn't earning its place.
- We use one idea per sentence.
- We never use three words when one will do.

**Yes:** "Paste your essay. Get a grade in 30 seconds."
**No:** "Leverage our AI-powered assessment engine to receive comprehensive feedback on your written work in near-real-time."

### 2. Confident

We know the mark scheme. We know what a grade 9 looks like. We do not hedge, apologise, or pad our sentences with "just" or "might" or "kind of."

- We state, we do not suggest.
- We tell students what to do, not what they could "maybe try."
- We own our product. We do not call it a "humble attempt" or a "little tool."

**Yes:** "Start with the verb. Show the examiner what the writer is doing, not what the text is about."
**No:** "You might want to perhaps think about starting with a verb, if that works for you."

### 3. Kind

We are kind because kindness is the most important teaching tool. A student who feels mocked stops writing. A student who feels seen writes more. We never make fun of a student's work, a teacher's subject knowledge, a parent's anxiety about grades, or a school's budget.

- We never imply English is easy, or that the student should already know something.
- We never compare students to each other.
- We never write copy that shames a family for not buying private tutoring.
- When a student is struggling, our tone softens. When a student is succeeding, our tone celebrates.

**Yes:** "Writing a grade 9 essay is hard. Here's the one change that makes the biggest difference."
**No:** "Most students get this wrong — don't be one of them."

## Tone — by situation

Our voice stays the same. Our tone shifts depending on where the reader is, what they are trying to do, and how they feel.

### Product (in-app copy, buttons, empty states, microcopy)
- Direct and helpful.
- Use the second person ("you"), never "the student."
- Short. Buttons are verbs: "Mark my essay", "Start mock exam", "See my grade."
- Empty states are encouraging, not blank: "No essays yet. Paste your first one and I'll mark it in 30 seconds."
- Never use exclamation marks in buttons. One exclamation mark per screen, max.

**Example:** "Nice — that's a grade 7. Your AO1 is strong. Here's the single change that would get you to 8."

### Support (help articles, live chat, email replies)
- Calm, patient, explicitly on the reader's side.
- Always answer the question asked, *then* expand.
- Always offer a next action.
- Use the reader's name if we have it.
- Apologise when something is our fault, once, and then fix it.

**Example:** "Hi Aisha — sorry about that. The essay feedback took longer than usual because of a queue on our side. I've given you a free month on the house. If it happens again, reply to this message and I'll look personally. — [NAME], Founder."

### Marketing (landing pages, ads, social, blog)
- Warm and confident. Focus on the student outcome, not the feature.
- Lead with a concrete promise, not a vague benefit.
- Humour is allowed. Cynicism is not.
- Never imply teachers aren't doing their jobs. We are on the same team as teachers.
- Never use fear marketing ("you'll fail your GCSEs if you don't use us").

**Example headline:** "Your essay, marked in 30 seconds. At 3am, the night before the mock."

### Crisis (outages, security incidents, bad news)
- Clear. Honest. Human. No corporate-speak.
- Lead with what happened, in plain language.
- Explain what we are doing about it.
- Say sorry, once, and mean it.
- Give a timeline — even a rough one.
- Update proactively.

**Example:** "At 09:12 today our essay-marking service went down for about 40 minutes. No student data was lost. If you had an essay marked during that window that failed, it is now processed and you will see the result. I'm sorry — this shouldn't happen during mock season. We are moving the queue to a more resilient provider this week. — [NAME], Founder."

### Press and investor
- Serious, composed, precise.
- Numbers only from `04-boilerplate/key-statistics.md`.
- No hedging verbs ("we hope", "we're trying"). Replace with "we will" or "we are."
- No exclamation marks at all.
- No emojis.

**Example:** "The English Hub today announced a £[X] seed round led by [INVESTOR]. The capital will fund the expansion of the platform's AI marking into the Cambridge IGCSE curriculum and the opening of a Gulf region go-to-market team."

### Student-facing social (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts)
- Informal, conversational, but never sloppy.
- Emojis are OK in captions, never in alt text.
- Jokes are OK. Sarcasm sparingly.
- Absolutely never talk down to students or use "kids these days" energy.

## Words we use

- "Mark scheme", "examiner", "assessment objective", "AO1/AO2/AO3/AO4/AO5/AO6" — we are specific, because specificity is how students trust us.
- "Set text" (not "the book they're studying").
- "Your essay", "your grade", "your teacher" — we keep the student at the centre.
- "We" for the company. Not "the team" or "the company" — "we."
- "You" for the reader, always.

## Words we don't use

- "Leverage", "empower", "unlock", "revolutionise", "disrupt", "game-changer", "best-in-class", "cutting-edge", "AI-driven", "transform" (when used as a verb on landing pages).
- "Kids", "children" — we say "students."
- "Passionate about", "excited to announce" — replace with the actual thing.
- "Please note" — just state the note.
- "Our amazing team" — state what the team did.
- "Don't forget to" — tell the reader what to do instead.
- "Click here" — use a real verb that describes the action.

---

## The one-line test

Before publishing anything — a tweet, a landing page, an email, a press release — read it out loud once. If any sentence in it would be embarrassing coming out of a real teacher's mouth, rewrite that sentence.
