When your first language is Arabic, English is hard in specific, predictable ways — and almost none of them are about ability. Arabic has no "a/an". It uses ال differently from "the". Word order, tenses and prepositions map across differently. The English Hub teaches around exactly those patterns, in English and Arabic, so the language finally clicks.

Why English trips up Arabic speakers

These are the most common transfer points — the places where Arabic and English work differently. They are learnable rules, not mistakes about intelligence:

✗ "I bought book yesterday."   ✓ "I bought a book yesterday."
العربي ما عنده أداة تنكير — في الإنجليزي كل اسم مفرد معدود يحتاج a / an / the.
  • Articles (a / an / the): the single biggest grammar gap for Arabic speakers — Arabic has no indefinite article and uses ال differently from English "the".
  • Word order: moving comfortably to English subject–verb–object patterns.
  • Tenses: present perfect and the past simple / continuous / perfect, which don't map one-to-one from Arabic.
  • Prepositions (in / on / at), subject–verb agreement, plurals and uncountable nouns, and phrasal verbs (get up, look after, give in).
