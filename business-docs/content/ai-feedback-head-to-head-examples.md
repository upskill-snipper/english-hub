## The student essay (identical across all three marks)

**Question:** How does Shakespeare present the character of Macbeth as a tragic figure in the play *Macbeth*?

**Year 10 student response — 400 words, timed exam conditions:**

> Shakespeare presents Macbeth as a tragic figure whose downfall is caused by his own ambition and the manipulation of others around him. At the start of the play, Macbeth is described as "brave" and a "worthy gentleman", suggesting he is a hero. The Captain's line, "For brave Macbeth — well he deserves that name", shows how much the other characters respect him. This makes his later fall from grace more tragic because the audience has seen how great he was before.
>
> However, Macbeth's ambition quickly takes over. After the witches tell him he will be king, he says "Stars, hide your fires; Let not light see my black and deep desires." The use of the imperative "hide" and the contrast between "light" and "black" shows that Macbeth knows his thoughts are wrong but he still wants to pursue them. This is when his tragic journey really begins, because he is choosing ambition over morality.
>
> Lady Macbeth also plays a big role in Macbeth's downfall. She calls him a "coward" and questions his manhood, saying "When you durst do it, then you were a man." This emotional manipulation pushes Macbeth to commit the murder even though he has doubts. Shakespeare shows through this that Macbeth is not fully in control of his own fate, which is a key feature of a tragic hero.
>
> After killing Duncan, Macbeth says "I am afraid to think what I have done." This shows his guilt and the psychological torment that follows the murder. The short, blunt sentence structure emphasises his shock. This is classic tragic hero behaviour — the protagonist realises the cost of his actions but cannot undo them.
>
> By the end of the play, Macbeth has become a tyrant and says "Life's but a walking shadow, a poor player". The metaphor of life as a pointless performance shows how empty his ambition has left him. Despite being warned by the apparitions, he still believes he is invincible, and this hubris leads to his death.
>
> In conclusion, Shakespeare presents Macbeth as a tragic figure by showing his descent from a respected war hero to a guilt-ridden tyrant. His ambition, combined with external manipulation, makes him a classic example of a tragic hero whose own flaws cause his downfall.

## MARK 1 — AQA GCSE English Literature (8702), Paper 1 Section A

**Spec:** AQA 8702 · **AO weighting on this question:** AO1 (12) · AO2 (12) · AO3 (6) · AO4 (4) · **Total: 34 marks** (30 + 4 for AO4)

### Band placement
**Level 5 (Convincing, critical analysis and exploration) — bottom of band · 25/34 marks · Grade 7**

### AO1 — Read, understand and respond to texts · 10/12
**Examiner comment (AQA mark-scheme lexis: "thoughtful considered response"):**
> Your response is a *thoughtful considered response* to the task. You track Macbeth's tragic arc from "brave" soldier through ambitious regicide to hollow tyrant, and you select well-judged evidence at each stage ("brave Macbeth", "stars, hide your fires", "life's but a walking shadow"). Your conceptualised overview — ambition + manipulation = downfall — is clear from the outset.
>
> **To push from Level 5 to Level 6 ("critical, exploratory, conceptualised response"):** your conclusion restates the opening rather than complicating it. Strong Level 6 answers return to the opening reading and *unsettle* it — for instance, ask whether Shakespeare wants us to see Macbeth as a tragic victim of the witches' prophecy or whether the prophecy only reveals a flaw that was always there.

### AO2 — Analyse the language, form and structure · 9/12
**Examiner comment (AQA mark-scheme lexis: "analysis of writer's methods"):**
> Good *analysis of writer's methods* with subject terminology used accurately (imperative, contrast, metaphor, sentence structure). The "hide/light/black" analysis is the strongest moment — you identify the contrast and you explain what it tells us about Macbeth's moral awareness.
>
> **To reach Level 6:** two fixes. **First**, zoom into word-level analysis more often — "durst" is doing more work than you acknowledge (archaic, challenging, provocative). **Second**, address *form* (soliloquy vs dialogue) and *structure* (where in the play each quote sits, and why Shakespeare placed it there). AQA Level 6 requires all three — language, form, structure — not just language.

### AO3 — Context · 5/6
**Examiner comment (AQA mark-scheme lexis: "contextual factors"):**
> *Contextual factors* are integrated ("classic tragic hero", "hubris") rather than bolted on, which is exactly what AQA rewards. You could strengthen this by naming the Jacobean context once — James I's interest in witchcraft (*Daemonologie*, 1597) and the Gunpowder Plot of 1605 — to anchor why regicide was such a loaded act for Shakespeare's first audience.

### AO4 — Vocabulary, sentence structures, spelling and punctuation · 1/4
**Examiner comment (AQA mark-scheme lexis: "technical accuracy"):**
> *Technical accuracy* is broadly secure but your vocabulary ("big role", "classic tragic hero behaviour", "really begins") drops into informal register at points. For the full 4 marks, aim for consistent academic register throughout.

### What to do next (AQA-specific)
1. Rewrite one paragraph applying the **language + form + structure** checklist (AO2 Level 6 requirement).
2. Add **one Jacobean context anchor** (James I, regicide, divine right) in a future response.
3. Upgrade three informal phrases to academic register in this essay.

### Grade predictor (AQA Lit Paper 1 Section A, 2024 boundaries)
**This question: Grade 7.** Extrapolated paper total (if Section B and Paper 2 match): **Grade 7–8 range.**

## MARK 2 — Edexcel (Pearson) GCSE English Literature (1ET0), Paper 1

**Spec:** Pearson Edexcel 1ET0 · **AO weighting on Shakespeare question:** AO1 (8) · AO2 (12) · AO4 (4) · **AO3 is NOT assessed on this question** · **Total: 24 marks (+ 4 AO4)**

### Band placement
**Level 4 (Thoughtful, developed response) — middle of band · 17/24 marks · Grade 6–7 borderline**

### AO1 — Response to text, supported by textual references · 6/8
**Examiner comment (Edexcel mark-scheme lexis: "thoughtful, developed response"):**
> A *thoughtful, developed response* that maintains a clear line of argument about Macbeth as tragic figure. References are apt and show good knowledge of the play's trajectory.
>
> **To move into Level 5 ("critical and evaluative"):** be *more selective*. Level 5 answers on Edexcel don't cover every Act — they pick two or three moments and interrogate them. Cut the conclusion's summary of the whole play and replace it with a reconsideration of a single quote.

### AO2 — Language, form and structure · 7/12
**Examiner comment (Edexcel mark-scheme lexis: "analysis of writer's craft"):**
> Some *analysis of writer's craft* — the imperative "hide" and the contrast language/black work is solid. However, Edexcel Level 5 requires **methods analysis on every piece of evidence you use**. In your essay, four of your six quotes have no methods analysis attached (they're used as narrative proof rather than analytical evidence).
>
> **To push higher:** rewrite so every quote has at minimum one methods observation. Edexcel reports consistently highlight this as the Level 4 → Level 5 gap.

### AO4 — Spelling, punctuation and grammar (including vocabulary) · 3/4
**Examiner comment (Edexcel mark-scheme lexis: "high degree of accuracy"):**
> *High degree of accuracy* in SPaG. Minor vocabulary slips ("big role", "really begins") prevent the full 4.

### What to do next (Edexcel-specific)
1. **Do NOT add more context** — AO3 is not assessed on this question on Edexcel. The Jacobean detail suggested for AQA would waste time here.
2. **Cut breadth, add depth.** Edexcel rewards fewer quotes analysed more deeply over comprehensive coverage.
3. **Methods analysis on every quote** — this is the single Level 4 → 5 move on Edexcel.

### Grade predictor (Edexcel 1ET0 Paper 1, 2024 boundaries)
**This question: Grade 6.** Extrapolated paper total: **Grade 6–7 range.**

## MARK 3 — OCR GCSE English Literature (J352), Paper 1

**Spec:** OCR J352 · **AO weighting on Shakespeare question (Section A):** AO1 (10) · AO2 (10) · AO3 (5) · AO4 (5) · **Total: 30 marks**

### Band placement
**Level 5 (Considered, developed response with perceptive exploration) — bottom · 21/30 marks · Grade 7**

### AO1 — Read, understand and respond · 7/10
**Examiner comment (OCR mark-scheme lexis: "considered, developed response"):**
> A *considered, developed response* with a clear conceptual thread. OCR Level 6 ("perceptive, convincing, exploration") would require you to move beyond the essay's current reading (ambition + manipulation = downfall) into something more *interpretative* — for example, arguing that Macbeth is *more* tragic because Lady Macbeth's manipulation only succeeds by exploiting an insecurity he already holds.

### AO2 — Analysis of writer's methods · 6/10
**Examiner comment (OCR mark-scheme lexis: "analysis of writer's methods, including effects on the reader"):**
> OCR is distinctive in explicitly rewarding *effects on the reader*. Your essay analyses methods but rarely names the effect. For example: "the imperative 'hide' shows Macbeth wants to conceal his ambition" identifies the method but not the effect. A Level 6 rewrite: "the imperative 'hide' positions the audience as witnesses to Macbeth's self-censorship; Shakespeare implicates us in a soliloquy he would rather not be heard, making us complicit in the tragedy."
>
> **To push higher:** add a reader-effect sentence after every language point.

### AO3 — Contextual understanding · 4/5
**Examiner comment (OCR mark-scheme lexis: "relevant contextual understanding"):**
> *Relevant contextual understanding* via your naming of tragic hero conventions and hubris — OCR rewards literary-tradition context (Aristotelian tragedy, morality plays) even more than historical context, so your instinct is correct. To reach the full 5, name one specific convention — *hamartia*, *peripeteia*, or *anagnorisis* — and attach it to a specific moment in your essay.

### AO4 — Accurate use of vocabulary, sentence structures, spelling and punctuation · 4/5
**Examiner comment:** As above — broadly secure, some informal register slips.

### What to do next (OCR-specific)
1. **Add a reader-effect sentence** after every language point. This is the single biggest move on OCR.
2. **Name one tragic-convention term** (hamartia / peripeteia / anagnorisis).
3. **Re-cast the conclusion** as an interpretive claim, not a summary.

### Grade predictor (OCR J352 Paper 1 Section A, 2024 boundaries)
