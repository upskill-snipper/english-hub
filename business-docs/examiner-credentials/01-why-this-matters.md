# Why formal examiner IP assignment matters — the valuation case

**Audience:** Founders, board, prospective investors, deal advisors
**Length:** ~1,500 words
**TL;DR:** Without formal written IP assignment, the examiner credential lives with the individual, not the company. A buyer will not pay for an asset that is not transferable. The spread between "examiner roster documented and assigned" and "handshake arrangement" is conservatively £200K to £500K of exit value for a business at the size of The English Hub.

---

## 1. The core legal point

English IP law draws a clear line between:

- **Employee-created work**, where copyright in a literary work created by an employee in the course of employment vests automatically in the employer under s.11(2) Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 ("CDPA"); and
- **Contractor-created work**, where copyright vests in the **author** (the contractor) by default, unless there is a **written assignment** to the other party (s.90(3) CDPA — "an assignment of copyright is not effective unless it is in writing signed by or on behalf of the assignor").

A freelance examiner who writes a practice paper for The English Hub, with no contract, **owns the copyright** in that practice paper. The English Hub has at best an implied licence to use it for the purpose it was commissioned for. It does not own it. It cannot freely modify, re-licence, or transfer it on sale.

Worse, an implied licence is narrower than most founders think. Robin Ray v Classic FM [1998] FSR 622 confirmed that an implied licence only goes as far as is necessary to give business efficacy to the original arrangement — and it is **not transferable** to a third party without the author's consent.

## 2. What this means for "written by a former AQA examiner"

A claim of that kind has two components:

1. **Factual accuracy** — there genuinely was a person with the credential involved in producing the content.
2. **Ongoing permission** — the company has the right to keep making that claim in marketing.

Permission (2) is a **personality right** — a claim about an identified living person. Under the common-law tort of passing off, and under GDPR/DPA 2018 for personal data, the company needs that person's documented consent to use their name, likeness, biography and professional identity in marketing collateral. Without it, the company is one disgruntled ex-contributor away from a takedown letter.

A written agreement resolves both points. It records the fact for the evidential file and it grants the marketing permission in perpetuity.

## 3. The commercial problem on exit

When a buyer's lawyers review the data room, they run an **IP chain of title** exercise. For every asset the company claims to own, they trace it back to a signed assignment from the original author. The checklist usually looks like this:

- [ ] Employment contracts for all employees (with IP assignment clause)
- [ ] Consultancy / contractor agreements for all non-employees (with IP assignment clause)
- [ ] For any historic work where there was no contract, a signed retrospective assignment
- [ ] For any third-party content (stock images, fonts, open-source code), a licence in the name of the company
- [ ] For any personality rights used in marketing (photos, names, biographies), a signed release

If the chain breaks anywhere, the relevant asset is flagged. What happens next is a matter of negotiation, but the options from the buyer's side are:

1. **Price chip** — reduce the headline price by the estimated value of the flagged asset.
2. **Specific indemnity** — make the seller personally liable if the asset turns out to be contested. This exposes the founder to uncapped liability for years.
3. **Escrow / retention** — hold back a portion of the price until the defect is cured.
4. **Walk** — rare, but possible if the flagged assets are central to the commercial story.

For a business whose marketing leads with "written by examiners", the examiner credential is a central asset. A buyer will not accept option (1) as a token price chip. They will pursue option (2) or (3) aggressively.

## 4. The quantified impact

Valuation math for a UK digital education business at The English Hub's stage typically looks like this:

- EBITDA or revenue multiple (let us use 3× ARR as a sector-benchmark for EdTech content businesses at £1m–£3m ARR).
- At £1m ARR, headline valuation is £3m.
- The "examiner written" claim is a material differentiator — without it, the business is a generic GCSE content site competing with BBC Bitesize, Seneca, Save My Exams and Quizlet. The premium the claim supports is, conservatively, **10–20% of headline value**. That is £300K–£600K.
- A buyer who sees an un-contracted examiner roster will do one of two things:
  - (a) **Escrow 50–80% of that premium** pending remediation, holding £150K–£480K for 12–24 months; or
  - (b) **Discount the headline** by the full premium if they do not believe remediation is feasible.

Either way, the founder loses real money. **£200K–£500K is a conservative central estimate** of the swing between "tidy" and "un-contracted". For a higher-multiple or higher-ARR business the number is larger.

It is also worth noting that the work to close the gap is cheap. A solicitor in a regional UK firm will draft an IP assignment template for a few hundred pounds; signing 10–30 contributors to it is an afternoon of e-signature admin. The ROI is absurd.

## 5. The hidden risks beyond valuation

Even before an exit, under-contracted examiner relationships create live operational risk:

- **Takedown risk.** An ex-contributor writes to the ICO or to the company claiming misuse of their name and image. The company has to pull marketing, issue corrections, and potentially pay damages.
- **Exam board risk.** An examiner who has not signed a confidentiality warranty might — deliberately or accidentally — share unreleased mark schemes or confidential meeting notes that they are contractually bound to keep private. The exam board's complaint does not go to the examiner alone; it goes to The English Hub too, by association, and becomes a reputational problem on the website.
- **Competitor risk.** Without a non-compete, a lead examiner-contributor can walk tomorrow and set up a rival business, taking their credential and their goodwill with them.
- **Payroll / employment status risk.** HMRC takes an interest in relationships that look like employment but are contracted as freelance. A tight written agreement with an explicit contractor-status clause is the best defence against an IR35 or status reassessment.

## 6. What "doing this properly" looks like

A properly run examiner IP programme has five visible elements:

1. **One register** (see `04-evidence/examiner-register.csv`) with every contributor, current and past.
2. **One agreement per contributor**, signed and dated, stored centrally, indexed to the register.
3. **One verification file per contributor**, with CV, exam board evidence, and a contemporary photo/bio release.
4. **One marketing review process** (see `03-disclosure-restrictions/marketing-claims-review-template.md`) that every outbound claim passes through before publication.
5. **One owner** inside the company — usually the CEO at this stage, later the Head of Content or General Counsel — who is named as accountable for the register being current.

A buyer who sees those five elements in diligence immediately de-risks the credential question. It is perhaps the single highest-leverage piece of pre-exit housekeeping available at this stage of the business.

## 7. Timing

Do this **before** a growth equity round, **well before** any formal sale conversation, and certainly **before** the next hire of an examiner contributor. Each month that passes without the programme in place creates more historic work that needs a retrospective assignment, and retrospective assignments are harder to get the longer the gap (people move, retire, or lose interest).

The template pack in this folder is the first draft. The next step is a 60–90 minute conversation with a solicitor to tailor it to the company's specific facts. Everything after that is administration.
