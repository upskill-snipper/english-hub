# Why buyers care about examiner credentials

**Audience:** Founders, board, deal advisors.
**Purpose:** To put the examiner credential programme into the specific language that buyers, their lawyers and their bankers use — so that what we build now is directly responsive to what gets tested in diligence.

---

## 1. Who "the buyer" is

For a business like The English Hub, realistic acquirers fall into three camps:

1. **Strategic consolidators in UK EdTech.** Think of an established online learning brand buying a smaller specialist to broaden its subject coverage or acquire a strong brand in a segment. These buyers understand the examiner framing well and will test it forensically because it matters to their own brand.
2. **International publishers and education groups.** These buyers have deep pockets and global brands. They understand editorial governance and are very familiar with IP chain-of-title work — they do it as standard practice.
3. **Private equity and growth equity.** PE buyers may be less steeped in education-specific nuance, but their lawyers are extremely experienced at IP diligence and will not tolerate sloppy paperwork. They are the most likely to apply the largest price chips when they find gaps, because they are pricing the business for a further onward sale.

All three care about the same thing: **can the company credibly claim, post-acquisition, what it claims today about its content and its people?**

## 2. What buyers actually check

Here is what a typical IP / commercial diligence stream looks like for a content business of our size:

1. **Inventory the content.** List the material assets: lesson plans, videos, practice papers, rubrics, worked answers, marketing copy, photographs, brand marks, domain names, etc.
2. **Trace each asset to its author.** For every asset, who wrote/created/recorded it?
3. **Trace each author to an agreement.** For every author, is there a written contract with an IP assignment? Is it signed? Is it in the company's name (not the founder's personal name)? Does it cover the work in question?
4. **Separately trace marketing claims to supporting evidence.** For every factual claim in marketing ("written by former AQA examiners", "14 years experience", "reviewed by a Cambridge assessor"), is there a file that proves it?
5. **Sample interview.** Often the buyer's lawyers will email a handful of contributors directly (after completion, but with an exclusive period clause) to verify that the contributors believe they assigned IP, believe their biography is accurate, and are not unhappy about how their name has been used. This sometimes happens in pre-completion diligence too, under NDA, with the seller's consent.

If the chain is clean, the asset is "in scope" of the acquisition and is fully valued. If the chain is broken, the asset is flagged. A flagged asset leads to one of these outcomes:

- A **carve-out** from the warranties (meaning the buyer will not rely on the asset, and implicitly discounts it).
- A **specific indemnity** (meaning the seller personally guarantees the outcome if the asset turns out to be contested post-completion).
- A **price chip** — a direct reduction in headline price.
- An **escrow** or **retention** — the buyer withholds part of the price until the defect is cured.
- A **fundamental "don't buy"** — unusual, but it happens where the flagged asset is the reason the buyer is interested in the first place.

## 3. The specific risks for examiner content

For a business that leans on examiner credentials in its marketing, the following are the concrete failure modes a buyer will test for:

### 3.1 Missing IP assignment for core content

Scenario: A former AQA examiner wrote 50 practice papers in 2022–2023 on a handshake basis. There is no contract. The papers are published on our site today.

**Buyer's view:** The company does not own the copyright in those papers. The author can demand removal, can demand royalties, can license the material to a competitor, or can block reuse after acquisition. The company's published library is materially encumbered.

**Buyer's likely response:** Specific indemnity plus requirement to obtain signed retrospective assignments within 60 days of completion, failing which a price chip of [value of affected content].

### 3.2 Marketing claims not supported by evidence

Scenario: The homepage says "written by experienced examiners" but there is no register, no signed agreements, and no CVs on file. The founder "knows" who the contributors are but has not documented it.

**Buyer's view:** The central differentiator in the marketing is unverified. Either (a) it is true and could be documented but has not been, which is negligence; or (b) it is partially or fully false, which is misrepresentation and potential Advertising Standards Authority exposure.

**Buyer's likely response:** Full warranty on marketing truthfulness with uncapped liability for misrepresentation, plus a requirement to audit and remediate every marketing claim pre-completion.

### 3.3 Personality rights not licensed

Scenario: Examiner photographs and biographies are on the website with verbal permission only. One of the examiners had a falling-out with the founder and has asked, politely, for their photograph to come down.

**Buyer's view:** This is a live takedown risk. Each "named examiner" on the site is a potential future takedown story. The value of the marketing claim depreciates with each removal.

**Buyer's likely response:** Requirement to obtain written permission from every named individual pre-completion, with deletion of unpermissioned content.

### 3.4 Examiner confidentiality breach

Scenario: A blog post quotes "our examiner" saying "we saw in this year's standardisation meetings that students are struggling with context-based marking". Technically this might be a paraphrase of a generic observation, but a buyer's lawyer will read it as potentially leaking confidential meeting content from AQA.

**Buyer's view:** This is a regulatory, reputational and contractual risk for the examiner personally — and by extension for the company. Even the appearance of it in the archives is a problem.

**Buyer's likely response:** Requirement to audit the content archive for any statements that could be read as sourced from confidential material, plus revised editorial policy going forward.

### 3.5 Examiner relationships in the founder's personal name

Scenario: The founder personally recruited all the examiner contributors, the emails go back and forth from the founder's personal Gmail, fees were paid from a personal bank account in the early days before the company was incorporated, and several biographies on the site were written by the founder for the contributors using the founder's own words.

**Buyer's view:** The relationships — and therefore the credentials — may sit with the founder personally, not with the company. In a share sale, buying the company doesn't necessarily transfer those relationships. In an asset sale, specific consents are needed.

**Buyer's likely response:** Requirement to obtain signed written confirmations from every contributor that the relationship is with the company, not with the founder personally, and to migrate any remaining personal correspondence and personal payments onto company systems with a clear handover note.

## 4. What "good" looks like

A buyer presented with the following artefacts will, in nearly every case, de-risk the examiner credential question within the first week of diligence and move on.

- **A single register** covering every contributor, current and historic, with their verification status.
- **A folder per contributor** containing the signed agreement, the verified CV, the retrospective assignment where relevant, and the biography/photograph with written permission.
- **A documented editorial process** showing that every marketing claim about examiners is checked against the register pre-publication.
- **A short memo** from the founder or General Counsel summarising the programme: how many contributors, how many are under agreement, how many have been verified, what retrospective work remains, and the timeline for completing any gaps.
- **Clean historical email / Slack / drive records** showing company (not personal) correspondence with contributors, and company payments.

A buyer who sees all of this assumes competence and moves on. A buyer who has to dig for any of it assumes incompetence and starts escrowing.

## 5. Red flags that destroy trust

A buyer will lose confidence immediately if they see any of the following:

- An enthusiastic list on the website of "our examiners" that does not match any internal document.
- Conflicting versions of the examiner roster (the pitch deck has 8, the website has 6, the press release has 10).
- Any suggestion that "we have insider knowledge" or "we see the exam board from the inside".
- Founder emails to contributors using casual language that suggests the founder does not understand the IP or confidentiality issues.
- A founder-owned laptop that contains the only copy of the examiner register.
- An examiner contributor who is, in fact, the founder's partner or family member, not disclosed as a related-party relationship.
- Any prior dispute with a contributor over credit, pay or IP that is not disclosed in the diligence process.

Fixing these before the process starts is an order of magnitude cheaper than fixing them during the process.

## 6. Timing

The examiner credential programme should be **in place and mature** by the time a formal sale process starts. Attempting to build it mid-diligence is the worst of all worlds: it creates a paper trail showing the founder *did not have* the programme, which is itself evidence of negligence; contributors are harder to mobilise under time pressure; and the founder's attention is split between diligence responses and remedial paperwork.

A reasonable minimum time to reach maturity is **six months** from zero, assuming modest contributor numbers (under 20). Start now. The advisors who lead the sale process will thank you for it.

## 7. Summary

Buyers care about examiner credentials because examiner credentials are the **differentiator** our marketing relies on. If the credentials transfer with the company, they are worth the premium our positioning implies. If they do not, they are worth nothing to the buyer at all. The entire programme in this folder is designed to produce evidence that they transfer.
