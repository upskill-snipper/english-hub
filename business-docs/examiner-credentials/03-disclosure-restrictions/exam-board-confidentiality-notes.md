# Exam board confidentiality — what examiners cannot share

**Audience:** Anyone at The English Hub who commissions, edits, reviews or publishes content from an examiner contributor.
**Purpose:** A plain-English reference so that nobody in the company ever accidentally asks an examiner to breach their duty to an Awarding Organisation.

> This document is a summary of the **operational risk** and the **commercial red lines**. It is not legal advice and it does not replace the confidentiality clauses in the contributor agreement. If in doubt, do not publish.

---

## 1. The basic rule

Every person appointed as an examiner, assessor, moderator or team leader by a UK Awarding Organisation (AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, Cambridge Assessment, WJEC, Eduqas and others) signs a **confidentiality undertaking** as a condition of their appointment.

The undertakings vary in detail between boards, but they converge on the same principle: **anything an examiner sees, hears, writes, marks or discusses in their capacity as an examiner is confidential to the awarding organisation** unless and until that awarding organisation has published it.

An examiner who contributes to The English Hub wears two hats: their examiner hat (bound by their board's confidentiality terms) and their contributor hat (bound by our Examiner Contributor Agreement). **The confidentiality duty to the board applies even when they have their contributor hat on.** It does not go away when they are writing for us, nor when they are chatting with our editors about a paper.

We must not ask an examiner to take off their examiner hat. Doing so is unprofessional, may expose us to legal claims from the awarding organisation, and will trigger precisely the kind of reputational story that destroys consumer trust in an education brand.

## 2. The red list — do not ask for and do not accept

An examiner contributor must **never** share the following with anyone at The English Hub, including editors, writers, technologists, marketers or the CEO:

### 2.1 Unreleased past papers

Drafts of GCSE or A-Level papers that have not yet been sat by candidates, or that have been sat but have not yet been publicly released by the board, are confidential. Even the **structure** or **topic choice** of an unreleased paper is confidential — not just the exact text.

**Red flag prompts to avoid asking:**
- *"Can you tell me roughly what's coming up in the summer paper?"*
- *"What topics should we prioritise in our revision content because you've seen what's coming?"*
- *"Any hints about this year's texts?"*

### 2.2 Unpublished mark schemes

Mark schemes are released publicly after the paper has been sat and results have been published. Prior to that, they are confidential. **A draft mark scheme an examiner has seen in a standardisation meeting is confidential indefinitely** — the final published version is what we can refer to, not the draft.

**Red flag prompts to avoid asking:**
- *"Do the marks go 1/1/1 or 1/2/0 on this question? I know you've seen the real mark scheme."*
- *"Our candidate answer — what would get it the top band in the real scheme?"*

### 2.3 Candidate scripts

**Actual candidate answers written in an examination are confidential to the candidate and to the board forever.** An examiner may never share a script, a photograph of a script, a transcription of a script, or a paraphrase of a script that could identify the candidate or the examination session.

**Red flag prompts to avoid asking:**
- *"Can you share a few example answers you've marked so we can show pupils what level 6 looks like?"*
- *"Could you send us anonymised bits of real answers?"* — note: "anonymised" does **not** make it OK. The candidate did not consent and the board did not authorise.

**What we can do instead:** commission the examiner to write **fresh, simulated** candidate answers at each level of the mark scheme, drawing on their professional judgment. These are the examiner's own work, assignable to us under the contributor agreement, and safe to publish.

### 2.4 Confidential meeting minutes and internal discussions

Standardisation meetings, examiner conferences, Ofqual liaison meetings, subject team meetings and similar events are typically run under explicit or implicit confidentiality. **Anything said or circulated in those meetings is confidential** — including anecdotes, quotes from chief examiners, disagreements between examiners, or internal debates about marking.

**Red flag prompts to avoid asking:**
- *"What did the chief examiner say about this year's cohort?"*
- *"Any gossip from the standardisation meeting?"*
- *"What are the examiners arguing about behind the scenes?"*

### 2.5 Live moderation data, sample sizes and statistical outputs

Board-internal statistics about how a paper performed, sample sizes, moderation adjustments, grade boundaries before they are published, and similar operational data are all confidential.

### 2.6 Any material labelled "in confidence", "restricted", "for examiners only" or similar

If a document carries a confidentiality marking, it is confidential. We never ask for it, never look at it if it is sent to us by mistake, and we notify the examiner immediately if something of this kind reaches us.

## 3. The safe list — what examiners CAN share

Examiners are absolutely allowed to share, and it is useful to us, the following:

- **Their own professional opinions and judgment** about what makes a strong answer, a weak answer, or a common mistake — framed as their own expertise, not as "what the mark scheme says".
- **Publicly released past papers and mark schemes** — these are on the boards' websites and can be discussed openly.
- **Publicly released examiner reports** — the board-published commentary that follows each exam series is public and can be quoted (with attribution).
- **Published specifications and subject content guidance** — these are on the boards' websites.
- **General pedagogical knowledge** — how to teach a skill, how to structure revision, how to approach a question type. This is the examiner's professional know-how, not confidential material.
- **Simulated answers written fresh by the examiner** — not copied from real scripts. These are the examiner's original creative work and can be commissioned and assigned to us under the contributor agreement.
- **Anonymised accounts of general marking experience** — e.g. *"in my experience, students often confuse X with Y"* — as long as no specific paper, session, candidate, or meeting is identifiable.

## 4. Practical rules for editors and commissioners

Before sending a brief to an examiner contributor, the editor should:

1. **Specify the source.** The brief should explicitly say the examiner may draw **only on public specifications, past papers and examiner reports, plus their own professional judgment**.
2. **Commission simulated, not actual, material.** Never ask for "real" answers. Ask for simulated answers at each band.
3. **Avoid asking about the current/forthcoming series.** Work 12+ months behind the live exam cycle.
4. **Pay the examiner to write, not to leak.** The fee is for original creative work, not for insider information.
5. **If the examiner offers something that feels confidential, politely decline** and remind them of their obligations. Document the decline in the commissioning notes.

## 5. What to do if something confidential is shared by accident

If an editor or reviewer believes an examiner contributor has inadvertently shared confidential material:

1. **Do not publish.** Pull the affected Deliverable from the editorial workflow immediately.
2. **Do not forward or circulate the material further** inside the company.
3. **Notify the contributor**, explain what has happened, and ask them to confirm in writing whether the material is in fact confidential.
4. If it is, **delete the copies** the company holds and keep a written record that the deletion has been done.
5. If the material has already been published (e.g. in a practice paper, a blog post, a video), **remove it immediately** and replace with a freshly commissioned, clean version.
6. **Escalate to the CEO and (once appointed) the general counsel / solicitor** and record the incident in an internal register. Legal advice may be needed depending on what was shared.
7. The contributor agreement includes an indemnity for breaches of the confidentiality warranty. Do not invoke it without legal advice, and do not use the incident as a reason to threaten the contributor — this is an operational risk management exercise, not a dispute.

## 6. Marketing implications

Nothing in our marketing should ever imply that we have **insider** knowledge. Phrases like "based on the real mark scheme", "from an examiner who has seen the paper" or "written from inside the exam board" are **not permitted** under any circumstance. See `safe-marketing-claims.md` for the full list of permitted and prohibited marketing statements.

## 7. Training

Everyone at The English Hub who commissions work from examiner contributors should read this document and confirm they understand it. The CEO or Head of Content is responsible for keeping an acknowledgment log.
