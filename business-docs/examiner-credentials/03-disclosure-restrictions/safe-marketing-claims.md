# Safe marketing claims — what we can and cannot say

**Audience:** Marketing, editorial, product, and anyone drafting customer-facing copy.
**Purpose:** A quick-reference list of phrases that are safe to use, phrases that are risky, and phrases that are never to be used.

> Any claim about examiner involvement must be **truthful**, **non-misleading** and **supported by evidence on file**. The evidence must exist in the examiner register (`04-evidence/examiner-register.csv`) before the claim can appear publicly. No entry, no claim.

---

## 1. The three tests every claim must pass

Before any marketing claim about examiners or accreditation is published, ask three questions:

1. **Is it true?** Can we evidence it with a signed agreement, a verified CV and, where relevant, an exam board appointment letter?
2. **Is it precise?** Does it state exactly what the person did (e.g. "GCSE examiner for AQA") rather than a vague elevation (e.g. "exam expert")?
3. **Does it avoid implying endorsement?** Does the claim make clear that examiner experience is the individual's, not an endorsement by the exam board of our product?

If any answer is "no" or "not sure", the claim does not go out.

## 2. Safe claims — green light

These are all safe **provided they are true and evidenced**.

| Claim | Why it's safe |
|---|---|
| *"Written by a former AQA examiner"* | Factual description of an individual's prior role; not a statement about the board endorsing us. |
| *"Written by experienced examiners"* | Generic, accurate, verifiable. |
| *"Authored by subject experts with examiner experience"* | Broad and safe — implies nothing about endorsement. |
| *"Our content is reviewed by a former GCSE English examiner"* | Specific about the individual and the role, not about endorsement. |
| *"Our lead author has over 10 years of marking experience for AQA and Edexcel"* | Specific, factual, evidenced in register. |
| *"Developed with insights from a former Pearson Edexcel assistant principal examiner"* | Clear that the insight is from the individual, not an official view. |
| *"Our team includes former examiners from AQA, Edexcel and Cambridge Assessment"* | Accurate statement about personnel. |
| *"Materials aligned to the AQA 8700 specification"* | Statement about specification mapping — not about endorsement. |
| *"Covers every assessment objective in the Pearson Edexcel 1EN0 specification"* | Descriptive and evidenceable. |
| *"Practice papers modelled on the published format of Paper 1"* | Clear that it is a model, not a real paper. |

**Always pair with a disclaimer** (see section 5) where practicable — footnotes, "about" pages, landing-page small print.

## 3. Risky claims — amber light

These may be acceptable **with careful wording and legal review**. They walk a fine line and require a specific evidence trail.

| Claim | Why it's risky | What to check |
|---|---|---|
| *"Examiner-approved"* | Implies a specific act of approval by an examiner. | Verifiable approval record per item — who reviewed, when, what they signed off. If you cannot produce that, do not use this phrase. |
| *"Trusted by examiners"* | Sounds like a testimonial from an examiner community. | Only use if you have actual testimonials from multiple, named, verified examiners — with their written permission. |
| *"Marked by a real examiner"* (for a marking tool) | True only if a real examiner genuinely marked it. | Do not use automated/AI marking with this wording. It must be a human who is a verified examiner on the register. |
| *"Examiner-approved mark scheme"* | Could be read as "an official mark scheme". | Only use for a mark scheme the examiner **actually wrote** under contract. Phrase explicitly as "mark scheme written by former AQA examiner [Name]" if at all possible. |
| *"Insider knowledge"* | Suggests access to confidential exam board information. | Avoid this phrase entirely — see below. |
| *"From an AQA examiner"* | Present tense implies current appointment. | Only use if the person currently holds the appointment and has expressly agreed. Safer: "former". |

## 4. Prohibited claims — red light

**Never use these, no matter how tempting.**

| Claim | Why it is prohibited |
|---|---|
| *"AQA-approved"* / *"AQA-endorsed"* / *"Approved by AQA"* | Implies formal endorsement by the awarding organisation. Only use if we have a signed endorsement contract with AQA and the relationship is live — we do not. |
| *"Official AQA resource"* | Implies official status. False unless we have a formal licensing deal. |
| *"Edexcel-accredited"* / *"Cambridge-approved"* | Same as above. Do not use without a written endorsement agreement. |
| *"In partnership with AQA / Edexcel / Cambridge"* | Implies a commercial partnership. Unless such a partnership exists and is documented, this is actionable misrepresentation. |
| *"Insider knowledge from inside the exam board"* | Implies access to confidential material in breach of the examiner's duty to the board. Also attracts regulatory interest. |
| *"Written from the exam board's perspective"* | Implies authorisation by the board. |
| *"Based on the real, confidential mark scheme"* | Implies use of confidential material — a serious accusation we should never make against ourselves. |
| *"See next year's paper before anyone else"* | Implies knowledge of unreleased material. |
| *"Our examiners know what's coming up in this year's exam"* | Same as above. |
| *"Written by an AQA examiner"* where the examiner is in fact for a different board | Factually false. Always state the correct board. |
| *"Trained by AQA"* / *"Certified by AQA"* | Implies formal certification. Only use if there is evidence of such certification for the specific individual, not just "has examined for". |
| Any claim using the AQA, Pearson, Edexcel or Cambridge Assessment **logo** | Using these logos typically requires a licence from the relevant board. Do not use them in marketing or on the product. |

## 5. Disclaimers — the safe boilerplate

Wherever a claim about examiner involvement appears prominently in marketing or on the product, include a disclaimer. The recommended wording is:

> *"References to examiner experience describe the personal professional background of named contributors. The English Hub is independent and is not endorsed, approved, accredited or otherwise affiliated with AQA, Pearson Edexcel, OCR, Cambridge Assessment, WJEC, Eduqas or any other awarding organisation."*

This can live in a footer, on an "About our contributors" page, or in the small print under a landing-page headline. Link to it from the main marketing page rather than repeating it everywhere — but **it must exist somewhere visible**.

## 6. Handling the press and investors

Investor decks, press releases and pitch materials must follow the same rules. The temptation to round up — "we work with AQA" when really we work with a former AQA examiner — is commercially damaging and has torpedoed deals at later stages. Always use the precise, factual, unambiguous form.

## 7. Third-party claims (affiliates, PR, customer testimonials)

If a blogger, affiliate, press article or customer review describes our content using a prohibited phrase:

- **Ask the author to correct it** if we have influence over the channel (e.g. affiliates we pay, PR wires we issue).
- **Do not amplify it** by reposting, retweeting, embedding or quoting.
- **Consider whether the misleading impression is our fault** — if our own marketing is ambiguous enough that a good-faith reporter rounded up, fix our marketing.

## 8. What changes if we get a real endorsement

If The English Hub signs a formal endorsement or licensing agreement with an Awarding Organisation (see the sister folder `business-docs/exam-board-endorsement/`), the permitted claims will **expand**. But until that agreement is signed and live, everything in section 4 above remains prohibited.

## 9. Pre-publication review

Every piece of customer-facing marketing that mentions examiner involvement, awarding organisations, or accreditation must pass the `marketing-claims-review-template.md` checklist before publication. No exceptions for "quick" tweets or hot-takes.
