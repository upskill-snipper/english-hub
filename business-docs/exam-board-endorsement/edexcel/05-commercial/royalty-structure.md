# Commercial — Expected Royalty Structure for Pearson Endorsed Resources

**Purpose:** Model the expected royalty structure under a Pearson endorsement agreement, so that the founder and the board can decide what commercial terms are acceptable before entering negotiation with Publisher Relations.
**Status:** Internal working model. All numbers are placeholders until Pearson confirms its current royalty position in writing.
**Key caveat:** Pearson does not publish a fixed royalty rate. The numbers used here are drawn from published UK textbook royalty norms and must be re-priced against whatever Pearson proposes during negotiation.

---

## 1. What "royalty" means in this context

Under a typical awarding body endorsement agreement, the publisher pays the awarding body a royalty expressed as a percentage of the publisher's net sales of the endorsed resource. The royalty is consideration for:

1. The independent review of the resource
2. The right to display the "Endorsed for Pearson Edexcel Qualifications" logo
3. The catalogue listing and co-marketing that follows
4. The awarding body's ongoing willingness to put its name to the resource

It is **not** a share of Pearson's assessment fee revenue. It is **not** a licence fee for the specification (which is free). It is strictly a royalty on sales of the endorsed resource itself.

## 2. Typical royalty bands in UK textbook publishing

For published textbook endorsement relationships in the UK, common royalty bands are:

| Band | Rate on net | Typical scenario |
|---|---|---|
| Low | 5% | Mature publisher, high volume, physical textbook at discounted educational pricing |
| Mid | 7–10% | Standard endorsed student book, moderate volume |
| High | 10–15% | Premium or niche resource, digital product, low volume with high per-unit margin |

Edge cases:

- Fixed per-title fee instead of a royalty (rare, but used where volume is negligible)
- Hybrid: upfront review fee + lower running royalty
- Tiered royalty with a step-up at a volume threshold
- Minimum annual royalty to protect the awarding body from dormant licence

**Critical:** These bands are indicative. Pearson may propose something different. Our position in the first call is to listen before we counter.

The board should decide which scenario is a walk-away point. Our internal working view is that anything above mid (10%) for a digital subscription product should be pushed back against, on the basis that digital distribution has different margin characteristics from a printed textbook.

### 3.3 Attribution problem — how do we define "endorsed revenue"?

This is the single trickiest question in the negotiation. Our subscription product covers the whole of GCSE / IGCSE English. Pearson endorsement covers only the specifications we apply for. Three possible approaches:

| Approach | Description | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Per-specification share** | Split Hub revenue by the number of specifications supported, and apply royalty to the Edexcel share | Fair, defensible, proportional | Complex reporting; definition of a "specification" is fuzzy |
| **Opt-in usage** | Track which users actively use Edexcel content and attribute revenue by usage | Most accurate | Operationally heavy; requires rich analytics; may require Pearson data-sharing agreement |
| **Standalone endorsed product** | Sell a separate "Pearson Edexcel English" tier of the Hub, royalty on that tier only | Simple, clean, unambiguous | Adds product complexity; may reduce overall adoption |

Our preferred approach is **usage-based attribution** with **per-specification share** as a fallback. We will not accept **whole-product royalty** because that would cross-subsidise Pearson endorsement with revenue from AQA or OCR-aligned users. Founder sign-off required on this position.

## 4. Our starting position in negotiation

1. **Preferred structure:** Running royalty only. No upfront fee. No minimum.
2. **Rate anchor:** 7.5% on attributable net revenue.
3. **Attribution:** Usage-based.
4. **Duration:** Aligned to the specification issue period, with automatic renewal unless the specification issue changes.
5. **Termination:** Either party on 6 months' notice.
6. **Reporting:** Quarterly royalty statements, with an annual audit right for Pearson capped at one audit per calendar year and with defined scope.
7. **Co-marketing:** Reciprocal commitments (Pearson lists us, we link to qualifications.pearson.com for authoritative specification text).
8. **Most-favoured-nation:** We ask for a soft MFN so that our rate is no worse than the standard rate Pearson offers other comparable digital publishers at similar scale.

## 5. Walk-away conditions

We will walk away from endorsement rather than agree to:

- A royalty rate above 15% of net
- A whole-product royalty (i.e., royalty on revenue that is not attributable to Edexcel-endorsed content)
- A minimum annual royalty so high that it forces a marketing-led launch we cannot afford
- Exclusivity clauses preventing us from pursuing endorsement with another UK awarding body
- Contract terms that require us to assign any IP in our content to Pearson
- Audit rights that are uncapped or that require disclosure of user-level personal data
- Indemnity clauses that leave us exposed to Pearson's own trademark defence costs
- A term that ties the endorsement to a specification issue that is about to be superseded
