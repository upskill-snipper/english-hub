# IP Considerations — Using AQA Specification References

> Internal strategy document. Not legal advice. Counsel review required before relying on anything here.
> Purpose: make sure we understand what we can and cannot legally do with AQA's specifications, past papers, mark schemes, and other published materials — both before and after we achieve approved status.

## 1. Landscape: what AQA publishes and who owns what

AQA publishes a lot of material that is relevant to us. The IP status of each category is different and must be understood separately.

| Material | Typical IP status | Our right to use |
|---|---|---|
| Specification document (8700, 8702) | Copyright AQA; typically free to read and reference | Can reference, cannot reproduce wholesale |
| Specification at a glance page | Copyright AQA | Can reference, not reproduce |
| Assessment Objectives | Set by Ofqual; same wording across boards for some AOs | Reference; factual use |
| Past papers | Copyright AQA | Limited reuse under copyright fair dealing; normally need permission for systematic use |
| Mark schemes | Copyright AQA | Similar to past papers |
| Examiner reports | Copyright AQA | Can summarise and quote short extracts; cannot reproduce wholesale |
| Set-text anthology content (poems) | Copyright to the individual poets and estates (not AQA) | Must clear permissions from the rights holder |
| AQA logo, badge, trademark | AQA trademarks | No use without licence |
| AQA's own examiner training materials | Copyright AQA, often confidential | No public use |

## 2. The two most important rules

### Rule 1: The AQA specification is referenceable but not reproducible.
We can say "Paper 1 Section A of the 8702 specification requires students to respond to a Shakespeare extract and the whole play." We cannot copy-paste long stretches of AQA's own wording verbatim and present it as ours. Where we need AQA's exact wording (e.g. quoting an AO), we quote it briefly, in quotation marks, and attribute it.

### Rule 2: Set-text content is usually not AQA's to license to us.
The poems in the AQA poetry anthology and the extracts on Language papers are typically licensed to AQA by the individual rights holders for the purpose of the qualification. That licence does **not** typically flow to publishers. If we want to reproduce a poem, we need a separate licence from the rights holder (or their estate), or we need to rely on fair dealing for criticism and review, which is narrower than people assume.

## 3. Fair dealing, not fair use

The UK has a *fair dealing* regime, not the US's fair use. Fair dealing for criticism, review, or quotation is the closest thing to an exemption we can rely on, and it is narrower:

- Quotations must be **no longer than necessary** for the criticism.
- Use must be **fair** (proportionate, not substitutive of the original).
- There must be **sufficient acknowledgement** (source and author).
- The work must have been **made available to the public**.

In practice this means:
- Quoting short extracts from poems for line-by-line analysis: usually OK, with acknowledgement.
- Reproducing whole poems alongside analysis: often **not** OK without a licence.
- Quoting short extracts from set-text prose for analysis: usually OK with acknowledgement.
- Reproducing entire chapters: not OK.
- Writing original analytical commentary on a text, quoting sparingly: OK.

Our editorial policy must err on the side of caution. Default: don't reproduce; paraphrase and quote the minimum needed for the point.

## 4. Past papers and mark schemes

AQA publishes past papers and mark schemes on its website. That does not make them free to use systematically.

- **Reference is OK.** "Question 2 on AQA's June 2022 8702 Paper 1 asked candidates to explore how Shakespeare presents…" is fine.
- **Linking to AQA's hosted PDF is OK.** We can link users to the authoritative AQA copy.
- **Selective quotation with attribution is OK.** "AQA's 2022 mark scheme describes a Level 5 response as…" with quotation marks and source.
- **Reproducing whole past-paper questions in our content is risky** and should be avoided as a default.
- **Systematic reproduction of past papers** (e.g. a library of past papers embedded in our platform) **requires AQA's permission** in most cases. Some educational uses may be permitted for schools under specific licences, but a commercial digital platform cannot assume it has these rights.

**Action:** before we embed any past-paper content in our platform, get explicit permission from AQA or base the content on original, AQA-style questions we have written ourselves.

## 5. Assessment Objectives

Assessment Objectives for GCSE English are set by Ofqual and are identical across boards for the common AOs. Reproducing the wording of an AO is low-risk because the exact wording is effectively a regulatory statement. We still attribute it and keep quotation short.

## 6. Examiner reports

Examiner reports are a valuable teaching resource: they tell students what real examiners actually saw in candidate responses. Use:

- **Short quoted extracts with attribution**: OK and valuable.
- **Summaries in our own words**: OK and the default approach.
- **Wholesale reproduction**: not OK.

## 7. The AQA anthology

This is where publishers get into trouble.

The poems in the AQA poetry anthology are copyrighted to the individual poets or their estates. AQA has licensed those poems for use in the qualification itself. A publisher who reproduces a whole poem from the anthology in a revision resource without a separate licence from the rights holder is almost certainly infringing.

Our policy must be:

- **Do not reproduce whole anthology poems** on the platform unless we have cleared the rights directly with the poet or estate, or the poem is in the public domain (many 19th-century Romantic and pre-20th-century poems are, but always check).
- **Quote short, specific lines** for analysis with attribution.
- **Build teaching around excerpts, not around reproduced whole texts.**
- **Maintain a rights register** of any anthology poem we have cleared.

## 8. Third-party content we include

Beyond AQA material, we include:
- Images (historical context, author portraits, manuscripts)
- Video (contextual explainers)
- Audio (dramatic readings)
- Third-party critical essays

Every third-party item must have:
- Identified rights holder
- Licence or permission on file
- Attribution as required by the licence
- Renewal tracking if time-limited

## 10. What AQA will ask us about IP during the approval process

Expect AQA's approval process to look for:

- Evidence we have rights to reproduce the content we claim we can
- Evidence we have not reproduced AQA materials without permission
- Evidence we have not reproduced anthology poems without licensing
- Evidence we have not used the AQA logo, badge, or trademark impermissibly
- Evidence our contributor agreements assign content ownership to us
- Evidence we have an errata process for removing content if an IP issue is discovered

## 11. Pre-submission IP audit

Before submitting the application pack we run a full IP audit:

- [ ] Every image on the platform has a documented source and licence
- [ ] Every audio/video asset has a documented source and licence
- [ ] Every quoted literary text is within fair dealing or licensed
- [ ] Every past-paper reference is properly attributed
- [ ] Every AQA quotation is short, in quotation marks, and attributed
- [ ] No AQA logo or badge appears anywhere on the platform
- [ ] No AQA trademark is used impermissibly
- [ ] Contributor IP agreements are on file for all contributors whose work is in the sample pack

## 12. Ongoing

IP is a discipline, not a single review. Editorial workflow (see `../03-evidence/editorial-process.md`) includes an IP check at publication and an annual audit.
