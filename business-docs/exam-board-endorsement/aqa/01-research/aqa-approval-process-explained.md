## What AQA says, in AQA's own words

AQA's published position, summarised (not quoted at length, to respect copyright):

- AQA selects publishing partners **carefully** to make sure there are high-quality resources that support their specifications.
- Using an "AQA approved" textbook means AQA's subject experts have checked that the resource provides good support for the specification.
- Textbooks without the AQA approved badge have **not** been through the process and do not carry the same reassurance, even if the AQA name is used in the title.
- AQA only approves **print and digital textbooks**.
- Purchase of approved books is **not essential** to deliver AQA specifications — teachers can use any resource they like. Approval is a trust signal, not a compulsion.

## The four stages

AQA refers to a "detailed 4-stage approval process" carried out by subject experts. AQA has not published a stage-by-stage breakdown in public, but based on peer reporting from other publishers and standard exam-board practice, the stages are understood to work roughly like this:

### Stage 1 — Proposal and eligibility check
- Publisher submits a formal proposal describing the resource, pedagogy, author team, and commercial plan.
- AQA confirms the resource is eligible (print or digital textbook), and that the proposal meets selection criteria.
- AQA signs a working agreement / confidentiality terms so the publisher can see specification drafts.

### Stage 2 — Content review against the specification
- AQA's subject experts review the content against every element of the specification: subject content, assessment objectives, sample assessments, command words, and mark schemes.
- AQA produces detailed written feedback.
- Publisher revises and resubmits.

### Stage 3 — Pedagogical and accuracy review
- Deeper review of pedagogy, accessibility, progression, and factual accuracy.
- AQA checks the resource does not misrepresent the specification, make guarantees about exam performance, or contain errors.
- Further feedback and revisions.

### Stage 4 — Final sign-off
- Final editorial sign-off.
- Commercial terms agreed (royalty, badge usage, fee schedule).
- Trademark / badge licence signed.
- Resource is added to AQA's approved list.

**AQA is explicit: approval is not automatic.** Publishers may be asked to repeat a stage if their resource has not yet met the required standard.

## Who decides

- **Subject experts** within AQA's English team — typically senior examiners, chief examiners, and specification authors.
- Final sign-off sits with AQA's **standards and approvals function**, which protects the integrity of the badge.
- Commercial and trademark terms are agreed with AQA's **partnerships / publishing team**.

## How publishers get in the door

This is the most misunderstood part of AQA's process.

**AQA does not operate an open application portal.** The normal route is:

1. AQA begins work on a new or revised specification.
2. AQA contacts publishers they already consider credible partners and invites them to enter the approval process.
3. Interested publishers submit a proposal against AQA's selection criteria.
4. AQA selects **2–3 publishers per subject** to actually work through the 4-stage process.

This has huge implications for The English Hub:

- Step 1 of our plan is **not "send the application"** — there is no portal to send it to.
- Step 1 is **"get on AQA's partnerships radar as a credible, serious candidate"** so that when the next 8700/8702 specification refresh begins, The English Hub is on the invite list.
- Everything in this pack is designed to support that positioning conversation, not a formal filing.

## What is assessed at each stage

Based on AQA's public guidance, peer reports, and standard exam-board approval practice, the following is assessed:

- **Specification coverage.** Does the resource cover every element of the 8700 / 8702 specification? Nothing missing, nothing out of scope.
- **Assessment objective alignment.** Does the resource explicitly teach and practise each Assessment Objective (AO1–AO4 for literature; AO1–AO9 for language including spoken endorsement)?
- **Accuracy.** Factual, critical, and textual accuracy — particularly on set texts and mark scheme wording.
- **Pedagogy.** Does the resource build skills progressively, scaffold correctly, and avoid "teach to the test" reductionism?
- **Tone and style.** Does the resource present the specification accurately, without implying AQA endorsement of claims AQA hasn't actually made?
- **Exam technique integrity.** Does the resource avoid "guaranteed grade" style claims?
- **Conflict of interest.** AQA has a published conflict-of-interest policy for authors who are also examiners. Any authoring examiners must be declared and managed.
- **Safeguarding and accessibility.** Resources used with minors must meet safeguarding and accessibility standards.
- **Commercial integrity.** Publisher must agree to badge-use rules and not imply broader AQA endorsement than has been granted.

## How long it takes

AQA has not published an official timeline. Based on peer reporting and plan-for-the-worst principles:

- **Best case:** 6 months from invitation to badge, if a specification refresh is underway and the resource needs only minor revisions.
- **Typical case:** 9–12 months.
- **Difficult case:** 12–18 months if a stage has to be repeated.

Schools buy on an academic-year cycle, so we should plan for the badge to be usable at the start of an academic year (September) rather than mid-cycle.

## AQA's conflict-of-interest policy for authors

AQA publishes an authoring conflict-of-interest policy (version 3.3 as of the last public capture). The policy governs what an AQA examiner can and cannot do as an author for third-party publishers. Key principles we need to internalise:

- Examiners cannot use non-public specification information in third-party resources.
- Examiners must declare authoring work to AQA.
- AQA can require authoring work to stop or be restructured if it creates a conflict.
- There is a cooling-off period for chief examiners who become authors [TBC exact length].

If we list examiner contributors on our team, we **must** confirm each one has declared their work to AQA and is within the policy.

## Strategic takeaways

1. **There is no application form to send.** Our first move is a positioning conversation with AQA partnerships.
2. **Timing matters.** The window opens when AQA begins a specification refresh. We need to know their cycle.
3. **The examiner bench is our single strongest credibility lever.** Anyone on our team with current or recent AQA English examining experience is valuable.
4. **Eligibility is a real risk.** If AQA interprets "digital textbook" narrowly, a pure web-platform may fail the eligibility gate. We should plan for a print/PDF companion artefact that clearly qualifies.
5. **Budget 9–12 months and £50k–£120k.** Anything faster or cheaper is optimism, not planning.
