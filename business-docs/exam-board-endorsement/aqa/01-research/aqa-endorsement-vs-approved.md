# AQA Approved vs. AQA Endorsed vs. "Aligned to AQA"

> Research document — internal use only.
> Why this matters: the wrong terminology on our marketing or our application is a trademark and regulatory risk. This doc pins down what each phrase actually means.

## TL;DR

| Phrase | Who grants it | What it means | Can we say it today? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **AQA approved** | AQA (after 4-stage process) | AQA subject experts have checked the resource. Comes with a badge and a trademark licence. | **No.** Only after approval. |
| **AQA endorsed resource** | Historically used by some boards; AQA's current public voice uses "approved" | Often used as a looser synonym in press; legally indistinguishable in AQA's world from "approved" and requires the same process | **No.** Only after approval. |
| **Aligned to AQA** | Self-claim | The resource was written with the AQA specification in mind and the publisher believes it matches. No AQA warranty. Must not use the AQA badge or logo. | **Yes**, carefully. |
| **AQA specification reference** | Self-claim | The resource names the AQA specification (e.g. "8702") for navigation. No claim of endorsement. | **Yes**, carefully. |

## 1. "AQA approved" — the badge

This is the gold-standard designation. It means:

- The resource has been through AQA's full four-stage approval process.
- AQA subject experts have signed off that it genuinely supports the specification.
- The publisher has agreed badge-usage terms and signed AQA's trademark licence.
- The resource appears in AQA's published approved-resources catalogue.

When AQA talks about publishers on its public site, "approved" is the word it uses. The green tick badge that appears on textbook covers — "AQA Approved" — is the visible expression of this.

**What it legally allows:**
- Using the AQA approved badge and logo on covers and marketing, subject to AQA's badge-use rules.
- Claiming in marketing that the resource is "AQA approved."
- Being promoted by AQA through its own channels (catalogue, CPD events, newsletters).

**What it legally does not allow:**
- Claiming AQA wrote, guarantees, or recommends the resource.
- Claiming grade guarantees, pass guarantees, or tiered outcome predictions.
- Using the badge on content that was not part of the approved scope.

## 2. "AQA endorsed resource"

This phrase appears in the wider exam-prep industry and is sometimes used interchangeably with "approved." Two things to know:

- **AQA's own current language is "approved," not "endorsed."** When AQA speaks about its publisher relationships, its public site uses "approved publisher," "approved textbook," and "AQA approved." The word "endorsement" does appear in AQA's GCSE English Language context, but it refers to the **Spoken Language Endorsement** — a candidate-level qualification endorsement, not a publisher endorsement. Confusing the two in correspondence with AQA signals that we don't understand their house language and is an immediate credibility hit.

- **Other boards (notably Pearson/Edexcel) do use the word "endorsed."** Pearson runs a formal endorsed-resources scheme. So in industry shorthand, "endorsed" and "approved" are often treated as synonyms, but when talking to AQA specifically, we should use **"approved."**

**Action for the team:** search and replace "AQA endorsed" with "AQA approved" in any AQA-facing document. The phrase "AQA endorsed" is fine in internal strategy docs and in general marketing where we're talking about the category ("we're pursuing exam-board endorsement"), but it is incorrect in AQA-facing copy.

## 3. "Aligned to AQA"

This is what any publisher can say without AQA's permission, as long as it's true. "Aligned to AQA" means:

- The resource was written with the AQA specification in mind.
- The publisher has mapped the content to the specification.
- The publisher believes (but AQA has not certified) that the resource supports the specification.

**What it legally allows:**
- Stating that the resource is "aligned to the AQA GCSE English Literature (8702) specification."
- Referencing assessment objectives and paper structure.
- Using the specification code (e.g. "8702") for navigation.

**What it legally does not allow:**
- Using the AQA badge, logo, or trademark beyond factual reference.
- Implying that AQA has reviewed, approved, or endorsed the resource.
- Using the word "approved" or "endorsed" in proximity to "AQA" in a way a reasonable reader would interpret as AQA's own warranty.
- Using the AQA name in a domain name, product name, or title in a way that implies endorsement.

**The risk:** aggressive "aligned" marketing can become confusable with "approved" marketing, and AQA actively monitors this. If AQA believes a publisher is implying approval it hasn't earned, AQA can issue a cease-and-desist and (critically) damage the relationship before we even get into the approval process. So we need to be careful here.

## 4. "AQA" in titles and SEO

We currently rank for — or want to rank for — terms like:

- "AQA GCSE English Literature"
- "AQA 8700"
- "AQA poetry anthology revision"

This is factual reference to the specification, not a claim of endorsement, and is legitimate. But:

- We must not use the AQA logo or green-tick badge.
- We must not use the word "approved" or "official" anywhere near AQA in our copy until we actually are.
- We must not use a tagline or metadata that reads as AQA's voice (e.g. "AQA's recommended platform").
- We must not use the AQA trademark in a way that implies partnership.
- On schools-facing marketing, we should lead with "aligned to AQA" rather than hinting at more.

## 5. What this means for our application

When we write our application (see `02-application/`), we must:

1. Use **"approved"** not "endorsed" whenever we refer to the outcome we're seeking from AQA.
2. Describe our current status as **"aligned to AQA specifications"** — never "endorsed" or "approved."
3. Describe our goal as seeking to become an **"AQA approved digital resource"** (or "AQA approved textbook" if we are taking a print+digital bundle route).
4. Cite the Spoken Language Endorsement (SLE) in its proper place — as a feature of the 8700 qualification, not a publisher status.
