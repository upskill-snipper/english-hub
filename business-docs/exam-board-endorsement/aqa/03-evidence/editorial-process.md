# Editorial Process — The English Hub

> Draft for inclusion in AQA application pack.
> Purpose: show AQA that our content is written, reviewed, and quality-assured with the rigour of a serious educational publisher.

## 1. Principles

1. **Every piece of content has a named author and a named reviewer.** No orphan content.
2. **No single person signs off their own work.** Writer and reviewer are different people.
3. **Subject expertise is non-negotiable.** Reviewers must be qualified to judge the content.
4. **Specification alignment is checked explicitly, not assumed.** Every unit is mapped to specification sections and AOs at sign-off.
5. **Changes are logged.** Every edit to live content is versioned and attributable.
6. **Errors are corrected quickly and transparently.** An erratum is not a crisis; it is a process.
7. **Style is a decision.** We have a house style guide and we use it.

## 2. Roles in the editorial workflow

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Commissioning editor** | Decides what content is needed, briefs writers, signs off on scope |
| **Writer** | Produces a first draft against the brief |
| **Subject reviewer** | An English teacher or examiner who reviews for accuracy, pedagogy, and specification alignment |
| **Copy editor** | Reviews for clarity, style, consistency, and accessibility |
| **Editorial lead** | Final sign-off before publication |
| **Safeguarding reviewer** | Reviews any content touching sensitive themes |
| **Accessibility reviewer** | Spot-checks for WCAG compliance and inclusive pedagogy |

No one person holds more than two of these roles on the same piece.

## 3. Workflow stages

### Stage A — Commission

- Commissioning editor identifies the content need (new unit, specification update, errata response).
- Writes a brief including: spec section(s) covered, AOs addressed, target audience (student / teacher / parent), target grade levels, word count, pedagogical approach, deadlines.
- Brief is logged in the editorial system with a unique ID.
- Writer is assigned based on subject expertise and capacity.

### Stage B — Draft

- Writer produces a draft in the platform's authoring environment.
- Draft is tagged against specification sections and AOs in the CMS metadata.
- Writer self-checks against the brief and against the house style guide.
- Writer flags any research questions or source uncertainties.

### Stage C — Subject review

- A subject reviewer (different person from the writer) reads the draft against:
  - The AQA specification wording for the relevant section
  - The AOs the content claims to address
  - AQA examiner reports where relevant
  - Our pedagogy principles
  - Factual accuracy
- Reviewer records comments and a verdict: **Accept / Revise / Reject**.
- Revisions loop between writer and reviewer until both agree.

### Stage D — Copy edit

- Copy editor reviews for clarity, consistency, house style, and accessibility.
- Copy editor does not make substantive subject changes.
- Any substantive issue is escalated back to the writer or subject reviewer.

### Stage E — Safeguarding check (if applicable)

- Any content that touches sensitive themes (violence, self-harm, racialised language in historical texts, mental health, bereavement) is reviewed by the safeguarding lead.
- Safeguarding lead may require framing, warnings, or excluding content.

### Stage F — Accessibility check

- Content is run against the accessibility checklist: heading structure, alt text, colour contrast, link text, reading level, captions.

### Stage G — Final sign-off

- Editorial lead reviews the final draft and sign-off notes from all reviewers.
- Editorial lead publishes the content with a version stamp and author attribution.
- Content enters the live platform.

### Stage H — Post-publication

- Content is added to the annual review schedule.
- Errata reports are monitored and triaged within [PLACEHOLDER — target response window].
- Specification updates trigger a review of all affected content.

## 4. Version control and audit trail

- Every piece of content has a version number and a last-updated timestamp.
- Every substantive change is logged with: what changed, who changed it, why, and when.
- Change history is retained for at least [PLACEHOLDER — 5 years] so that any past state can be reconstructed.
- Authoring and reviewing attributions are preserved across revisions.

## 5. Content types and tailored workflows

Not every piece of content goes through every stage. Tailored workflows:

| Content type | Workflow |
|---|---|
| New set-text study unit | Full workflow A–H |
| Revision of existing unit | B–H (commission implicit) |
| New quiz set | A–C–D–G |
| Single quiz question correction (erratum) | B–C–G (fast-track) |
| Teacher-facing scheme of work | A–B–C–G |
| Student guidance article | A–B–C–D–G |
| Platform-level explainer (non-subject) | A–B–D–F–G |

## 7. Metrics we track

- Time from commission to publication (median and p90)
- Time from erratum report to correction (median and p90)
- Review pass rate on first submission
- Reviewer comments per unit (proxy for thoroughness)
- Post-publication edit frequency
- Age of oldest un-reviewed unit (target: less than 12 months)

## 8. Annual editorial audit

Once a year we run a full editorial audit:

- Every set-text unit is re-read against the current live AQA specification
- Every poem study is re-read against the current anthology version
- Every language paper sequence is checked against the most recent AQA examiner report
- AOs and spec mapping are re-verified
- Errata are reviewed for recurring patterns
- Author/reviewer attribution is audited for accuracy

Audit findings are logged and drive the next editorial calendar.
