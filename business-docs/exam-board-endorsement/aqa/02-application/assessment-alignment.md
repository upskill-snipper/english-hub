## 1. Why this document matters

Any publisher can claim their content is "AQA aligned." What AQA actually tests in the approval process is whether the assessment materials inside the resource (a) use AQA's command words correctly, (b) credit the correct AOs at the correct weights, and (c) model answers that would earn the marks a real AQA examiner would give.

This document sets out — honestly — where The English Hub's assessment practice already matches AQA's style, where it is close but not yet matched, and where remediation work is needed.

## 4. Mark scheme shape alignment

AQA mark schemes for extended-response questions operate in **levels** (Level 1 through Level 5 or 6, depending on the question), with descriptors for each level and indicative content. They are not grid-score rubrics and they do not mark sentence-by-sentence.

The English Hub's self-assessment rubrics:

- [ ] Use AQA's level structure, not an arbitrary A–E or 1–10 grid
- [ ] Use AQA's descriptor vocabulary (e.g. "perceptive", "analytical", "clear", "simple")
- [ ] Explicitly map to AOs, not to a single "quality" number
- [ ] Include indicative content that resembles AQA's published indicative content
- [ ] Credit meaning and argument, not just number of quotations or techniques spotted

## 5. Model answers at grade levels

AQA publishes exemplar answers at different levels. Our model answers should:

- Cover Grade 4, Grade 6, and Grade 8 equivalents explicitly
- Use commentary language that mirrors AQA examiner reports
- Show the *difference* between levels, not just a "perfect" answer
- Avoid implying any formulaic approach that "guarantees" a grade

## 6. Assessment frequency and integrity

- Formative assessment is scattered throughout teaching content (low-stakes quizzes, comprehension checks)
- Summative practice uses full AQA-style questions with mark schemes
- We do not predict grades from quiz performance in ways that could mislead students or parents
- We do not sell "guaranteed Grade 9" content — see `03-evidence/accuracy-commitment.md`

## 7. What AQA will want to see at Stage 2

When AQA's subject experts review our assessment content at Stage 2 of the approval process, they will almost certainly:

1. Pick three or four of our questions at random and check that command words and AO weightings are correct.
2. Read our model answers against the live mark scheme for an equivalent real AQA question.
3. Read our rubrics and ask whether an examiner could use them to mark a real student response.
4. Check we are not implying that a student who uses our platform is guaranteed an outcome.
5. Check we are not reproducing copyrighted mark scheme content verbatim from AQA's materials.
6. Check we are not reproducing past-paper questions in a way that breaches AQA's reuse terms.

## 8. Red flags we must eliminate

Before submission, audit for and remove:

- [ ] Any question that misuses an AQA command word
- [ ] Any mark scheme that credits the wrong AO
- [ ] Any rubric that grades on "number of quotes" rather than analytical depth
- [ ] Any model answer that reproduces a past-paper question verbatim without attribution
- [ ] Any tip or trick that contradicts an AQA examiner report
- [ ] Any implied grade guarantee
- [ ] Any content that misrepresents the structure of an AQA paper
- [ ] Any practice question that uses a grading scale that is not AQA's

## 9. Submission artefact

For the Stage 2 content review, we will pre-package:

1. **Assessment alignment pack (this document)** — the claim
2. **A sample of 10 practice questions across both qualifications, with rubrics and model answers** — the evidence
3. **A marked student response** — one real, anonymised student attempt we have marked, with commentary explaining the level we awarded and why
4. **A comparison exhibit** — for each of the 10 questions, the equivalent real AQA question from a published past paper (with proper attribution), so AQA can see our questions side-by-side with the real thing
