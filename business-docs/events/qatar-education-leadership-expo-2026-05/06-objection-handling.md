# Objection handling — the 8 you will hear

Each entry: what they actually said, what they actually mean, your one-line response that opens the door rather than closing it.

---

## Objection 1 — "We already use [Magic School AI / Century Tech / Atom / Quillbot / Snapask]."

**What they're testing:** are you going to over-claim that you're a replacement.

**Your response:**
> "Good — you've already done the AI-literacy work, which means we don't have to start from scratch. We're not trying to replace [tool] generally; we're built specifically for GCSE and IGCSE English Language and Literature mark schemes. So a fair question is whether what we add on top of [tool] is worth £4,000/year. Easiest way to find out is the pilot — we'll set up a side-by-side for one of your Y11 classes and you can see whether the AO-specific feedback moves the needle on marking turnaround. If it doesn't, you don't renew."

**Why this works:** you've validated their existing investment, reframed the question (incremental value vs replacement), and offered a low-risk way to test the answer.

---

## Objection 2 — "AI isn't accurate enough to mark essays."

**What they're testing:** whether you'll over-claim accuracy.

**Your response:**
> "You're right to push on that — and that's exactly why our AI marking is positioned as **formative, not summative**. It's never the grade that goes on a report. What it gives the student is fast, AO-referenced feedback they can act on between human marking cycles. The teacher stays the final word on anything that counts. Where the AI is genuinely strong is volume — the student can practise five essays in a week and get specific feedback on all five, which no English department in the world can sustain by hand. Where it's weaker is the things teachers are good at — nuance, encouragement, context about the individual student. So we draw the line at: AI for volume practice feedback, teachers for everything that matters."

**Why this works:** concedes the limitation up-front, redirects to where AI genuinely is good, positions the teacher as the higher authority (which they want to hear).

## Objection 3 — "We're worried about Qatar data protection — PDPPL."

**What they're testing:** whether you've actually done the homework or are just claiming compliance.

**Your response:**
> "Good — most EdTech in the GCC hasn't done the PDPPL homework, so it's the right question. Three concrete things: one, we shipped the Article 17 cross-border consent banner into the signup flow last week, so Qatar-resident users see a named-destination consent panel before account creation. Two, we hold three v1.0 final DPIAs including the one specific to processing minors' data — happy to send those to your DPO. Three, the Article 16 permit application dossier for processing children's data is drafted and shelf-ready; we're holding submission until a Qatari school commits, because the legal-opinion engagement that goes with it is real money and we want to time it with revenue. The substantive compliance posture — consent, DPIAs, EU primary database, zero AI-training contractually — is in place regardless of whether the formal permit is filed. I've got a one-pager I can hand you that summarises all of this."

Hand them the compliance one-pager (file 05).

**Why this works:** gives three concrete artefacts, is honest about what's shelf-ready vs filed, doesn't over-claim.

## Objection 4 — "How much does it cost?"

**What they're testing:** whether you'll pretend it's cheap when it isn't.

**Your response:**
> "Pilot is £3,000 for a single year group, £4,000 for the whole English department — one term, defined success criteria. Annual deployment at the Founding rate is £4,000 per school per year for whole-school access. That doubles to £8,000 in August when we close the Founding cohort. So if your school can decide before August, you lock the £4,000 rate for the life of the licence. If you wait, you're at £8,000 from the start."

**Why this works:** direct answer with the urgency built in (without faking it). The "lock the rate for life" framing is a real benefit, not a synthetic urgency tactic.

## Objection 5 — "Are you endorsed by [Pearson / Cambridge / AQA / etc.]?"

**What they're testing:** whether you'll lie.

**Your response:**
> "No, and I want to be straight with you about that — we **cover** all six exam boards' specifications end-to-end, but no exam board has endorsed us. None has endorsed any AI marking platform that I'm aware of; the boards are deliberately cautious about that. What we do is map every mark scheme into the AI's prompt so the feedback references the actual AOs the student is sitting. If endorsement is a hard requirement for your school I should tell you up-front rather than waste your time."

**Why this works:** absolute honesty earns trust faster than any pitch trick. Most schools don't require board endorsement, but they do require you to not claim one you don't have.

## Objection 6 — "We have native English-speaking teachers — why would we need an AI?"

**What they're testing:** whether you understand the actual workload reality.

**Your response:**
> "I'm not asking you to replace your teachers. I'm asking you whether your English department has time to give every Year 11 student paragraph-level feedback on every fortnightly practice essay. Most departments I've spoken to have a real marking turnaround of 7–14 days, and when feedback lands it's necessarily light because no one has 90 minutes per essay. What we do is mark the practice essays — the ones that don't go on a report card — in under a minute, so the student gets immediate AO-referenced feedback they can act on. Your teachers' time is freed for the marking that genuinely needs human judgement: coursework, controlled assessment, the summative stuff. Honestly, if your department isn't running 7+ days behind on marking, you probably don't need us."

**Why this works:** acknowledges the teachers' value, surfaces the real operational pain, gives them an honest "you might not need us" reframe.

## Objection 7 — "We need to take this to our procurement / IT / governance / board."

**What they're testing:** whether you're going to push them past their actual procurement process.

**Your response:**
> "Of course. The cleanest way to make that easy for you is for us to give you the full procurement pack up-front so you're not chasing me for documents — I'll send a PDMS index, the RoPA, the three DPIAs, the sub-processor register, and the pilot terms in one email. Your IT and DPO can review in parallel with the academic conversation. If you can give me the email addresses, I'll cc the right people directly so nothing gets bottlenecked at one person. Who do I need to send the technical pack to, and who do I need to send the academic pack to?"

**Why this works:** you've recognised their process, given them the artefacts they'll need, and surfaced the actual stakeholders in their organisation.

---

## Objection 8 — "We have UK [British school chain] head office; we can't decide locally."

**What they're testing:** whether to dismiss you as not worth their time.

**Your response:**
> "Understood. Group-level decisions are exactly what our multi-campus pilot scope is built for — custom pricing, two-campus proof-of-concept escalating to group rollout. If you can introduce me to your group's COO or Director of Education, I can have a proposal in front of them within a week. Or, if your school can run a 'shadow pilot' off-cycle without group sign-off — say, your Y11 cohort only, for one term — we can get evidence of value before the group conversation. Both paths work. Which is easier for you?"

**Why this works:** you've offered two concrete paths instead of accepting "no" — one institutional (slow, big), one tactical (fast, small). The shadow-pilot path is the one most international school HoDs will recognise as how things actually get done in practice.

## Universal "I want to leave gracefully" objection

**They say:** "Let me think about it / send me an email."

**You say:**
> "Sure. Quickest way for me not to lose you is to send my follow-up to your email tonight while I still remember the conversation. Can I get the right address — and is there a one-line reminder of what we discussed that would be useful in the subject line? Something like 'Y11 IGCSE pilot' or 'PDPPL diligence' or 'thinking about it'?"

**Why this works:** soft commit to email capture, makes the follow-up about *their* useful subject line not yours, and lets them say "thinking about it" if that's all they've got — which is fine. They've still given you the email, which is the only thing you actually needed.

---

## What to do if you're stumped

If a question lands that you genuinely don't know the answer to:

> "Honest answer: I don't know. Let me find out tonight and email you tomorrow with the answer. What's your email?"

Then **do it**. Within 24 hours. This is how trust is built in launch-stage selling. Founders who say "I'll get back to you" and don't are remembered as exactly that.
