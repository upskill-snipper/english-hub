# 30-minute discovery call script

The call that turns a follow-up email into a signed pilot. Structure: 4 minutes warm-up, 12 minutes their pain, 8 minutes our fit, 5 minutes specific next step, 1 minute close.

**Before the call:** open the lead-capture record for this contact. Re-read the quotes you wrote down at the booth. Have the leave-behind, the pilot offer, and the compliance one-pager open in tabs.

**Tools:** Calendly booking, video by Google Meet or Zoom, share-screen on hand for the live demo.

---

## Pre-call (5 minutes before)

- Confirm the right pronunciation of their name and their school's name
- Open: their school's website, their LinkedIn, the lead-capture sheet, the live demo on the spare screen
- Check: any school news in the past 48 hours (new principal, OFSTED-equivalent inspection, exam-board change) — drop into conversation if relevant
- Have a glass of water; nothing kills credibility on a call faster than a coughing fit at minute 14

---

## Minute 0–4 — warm-up

> "Thanks for making time, [First name]. Before we start — quick check on how you'd like this call to run. Do you have a specific outcome in mind, or is this a 'let me find out more' conversation? Both work, I just want to use your 30 minutes well."

[Listen.]

> "OK. To make sure I don't repeat what you already know — quick context on us in 60 seconds. The English Hub is a UK company; we mark GCSE and IGCSE English essays with AI, against the actual mark schemes for AQA, Edexcel, OCR, Eduqas, WJEC, and Cambridge IGCSE. We've been live since [date], we're at [number] active students. We're admitting 10 founding schools at £4,000 a year, of which [N] places are taken — Qatar is the first city I've actively pitched the cohort, so you've got first-mover position on the remaining seats. Anything you want me to elaborate on before we get into your situation?"

[Listen. If they say "tell me more about X" — give 60 seconds, then redirect.]

> "Great. The most useful thing for me to understand is what your English department's reality looks like right now, so I can tell you honestly whether we're a fit. Mind if I ask a few questions?"

---

## Minute 4–16 — their pain (12 minutes, mostly listening)

Five questions. **Listen for 90 seconds after each one before asking the next.** The temptation to fill silence is the single biggest mistake on discovery calls.

1. **"What's the size of the English department and the year-group mix you'd be thinking about?"**
   — establishes addressable scope.

2. **"What does the marking turnaround actually look like in your department for fortnightly practice essays?"**
   — surfaces the operational pain. Look for: a real number (7–14 days), a sigh, a "we've talked about this in department meetings".

3. **"Of your current Year 11 cohort, what's the single biggest thing standing between your students at grade 6/7 and a grade 8/9 in IGCSE English?"**
   — surfaces what they think they need to fix. Could be content knowledge (we partially help), exam technique (we help a lot), volume of practice (we help fully), feedback quality (we help fully).

4. **"How does AI currently get used in your school for English — if at all? Magic School AI? Century? Anything teacher-driven?"**
   — find out who they're already paying. Important: do NOT bash competitors; ask what's working and what isn't.

5. **"If I sketched out a one-term pilot for your Year 11 IGCSE cohort, who else would naturally need to be in the room before you committed — Deputy Head Academic? DPO? Board?"**
   — surfaces decision-maker map without asking "who decides".

---

## Minute 16–24 — our fit (8 minutes)

Now you've heard them. Don't pitch generically — pitch back what they just told you.

> "OK, based on what you've described — [paraphrase their three biggest pain points back to them in their own words] — here's what I think the shape of a pilot for [School name] would look like:"

Open the pilot offer (file 04) on screen. Walk through:

1. **Scope** — match it to their year-group mix. Default to Year-group pilot (£2,500) unless they signalled they want the whole department.
2. **Success criteria** — name 2–3 that map directly to the pain points they described. ("You said marking turnaround was the headline — let's make 'median turnaround under 1 hour' the first success criterion.")
3. **What you commit to** — 24-hour founder support; bi-weekly platform updates; the compliance pack to your DPO in Week 1.
4. **What they commit to** — one project owner, 60 minutes a week, kickoff + mid-review + close-out calls. **That's it.**
5. **The "if it doesn't work" answer** — "No auto-renew. You don't keep paying. 48-hour exit pack."

Then **live demo — 4 minutes maximum**. Share screen, walk through:
- A Year 11 student submitting an Edexcel IGCSE essay
- The AI marking output appearing in under a minute
- The AO-referenced feedback
- The teacher dashboard view of the same essay
- The parent weekly email preview

**Do NOT** walk through every feature. The demo is to make the product real, not to sell it. If they ask "can it do X?" answer honestly; if X exists, show it; if not, say "not yet, on roadmap, [timeline]."

---

## Minute 24–29 — specific next step (5 minutes)

> "OK — what I'd love to do, if it sounds workable, is send you the full pilot proposal by end of business today. That's a 4-page document with the exact scope, the success criteria as we've discussed them, and the legal terms. If you and your [Deputy Head Academic / Principal / Board] are happy with it, we sign and kick off within a week. Does that work?"

If YES:
> "Great. Three quick things to confirm so I can get the proposal right first time:
> 1. Pilot scope — [Year group / English department / Multi-campus]?
> 2. Project owner on your side — name + email I should address the proposal to?
> 3. Anyone I should cc — your DPO, Deputy Head, anyone else?"

If MAYBE / NOT YET:
> "OK — what would have to be true for this to be a yes? Specifically what's the next decision point?"

[Listen. Then offer a smaller commitment.]

> "Got it. Two options: I send you (a) the pilot proposal so it's on file when you do come back to it, or (b) just the compliance one-pager and DPMS index so your DPO has it for when you need it. Which is more useful?"

If NO:
> "Honest reaction — that's totally fine. Can I ask: was there a specific thing in the conversation that made it a 'not for us', or is it more 'right thing wrong time'? Either answer is helpful to me as a founder."

[Listen. Whatever they say, thank them and offer to stay in touch. They might come back in 6 months.]

---

## Minute 29–30 — close

> "Thanks for the time, [First name]. To recap our next step: [you'll send X by Y, they'll do Z by W]. I'll send a one-line summary by email within the hour confirming exactly what we agreed."

Then SEND THE SUMMARY EMAIL WITHIN THE HOUR. This is the single biggest signal of seriousness you can give. Most founders forget this. Don't.

---

## The summary email (send within 60 minutes of call end)

**Subject:** `Recap — discovery call with [School name], [date]`

> Hi [First name],
>
> Quick recap of what we agreed on the call just now:
>
> 1. **Pilot scope under consideration:** [Year-group pilot / English department / Multi-campus] for your [year group(s)], approximately [N] students.
> 2. **Headline success criterion:** [the one they specifically named, in their words].
> 3. **My next step:** send the full pilot proposal + compliance pack to [their email + cc list] by [time / date].
> 4. **Your next step:** [share with Deputy Head / DPO / project owner] and reply by [date] with a yes/no/let's-discuss.
> 5. **Decision point:** if it's a yes, we sign within a week and kick off [proposed Week 1 date].
>
> If I've got any of that wrong, reply and tell me — I'd rather fix it now than discover the misalignment after I send the proposal.
>
> Thanks again,
> Calum

## Calls that go badly — recovery

**If they get pulled away after 10 minutes:**
> "Totally fine. Three things I'd love to send by email so you've got them when you're ready: pilot offer, compliance one-pager, calendar link to reschedule. Want all three or just the calendar link?"

**If they reveal mid-call that they've already chosen a competitor:**
> "Good — that means you've already done the AI-literacy work, which makes my life easier. Two questions: what specifically are you using them for, and where are the edges of what they do well? If there's no incremental room for us, fine — but I'd rather know that than chase you for six months."

**If they say "your price is too high":**
> "OK — what's the budget you're working with? I'd rather tell you up-front whether we can or can't get to that number than pretend and waste the conversation."

If their budget genuinely can't fit £3,000–£4,000 for a single-term pilot, they are NOT a Founding-cohort target. The cohort price is £4,000/year — schools that can't fit £4,000/year are not your target buyer. Be polite, mention the £29.99/year student subscription as an alternative, end the call gracefully.

**If they say "we'd want to try it free first":**
> "We don't run free pilots for two reasons. One, a free pilot has no commitment on either side, which means it never actually launches inside your school. Two, we're a 4-person company and free pilots burn resources we need to spend on paying customers. The pilot fee — £2,500 for a single year group — is the lowest commitment that makes a pilot real. If that's a dealbreaker, I'd rather know now."

If they push back hard on this, they're not a serious buyer. Polite exit, leave the door open.
