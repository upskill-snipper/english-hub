# Elevator pitches — 15s / 30s / 60s / 2min

Four versions. Don't memorise word for word — memorise the **structure** so you can adapt when the conversation does something unexpected.

The **structure** that runs through all four:
1. **What we do** (one sentence)
2. **Who it's for** (specific, not "students")
3. **What's different** (the one thing they won't have heard at any other booth)
4. **What to do next** (always include a call to action)

---

## 15 seconds — "the polite walk-past"

For the person who slows down at the booth but doesn't fully stop. Goal: get them to stop properly.

> "We mark GCSE and IGCSE English essays with AI in under a minute, against the actual AQA, Edexcel, OCR, and Cambridge mark schemes. Built in the UK, hosted in the EU, and we just shipped the Qatar PDPPL cross-border consent flow last week. Sixty seconds to show you?"

If they stop → go to the 60-second pitch. If they nod and keep walking → "QR code on the banner if you want to look later — and grab a one-pager."

---

## 30 seconds — "you have my attention"

For the person who stops at the table. Goal: qualify them in 30 seconds (which role? which school?) and start the discovery.

> "We're The English Hub. We do AI essay marking for GCSE and IGCSE English — all six UK exam boards plus Cambridge IGCSE. Two minutes to submit a 600-word essay, get a band marked against the assessment objectives, and get specific feedback on what to fix.
>
> We're built in the UK, our primary database is in the EU, and we just shipped the Qatar Article 17 cross-border consent flow into the signup last week — so we're one of the very few EdTech platforms that's actively PDPPL-aware, not just claiming it on a brochure.
>
> Are you a school, or are you here as a parent or student?"

Note the **last sentence** — that's the discovery hook. Don't drop it.

---

## 60 seconds — "you stopped, you're listening"

For someone who is clearly engaged and gave a real role/school answer to the qualifying question. Goal: book a meeting or hand the leave-behind + pilot offer.

> "OK, so for a school like [their school / their role] — say you're running a department of [Year 10 / Year 11 / IGCSE] and you've got, what, 80–120 students writing fortnightly practice essays. That's 80 to 120 essays your English department has to mark each fortnight. Most departments I've spoken to are running 7–10 days behind on marking turnaround, and the feedback when it lands is necessarily light because no one has 90 minutes per essay to give.
>
> What The English Hub does is mark each essay in about a minute, against the exact mark scheme the student is sitting — AQA, Edexcel, OCR, Eduqas, WJEC, Cambridge IGCSE, all six — and gives them paragraph-level feedback referencing the actual AOs. Teachers stay the final word — the AI feedback is **formative**, not a grade that goes anywhere official — but it cuts the wait time from a fortnight to a minute and frees the department's marking time for the essays that genuinely need a human eye.
>
> The compliance posture matters for a Qatar school in particular — we hold three v1.0 DPIAs, the Children's Code high-privacy defaults are enforced in code, and as I said the PDPPL Article 17 consent banner is live in the signup as of last week. We've appointed a separately-named DPO and DSL. Data sits in Frankfurt; AI processing in the US under a zero-retention DPA with Anthropic.
>
> We're opening a Founding Schools cohort — first ten schools in at £4,000 a year, which is roughly what you'd pay one full-time teacher for two days a week of marking. The pilot version of that is £3,000–£4,000 for one term, defined success criteria, no auto-renew. Would it be useful if I sent you the pilot terms after the expo, and we set up a 30-minute call to walk through whether your Year 11 IGCSE cohort would be a good fit?"

End with **email**. If they say yes, get the email written into your notebook AND on the back of their card. Don't trust either alone.

## 2 minutes — "the principal at the Leadership Lounge"

For the moment you're in front of an actual decision-maker and you have their attention for longer than 30 seconds. Goal: leave them with a specific reason to remember you and a specific next step.

> "Thanks for the time. I'll be quick — I know you've got a queue.
>
> Quick context: I'm Calum Johnson, founder of The English Hub. We're a UK company. What we do is AI-assisted essay marking for GCSE and IGCSE English Language and Literature, across all six exam boards — AQA, Edexcel, OCR, Eduqas, WJEC, and Cambridge IGCSE. A student submits a practice essay, the AI marks it against the exact mark scheme in under a minute, and the student gets paragraph-level feedback tied to the assessment objectives. Teachers stay the final authority — what the AI returns is formative practice feedback, never an official grade.
>
> The reason I'm in Doha this weekend is that Qatari British and American schools sit right in our sweet spot — Year 11 and Year 12 students sitting the same IGCSE we already cover end-to-end, English-medium delivery, and the EAL track on the platform handles Gulf Arabic L1 students who are penalised disproportionately for structural transfer errors in conventional marking.
>
> The one thing I want you to leave this conversation knowing is that we have done the PDPPL homework. We just shipped the Article 17 cross-border consent flow into signup last week — Qatar-resident users see a named-destination consent panel before account creation. We hold three v1.0 DPIAs including the one specific to processing minors' data. Our primary database is in the EU. We have a separately-appointed DPO and DSL. The Article 16 permit application dossier for processing children's data is drafted and shelf-ready; we're holding submission until a Qatari school commits, because the legal-opinion engagement that goes with it is a $10–25k spend and we want to time it with revenue.
>
> What I'd ask: if your school does any commercial-cycle review of English-department tools in the next term, could we put a one-term pilot proposal in front of your Head of English and Deputy Head Academic? It's £3,000–£4,000 for one term, defined success criteria, no auto-renew. We're a founding cohort programme — first ten schools at £4,000/year all-in, which doubles in August. I'm happy to send the pilot terms over the same day we get back from the expo if you'll introduce me to your HoD."

Then **stop talking**. Wait. The next thing they say tells you whether to push for a meeting or hand the leave-behind and exit gracefully.

---

## Closing — the line you use to exit any conversation

> "Grab the one-pager on the way past, and the QR code on the banner takes you straight to the schools landing page. If you want to talk further, easiest way is `dpo@theenglishhub.app` — that comes straight to me — or my LinkedIn, Calum Johnson at The English Hub. Thanks for stopping."

The DPO email as the contact address is deliberate: it signals the seriousness of the data-protection conversation **and** it's already monitored. Don't substitute the marketing inbox.

---

## What to NEVER say at the booth

- ❌ "We work with [name a school]" — unless they have signed a written reference agreement; we have none yet.
- ❌ "We're endorsed by [exam board]" — we are not.
- ❌ "We guarantee a grade [X]" — we don't and can't.
- ❌ "We're already used by hundreds of schools" — we are not.
- ❌ "We have NIA certification" — we do not.
- ❌ "Our Article 16 permit has been granted by NCGAA" — it has not been submitted yet.
- ❌ "Cambridge International / Pearson / AQA recommend us" — they do not.
- ❌ "Our AI never gets anything wrong" — it can and does; that's why teachers stay the final authority.
- ❌ "We trained our AI on student essays" — we did not; Anthropic Claude API under zero-retention DPA.

If you slip and use one of these by accident, correct yourself in the moment: "Let me walk that back — I should have said…". Better to look human and correct than to be repeated by a procurement reviewer as "they claimed".
