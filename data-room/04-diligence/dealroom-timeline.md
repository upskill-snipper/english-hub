# Deal Timeline — Typical SaaS Acquisition (LOI → Closing)

**Purpose:** Set expectations — for the founder and the buyer — about how long a SaaS acquisition typically takes. Sub-scale deals can be faster than enterprise M&A, but the core stages still exist and shortcutting them causes deals to fall over later.

**Rule of thumb:** A clean, well-prepared sub-scale SaaS deal runs **10–16 weeks** from first serious conversation to closing. A messy one runs **6+ months** and often doesn't close.

---

## Stage overview

```
Week 0 ─────► Week 16 ─── Typical clean sub-scale deal
│
├── W1-2    Exploratory conversations, NDA, teaser
├── W3-4    Management meeting, initial Q&A, valuation framing
├── W5      Indication of Interest (IOI) → LOI drafting
├── W6      LOI signed (exclusivity begins — typically 60-90 days)
├── W7-10   Formal diligence (financial, legal, tech, commercial)
├── W11-12  SPA drafting, disclosure letter, reps and warranties negotiation
├── W13-14  Final diligence clean-up, third-party consents
├── W15     Signing
└── W16     Closing (condition-precedent clearance, funds flow, completion accounts)
```

---

## Stage 1 — Pre-LOI (Weeks 1–5)

### Week 1–2: First contact

**Buyer does:**
- Receives a teaser (one-pager / deck / intro via intermediary)
- Signs NDA
- Gets investor deck and high-level financials
- First founder-to-buyer call (30–60 min)

**Founder does:**
- Send `nda-before-sharing.md` template
- Provide teaser and deck
- Qualify the buyer: are they serious, funded, realistic on price?

**Red flags at this stage:**
- Buyer asks for detailed customer data before signing NDA
- Buyer says "we don't do NDAs" (never true from a serious buyer)
- Buyer has no clear rationale for the acquisition

### Week 3–4: Management meeting and initial Q&A

**Buyer does:**
- 2–4 hour management meeting, often in person
- Reviews high-level financials, product demo, customer summary
- Begins initial questions from their diligence framework

**Founder does:**
- Deliver management meeting (rehearse once)
- Respond to initial questions — Q&A master handles 90% instantly
- Start a `buyer-q-log.csv` for this buyer
- Introduce 1 reference customer if the buyer is serious

### Week 5: Indication of Interest / LOI drafting

**Buyer does:**
- Sends IOI: headline number, structure, key conditions
- Drafts LOI with exclusivity period (30, 60, or 90 days)

**Founder does:**
- Review IOI honestly: is the number acceptable? is the structure workable?
- Negotiate IOI terms before signing (exclusivity length, walkaway conditions)
- Involve solicitor before signing the LOI

**Key concept — exclusivity:** Once you sign an LOI with exclusivity, you cannot talk to other buyers until either (a) the deal closes, (b) the exclusivity expires, or (c) the buyer walks. This is a heavy commitment. Don't sign exclusivity to a buyer you don't trust.

## Stage 2 — Diligence (Weeks 6–10)

Once the LOI is signed with exclusivity, the buyer opens formal diligence. This is where the pre-prepared data room pays for itself — clean, complete, honest diligence materials shave weeks off this stage and catch issues before they become deal-breakers.

### Week 6–7: Financial and commercial diligence

- Buyer's accountant reviews management accounts, tax position, cap table
- Commercial diligence: customer cohort analysis, churn, concentration, pipeline
- Usually a Quality of Earnings (QoE) report prepared (buyer-funded)

**Founder does:**
- Grant CFO-level access to financials
- Arrange 1–2 reference customer calls
- Expect daily email exchanges — pre-agreed response time target (24h)

### Week 8: Technical and product diligence

- Buyer's technology team reviews architecture, codebase, security posture, scalability, key dependencies
- Often involves a half-day technical deep-dive with the founder

**Founder does:**
- Prepare a codebase walkthrough video or screen-share
- Have `architecture.md`, `tech-stack.md`, and key-person risk docs ready
- Expect questions on test coverage, CI/CD, production incidents

### Week 9: Legal and HR diligence

- Buyer's legal team reviews corporate record, contracts, IP, disputes, employment
- Sub-processor and compliance review

**Founder does:**
- Ensure `02-legal/` and `03-privacy/` folders are current
- Be ready to answer about any informal contracts, verbal agreements, or unusual terms

### Week 10: Diligence report and findings

- Buyer consolidates findings into a diligence report
- "Red" findings go back to the founder for explanation or remediation
- Valuation may be retested if surprises emerge

**Founder does:**
- Respond to each finding with a written, clear explanation
- If genuine issues are found, negotiate price or structure adjustments rather than hiding them

## Stage 3 — Documentation and negotiation (Weeks 11–14)

### Week 11–12: SPA drafting

- Buyer's lawyers send a first draft of the Share Purchase Agreement (SPA)
- Core negotiation points:
  - **Purchase price and structure** (cash at close, deferred, earnout)
  - **Reps and warranties** — statements of fact the seller gives; if they turn out to be wrong, the buyer can claim
  - **Warranty cap and baskets** — max exposure and small-claim threshold
  - **Disclosure letter** — the founder's way of carving out known issues from the warranties (this is critical; do it carefully with a solicitor)
  - **Indemnities** — targeted protections for known risks (tax, IP, specific liabilities)
  - **Non-compete and non-solicit** — what the founder can and can't do post-close
  - **Earnout terms** — metrics, measurement period, acceleration on bad leaver, operational covenants

### Week 13: Disclosure letter

- Founder and solicitor prepare the **disclosure letter** — the single most important legal document for the founder because it protects against warranty claims for known issues
- Every known issue goes in the disclosure letter: customer disputes, compliance gaps, pending hires, informal contracts, anything material

### Week 14: Signing preparation

- Final SPA agreed
- Board approvals gathered
- Third-party consents where required (customer change-of-control clauses, landlord, key suppliers)
- Accounts frozen for completion account purposes

---

## Stage 4 — Signing and closing (Weeks 15–16)

### Week 15: Signing

- All documents signed (SPA, disclosure letter, employment/consultancy agreements, earnout document, ancillaries)
- If "simultaneous signing and closing" — the deal completes the same day
- If "split signing and closing" — closing is conditional on conditions precedent (CP clearance)

### Week 16: Closing

- Conditions precedent satisfied (consents, regulatory approvals if any)
- Funds flow: buyer wires purchase consideration; founder signs share transfers
- Companies House filings (share transfer, appointment/resignation of directors)
- Customers, employees, and key suppliers notified
- Post-close integration begins

---

## Post-closing (Months 1–24)

- **Day 1–7:** Customer and employee communications, founder intro letters, integration team introductions
- **Month 1:** Financial systems integration, brand decision (keep or rebrand), vendor consolidation
- **Month 3:** First completion accounts true-up
- **Month 6:** First earnout measurement period check (if monthly or quarterly metrics)
- **Month 12:** First earnout payment (if annual)
- **Month 12–24:** Continued earnout measurement; founder's lock-in typically ends in this window
- **Month 12–24:** Founder departure handover if not staying long-term

## Expected time commitment from the founder

| Stage | Founder hours/week |
|-------|---------------------|
| Pre-LOI (W1-5) | 10–20 |
| Diligence (W6-10) | 25–40 (this is exhausting) |
| SPA negotiation (W11-14) | 20–30 |
| Closing (W15-16) | Flexible, depends on CP work |
| Post-close (M1-12) | Whatever the engagement letter says |

## Common reasons deals slip or fall over

1. **Poor diligence prep** — buyer finds things the founder didn't disclose; trust breaks
2. **Customer concentration surprise** — a big customer leaves during diligence
3. **Buyer financing delay** — especially for trade buyers not using their own balance sheet
4. **Disclosure letter disputes** — founder tries to disclose too much or too little
5. **Earnout structure disagreement** — buyer wants it heavy, founder wants it light
6. **Key-person anxiety** — buyer gets cold feet on key-person risk late in the process
7. **External events** — market shift, buyer's own reorganization, personnel changes

## Founder checklist by week

- **W0:** Pack is ready. `dd-qa-master.md` updated. Reference customers pre-briefed. Solicitor identified.
- **W1:** NDA sent. Teaser delivered.
- **W2:** Management meeting scheduled.
- **W3:** Management meeting delivered. First Q&A batch handled.
- **W4:** Second Q&A batch. Reference customer #1 introduced.
- **W5:** IOI received. Solicitor engaged. Valuation framing.
- **W6:** LOI signed. Diligence opens.
- **W7-9:** Daily Q&A discipline. Update the buyer Q log.
- **W10:** Diligence findings received. Respond in writing within 48h.
- **W11:** SPA first draft received. Solicitor reviews.
- **W12-14:** Negotiate. Prepare disclosure letter carefully.
- **W15:** Signing.
- **W16:** Closing.
