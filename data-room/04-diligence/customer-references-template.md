# Customer References — Template

**Purpose:** Curate 3–5 customer references who are pre-briefed and willing to speak to a prospective buyer or investor. This saves weeks of diligence and, done well, becomes the single most valuable piece of evidence in a deal.

**Rule of thumb:** Never introduce a reference until the buyer has signed an NDA and the deal is past the LOI or serious indication-of-interest stage. References are a trust asset — don't burn them on tyre-kickers.

---

## Reference selection criteria

Choose customers who tick **all five**:

1. **Strong outcome** — you can articulate a specific, quantified result they got from The English Hub.
2. **Engaged sponsor** — the person you'd put on a call actually uses the product and likes working with you.
3. **Diversity** — 3–5 references should cover different segments, use cases, and customer sizes. A buyer can spot a curated "best only" selection.
4. **Discreet** — the contact is trustworthy with confidentiality. Deals leak when references gossip.
5. **Willing** — they've agreed in advance to take a 30-min call with a prospective buyer.

## Pre-brief script (for the founder)

Call or email each reference before making the introduction. Say:

> "Hi [name], we're in early-stage conversations with a prospective [investor / acquirer] for The English Hub. They've asked to speak with 2–3 customers as part of their diligence. Would you be willing to take a 30-minute call with them?
>
> It would help me a lot. I'd like to pre-brief you:
> - The person you'll speak to is [name, title, company]
> - They're interested in understanding [specific angle — e.g., outcomes, support quality, renewal intent]
> - I'd encourage you to speak honestly — I don't want a scripted call; I want you to tell them what you really think
> - Topics I'd prefer you don't go into: [e.g., discount you received, specific roadmap commitments]
> - The conversation is confidential — please don't share externally that the call happened
>
> When would work for you in the next 2 weeks?"

Follow up with a short calendar invite and a confidentiality note.

## What to ask the buyer *before* handing over a reference

1. Who exactly on your team will take the call?
2. What are the 3 questions most important to you?
3. Can you confirm the reference will not be contacted outside the agreed call?
4. What's your deal timeline — is the reference a last-mile check or mid-diligence?

Use the answers to match the buyer to the right reference (a detail-oriented analyst wants Reference X; a CEO-to-CEO wants Reference Y).

---

## Questions a buyer is likely to ask the reference

Be ready for these because the references will be asked them and you want them to answer well:

- How did you first hear about The English Hub?
- What problem were you trying to solve when you signed up?
- What alternatives did you consider?
- How has the experience been — what's gone well, what's been frustrating?
- What outcomes have you seen that you can attribute to the product?
- How responsive is the team? Who do you deal with?
- Would you buy again? Would you recommend to a peer?
- If the company were acquired by [name / category] tomorrow, how would you feel?

That last one is diagnostic — it tells the buyer whether customers are loyal to the founder or loyal to the product.

---

## Post-call hygiene

After a reference call:

1. Thank the customer privately. Ideally a small gesture (handwritten note, coffee voucher, LinkedIn recommendation).
2. Ask them what the buyer seemed most interested in — this is intelligence for your next meeting.
3. Log the call in the `buyer-q-log.csv`.

---

## Reference rotation

Don't use the same reference for every buyer. Rotate across your roster so no single customer gets 5 calls. Overuse kills goodwill.
