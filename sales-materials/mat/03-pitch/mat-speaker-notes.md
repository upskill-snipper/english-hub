# MAT Pitch — Speaker Notes (30 minutes)

**Use this with**: `mat-pitch-deck-outline.md`.

**Tone**: Calm, evidence-led, slightly understated. MAT central teams are sceptical of salespeople. The person who sounds least like a salesperson wins.

**Time budget**: 22 minutes presentation, 8 minutes for questions woven through naturally, leaving 15 minutes of the 45-minute slot for deeper Q&A and next-steps discussion.

**Delivery rules**:
1. Never read a slide. The slides are a silent scaffold.
2. Name someone in the room in the first 60 seconds.
3. Stop talking when they interrupt. Their interruption is data.
4. If they check their phone, the next sentence must be a stat.
5. Never promise anything you cannot defend in the next sentence.

---

## Opening (30 seconds — before slide 1)

> "Thank you for the time. I'll keep this tight — about 20 minutes of slides, then I want to make sure we have a proper conversation. Before I start, [Director of Ed name], can I check: what's the one question about English across the trust that you'd most want to leave this meeting with an answer to?"

**Why**: Flushes the real agenda before you've committed to a script. Write their answer on your notepad.

---

## Slide 1 — Title (30 seconds)

> "I'm here to talk about English at the scale of [MAT name] — the curriculum, the data, and the economics of running it as one trust rather than [N] separate schools."

Move to slide 2 without ceremony.

---

## Slide 2 — The trust-wide English problem (2 minutes)

> "Every MAT we work with has the same set of problems in English. Not a couple of them — all four.
>
> First, fragmentation. Your Year 8 English in School A is not the same as Year 8 English in School B. Different schemes, different assessments, different tools. That might be by accident or by design, but the result is the same — you can't reason about English across your trust.
>
> Second, data. Your central team is probably collating termly returns from each school, in different formats, late, and usually inconsistent. You can't answer the basic question: how is Year 9 reading age trending across the trust?
>
> Third, workload. English teachers are among the hardest subjects to recruit and retain. The workload — marking, planning, assessment — is crushing. When a good English teacher leaves School C, a whole year group suffers.
>
> Fourth, Ofsted. The framework is sharpening on reading, cultural capital, and curriculum sequencing. Every school in your trust gets inspected every 3–5 years. One weak English judgement dominates the local press.
>
> None of this is your fault. It's the structural shape of running English across a MAT. The solution isn't working harder — it's working at the trust layer."

**Watch for**: Nodding on any one of the four. The one they nod at loudest is the lever you'll use later.

---

## Slide 3 — Current fragmentation in [trust] (2 minutes)

> "If I had to guess what English looks like across [trust name] today, it probably looks something like this. One school on Pearson ActiveLearn. Another leaning on Oak National plus Bedrock. A third with a brilliant Head of English who's built everything themselves on Google Drive. All great people. None of it comparable.
>
> Tell me honestly — how close is that to your trust today?"

**Then shut up.** Let them talk. This is the single most valuable 90 seconds of the meeting. Write down every tool they name.

---

## Slide 4 — The cost of fragmentation (2 minutes)

> "Fragmentation has a cost, and the cost is bigger than people expect.
>
> Financially, MATs we've benchmarked are spending 30 to 45 percent more on English tooling than they need to, because every school negotiates its own contracts.
>
> In time, we consistently find 0.2 to 0.3 FTE of central team capacity goes to collating English data from schools. That's £15 to £25k of senior time every year, doing work that should be automated.
>
> And in outcomes — you simply can't improve what you can't compare. If your Year 9 reading age gap between your best and worst school is two years, but you don't know that, you can't target the intervention.
>
> These numbers are ballpark, not your numbers. But they're the right order of magnitude for a trust your size."

---

## Slide 5 — What if it was centralised (2 minutes)

> "Imagine instead that every secondary school in your trust ran English on the same platform. Same curriculum backbone — editable at the school level, so schools keep their identity. Same assessment framework, so the data is comparable. One analytics dashboard, visible to you and to each school's leadership team. One procurement framework, one renewal, one invoice.
>
> And when someone on your board asks 'how is English going in the trust' — you have an answer. In real time. With evidence."

---

## Slide 6 — Introducing The English Hub (2 minutes)

> "That's what we've built. The English Hub is a secondary English platform — Years 7 through 11 — with a curriculum, an assessment engine, and a MAT-level analytics layer. It wasn't designed for a single school and extended to MATs afterwards. It was designed for MATs from day one. Our pricing, our data model, our console, our procurement process — all of it is built around how a trust actually operates.
>
> What you're looking at on screen is our MAT dashboard — this is what your central team would see. One view across every secondary school in your trust."

Pause for the screenshot to land. Point at one chart. Move on.

---

## Slide 7 — Consistency across schools (1 minute)

> "Consistency doesn't mean every classroom looks identical. It means the backbone is shared. Year 7 Unit 1 has the same learning objectives, the same vocabulary set, the same reading expectations, the same assessment structure — in every school. But the texts a school chooses, the order they teach in, and the local context — those are the school's own.
>
> The practical effect: if a pupil moves between your schools mid-year, they don't lose a term. If a teacher moves, they're productive on day one. And your moderation conversations across schools actually work."

---

## Slide 8 — Data and insights (3 minutes)

This slide gets more time than the others. The dashboard is the single most compelling thing in the pack.

> "Now — this is the slide I'd want you to spend a moment on.
>
> This is what your central team would see every morning if [trust name] used The English Hub. Reading age progression by school. Pupil Premium gap by school. GCSE forecast by school. Every metric comparable across every school in your trust, updated as pupils complete work.
>
> [Name of Director of Education], if I asked you right now, 'which of your secondary schools has the fastest-improving reading age among Pupil Premium Year 8 boys' — how would you answer that today?"

Pause for the answer. The silence is the sale.

> "In our dashboard, you click two things. That's the level we're targeting."

---

## Slide 9 — Ofsted and cultural capital (2 minutes)

> "Ofsted's framework on English has sharpened in the last few years — reading fluency, cultural capital, curriculum sequencing, pupil progress. Every school in your trust is exposed to it.
>
> We can't promise you an inspection outcome. Nobody can. What we can promise is that when an inspector arrives on the Monday morning, the Head of English, the Head of School, and your central team have exactly the same one-click inspection pack — curriculum intent, sequencing maps, cultural capital rationale, reading age data, pupil progress. Ready to hand over.
>
> That doesn't make the inspection easy. It removes 80 percent of the evidence-gathering burden from the leadership team, so they can spend inspection week leading, not collating."

---

## Slide 10 — Teacher workload and retention (2 minutes)

> "The last pillar, and the one most likely to get you a champion in every school, is teacher workload.
>
> English teachers are among the hardest to recruit and retain in secondary. The workload is brutal — every piece of extended writing is slow to mark, every lesson needs careful planning, every assessment needs creating.
>
> Our customers consistently tell us that after a term on the platform, English teachers report getting 4 to 6 hours per week back. That's not because we're marking their Year 11 mock essays for them — it's because the objective assessment is automated, the lesson resources are ready and editable, and the assessment data they needed to manually tabulate just appears.
>
> Every teacher you keep this year instead of losing to the school down the road is worth 10 to 15 thousand pounds to the trust. Three teachers retained and this contract has paid for itself."

---

## Slide 11 — How a pilot would work (1 minute)

> "We never expect a MAT to commit without seeing this in their own schools. Here's the pilot shape we usually run.
>
> Two schools from your trust. Twelve weeks. Full platform access, our onboarding team does the heavy lifting. Weekly check-ins with your central team. At week twelve, we do a structured review with your leadership — pupil feedback, teacher feedback, outcome data, and a go/no-go decision.
>
> No long-term commitment until that decision is made. You pick the two schools; we suggest your most ambitious Head of English and your most pragmatic one."

---

## Slide 12 — Commercials (2 minutes)

> "On commercials — we price per student, banded. The more students across the trust, the lower the per-student rate.
>
> For a trust your size, we'd be in the [X] to [Y] band. I don't want to quote a number without understanding your pupil numbers, your contract length preference, and your payment terms first — so I'll put real numbers in writing this week after today's conversation.
>
> What I will say is that the total first-year cost for a trust your size is roughly in line with what we see MATs spending on fragmented English tools today — meaning the saving comes in year two, and compounds."

**Rule**: Never quote an exact number here without being asked. If asked, give a range and promise a tailored number within 48 hours.

---

## Slide 13 — Diligence and security (1 minute)

> "From a procurement and data protection perspective — we're UK-hosted, GDPR-compliant, Cyber Essentials Plus, and DfE digital standards aligned. Our Data Processing Agreement is pre-written for MAT deployments. We've pre-filled the standard MAT IT security questionnaire and can send it to your DPO in advance of the pilot kick-off.
>
> We know procurement kills deals. We've structured our paperwork to go through fast."

---

## Slide 14 — Why now (1 minute)

> "Three reasons I'd encourage you to move in this academic year. Your budget planning for next year starts in [month] — decisions taken then are hard to reverse. Ofsted's reading and cultural capital emphasis is sharpening each year. And the teacher retention battle gets harder every year — waiting a year means losing more teachers you didn't have to lose.
>
> None of those are dramatic. They're just the honest case for not waiting."

---

## Slide 15 — The ask (1 minute)

> "Here's what I'd like to ask for. A 60-minute scoping session in the next three weeks, with your Director of Education, your Head of English at one or two schools, and your data lead. We'll bring a demo with anonymised data shaped to your trust, a pilot design, and a real commercial proposal. Decision by [suggested date]. Does that work?"

## Handling the Q&A

### "How much will this cost our trust specifically?"

> "For a trust your size with [N] secondary schools and around [X] pupils in English, you'd be looking in the £[Y]–[Z] per student band. I'll put a specific number in writing within 48 hours, tailored to your exact pupil numbers."

### "We already use [competitor]."

> "Most of our MAT customers were using some combination of [tools] before. We rarely ask trusts to rip and replace in one go — we most often start with KS3 curriculum and assessment, and displace the fragmented stack over 18 months. Which tool would be hardest to leave?"

### "How long does procurement take?"

> "The fastest MAT deal we've closed was six weeks from this meeting. The longest was nine months. The difference is usually whether the procurement team is engaged early and whether legal review of the DPA starts in week one. I'd recommend we get our DPA template in front of your DPO immediately, even before the pilot decision."

### "What happens if we leave the trust, or a school leaves the trust?"

> "We'll cover TUPE and data portability in detail in the commercials doc. In short — the school's data belongs to the school. If a school transfers out of your MAT, we can migrate their data to whatever platform the receiving trust uses, or continue serving them directly. No lock-in."

### "Who else in the sector is using this?"

> "Happy to share references. I can put you in touch with [named customer] — they run [N] schools and piloted with us starting [date]. I'd rather they tell you honestly how it's going than me."

### "How do you integrate with SIMS / Arbor / Bromcom / Wonde?"

> "We integrate with [specific list]. Pupil data syncs automatically, so your teachers don't double-enter. I'll send the technical integration spec as part of the scoping pack."

### "Are you a real company or a startup that'll be gone in a year?"

> "Fair question and I'd be worried about the same thing. We're [company status — revenue, team size, funding, years operating]. Our oldest school customer has been with us for [X] years. I'll send our latest corporate diligence pack along with everything else after this meeting."

### Silence — they're thinking

Don't fill it. Count to eight in your head. Let them speak first.

---

## Closing the meeting

> "I've taken up your time and I appreciate it. Three things before I leave:
>
> One — I'll send a short email within 24 hours summarising what we discussed and the specific numbers tailored to your trust.
>
> Two — I'd like to book the scoping session now if that's possible. What does [Director of Ed name]'s calendar look like in the next three weeks?
>
> Three — is there anyone else in the trust I should introduce myself to in parallel? Usually the CFO wants to see this early."

Leave a physical one-pager on the table. Shake hands with everyone in the room, not just the CEO.

---

## After the meeting — within 2 hours

- Send the tailored email.
- Log the meeting in CRM.
- Send the executive summary one-pager as a follow-up if you didn't leave a physical copy.
- Connect on LinkedIn with everyone who was in the room.
- Debrief internally: what did they care about? Where did they push back? What was the "tell" moment?
