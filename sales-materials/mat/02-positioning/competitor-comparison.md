# Competitor Comparison — What MATs Currently Buy

**Purpose**: Arm sales with an honest, defensible comparison against the tools MAT central teams are most likely already using or evaluating. Assume the buyer has heard of and/or uses at least two of these.

**Posture**: Never trash a competitor. Acknowledge strengths, then show where the MAT motion specifically exposes their limits.

## Head-to-head — detailed comparison

### vs. Pearson ActiveLearn

**What it is**: Pearson's all-purpose subject platform, used by many schools for English through Bug Club, Wordsmith, Rapid, etc.

**Strengths**:
- Brand trust and inertia.
- Bundled with textbook purchases.
- Deep catalogue of named authors and titles.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Licensing is school-by-school, not trust-level. Central teams cannot get a unified view.
- Analytics are shallow — no cross-school comparison.
- Updates slow; product feels like a digitised textbook.
- Curriculum is broad but not specifically MAT-centralisable.
- Procurement is pushed through the Pearson rep model rather than a framework.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Pearson is a textbook publisher that built software. We are software built from day one around how a MAT actually operates — one framework agreement, one console, one data model, one curriculum backbone. If you're already spending £X on ActiveLearn across 12 schools, we can cover the same curriculum ground with genuine trust-wide analytics for less."

---

### vs. Oak National Academy

**What it is**: The DfE-backed free national curriculum and lesson resource bank.

**Strengths**:
- Free.
- Government-backed, so politically safe.
- Broad coverage.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- It's a resource library, not a platform. No assessment, no data, no dashboard, no teacher workflow.
- No accountability layer — a MAT cannot tell whether its schools are actually using Oak or using it well.
- No personalisation.
- Quality is variable across subjects.
- No support, no SLA, no customer success.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Oak is a fantastic free resource library. Your trust will almost certainly use it alongside us. What Oak doesn't do is tell you what's being taught, how well, or with what outcomes — and it doesn't reduce your teachers' marking load. We're the layer above Oak that turns good resources into measurable trust-wide outcomes."

---

### vs. Bedrock Learning

**What it is**: Vocabulary and grammar practice platform, popular in English departments for tier-2 vocabulary building.

**Strengths**:
- Focused and well-designed.
- Strong tier-2 vocabulary expansion.
- Teachers like it.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Narrow — vocabulary and grammar only. Doesn't cover reading comprehension, writing, literature, or GCSE prep at depth.
- Does not give the central team a trust-wide curriculum view.
- Pricing typically per-school.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Bedrock is a good tier-2 vocab tool and we don't compete with its narrow focus. What Bedrock doesn't do is cover literature, extended writing, GCSE practice, or give your central team a single view across your whole trust. Think of us as the curriculum-wide platform and Bedrock as an optional vocab add-on your schools can keep."

---

### vs. Seneca Learning

**What it is**: Free (freemium) cross-subject learning platform, heavily used by students for GCSE revision.

**Strengths**:
- Huge student adoption — students already use it.
- Free tier is genuinely usable.
- Covers many subjects.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Student self-direction, not teacher-led curriculum.
- No cross-school trust dashboard.
- Data quality for MAT decision-making is thin.
- No dedicated English curriculum design.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Seneca is a student-facing revision tool. Your students will still use it. We're the teacher-and-trust platform — the curriculum design, assessment, and analytics layer that lets your central team actually manage English across the trust."

---

### vs. Educake

**What it is**: Low-cost question bank and quizzing platform, used for homework and retrieval practice.

**Strengths**:
- Cheap, simple, well-loved by busy teachers.
- Good for retrieval practice.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Quizzing only — not a curriculum platform.
- Limited analytics beyond basic completion rates.
- No MAT-wide data model.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Educake does retrieval practice well. That's about 10% of what a MAT English provision needs. The other 90% — curriculum, writing, assessment, analytics — is what we do. If Educake is £X per student and we're £Y, our £Y replaces Educake plus several other tools your schools are separately paying for."

---

### vs. GCSEPod

**What it is**: Short-form video revision content for GCSE subjects.

**Strengths**:
- Bite-sized, student-friendly.
- Good for homework and independent revision.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Video library, not curriculum.
- No assessment or writing support.
- No central management layer.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "GCSEPod is a useful KS4 video library that sits under our curriculum layer. We integrate with content assets like this, not against them."

---

### vs. Tassomai

**What it is**: Adaptive quizzing platform popular in science and increasingly English.

**Strengths**:
- Adaptive algorithm.
- Good homework-completion tracking.

**Where it struggles for MATs**:
- Science-first product; English is an extension, not a purpose-built offer.
- Narrow question-only format.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Tassomai is a great adaptive quizzer. We're an English-first curriculum and assessment platform — purpose-built for the subject, not extended from one."

---

### vs. Ark English Mastery

**What it is**: Published knowledge-rich KS3 English curriculum from Ark Curriculum Plus. Sold as booklets, teacher guides, and assessments.

**Strengths**:
- Rigorous, research-informed, knowledge-rich.
- Deep curriculum design by a respected team.
- Widely adopted.

**Where it struggles for MATs** (not the MAT's own trust):
- Paper-first. Digital experience is limited.
- Assessment is manual and teacher-intensive.
- No live analytics.
- Ends at KS3 — no KS4 equivalent.
- Not bundled with teacher workload tools.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Ark English Mastery is one of the best KS3 curricula in the country. We don't replace it — we sit underneath it. English Hub is the assessment, data, and KS4 continuation layer that turns a brilliant curriculum into a measurable trust-wide outcome. Many of our best MAT conversations start with 'we use Ark Mastery at KS3 and then fall off a cliff at KS4' — that's exactly the gap we fill."

**Exception**: If the MAT is Ark itself, do not pitch against English Mastery. Position as complementary for KS4 and cross-school data.

---

### vs. "We built it in-house"

**What it is**: The most common competitor. MAT has a central English lead who has built schemes of work, assessment calendars, and tracking spreadsheets from scratch.

**Strengths**:
- Perfect cultural fit — built by and for the trust.
- No licence cost.
- Teacher ownership.

**Where it struggles**:
- **True cost is invisible**: probably 0.5–1.5 FTE of central team time.
- Fragile — when the person who built it leaves, the system collapses.
- Version control is a mess.
- No real analytics, just descriptive stats in Excel.
- Doesn't scale as the trust grows.
- Central team spends its time maintaining spreadsheets instead of improving teaching.

**Our counter-pitch**:
> "Your in-house system is a real accomplishment and your team should be proud of it. But here's the honest question: how much of your central team's time goes into maintaining it versus using it? We don't want to throw away what you've built. We want to give your team the platform so they can stop being spreadsheet custodians and start being school improvers. We'll even import your existing schemes of work in week one."

## Rules of engagement

1. **Never trash a competitor in writing.** Say "different purpose" or "complementary". Leave the "worse than us" framing for verbal conversations only.
2. **Assume the MAT is already paying for 3–5 of these.** Our wedge is consolidation: we replace ~60% of their current stack.
3. **If they love a specific product**, find the integration or coexistence story. Don't die on hills.
4. **Map the renewal calendar**. The best time to win a MAT is the 6 months before their biggest incumbent contract ends.
5. **Lead with consolidation savings**, not feature parity. Feature-parity arguments are a slow loss.
