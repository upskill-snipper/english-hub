# Case Study Template — Post-Pilot

This is the template used to capture results from a pilot school and turn them into a published case study. It is completed jointly between the founder and the school within two weeks of the end of a pilot (or, for a Founding School that has moved to a full licence, within the first term of use).

**Purpose of the case study:**
1. Provide social proof for prospective schools.
2. Give the Founding School a visible artefact that reflects well on them.
3. Create a reusable asset for website, LinkedIn, proposals, and sales calls.

**Sign-off:** The school must approve all public-facing copy before publication. Nothing is published without written consent.

---

## The template

Copy the section below into a working document to draft the case study. When complete, format the final version as a branded 2-page PDF and a web page.

---

### [SCHOOL NAME]

**Location:** [TOWN / REGION]
**Type:** [ACADEMY / INDEPENDENT / COMMUNITY / MAT MEMBER]
**Size:** [NUMBER] pupils on roll
**English department:** [NUMBER] teachers, [NUMBER] classes across KS3 and KS4
**Used The English Hub for:** [X] weeks, starting [DATE]
**Primary year groups in scope:** [YEAR GROUPS]

---

### The headline quote

A single quote from the Head of English, ideally under 25 words, that captures the most specific change. Short is better than comprehensive.

> "[DRAFT QUOTE]"
>
> — [HEAD OF ENGLISH NAME], Head of English, [SCHOOL NAME]

---

### The challenge

A 150-word paragraph describing the specific problem the school faced before using The English Hub. Be specific. Avoid generic workload framing.

**Drafting prompt:** Answer these questions in the interview with the HoD:

- What was the most frustrating thing about extended writing marking before?
- How long was the average marking turnaround for a Year 10 piece of extended writing?
- What had the department tried before that hadn't worked?
- What were the knock-on effects on teachers, pupils, or SLT reporting?

---

### What they did

A 200-word paragraph describing how the school used The English Hub during the pilot or early use. Include specifics: which year groups, which classes, how many tasks per week, which teachers were involved, how onboarding went.

**Drafting prompt:** Answer these questions in the interview:

- Which class did you start with, and why that class?
- How did the onboarding session go?
- How many tasks per week did the pilot teacher set?
- What surprised you in the first week?
- How did pupils respond to the new feedback style?
- Were there things you changed as you went along?

---

### What happened

A 250-word paragraph with the specific outcomes. This is the most important section.

**Drafting prompt:** Quantify where you can, qualify where you can't:

- Hours saved per teacher per week on marking (target: specific number)
- Feedback turnaround time before vs. during the pilot
- Specific pupil progress observed (in the teacher's own words)
- Any behavioural changes in pupils' approach to feedback
- Changes in teacher workload or wellbeing
- SLT or head teacher reactions

**Numbers to include if available:**
- Hours saved per teacher per week
- Pieces of extended writing marked per teacher per week
- Average feedback turnaround time (before and after)
- Proportion of pupils completing redrafts
- Any measured skill progression (analysis, inference, vocabulary)

---

### Teacher voice

A short section with one or two quotes from the pilot teacher (not just the HoD). These are often the most credible quotes for readers.

> "[DRAFT QUOTE]"
>
> — [TEACHER NAME], [YEAR GROUP / ROLE], [SCHOOL NAME]

> "[OPTIONAL SECOND QUOTE]"
>
> — [TEACHER NAME], [SCHOOL NAME]

---

### Pupil voice (optional, with consent)

If the school is comfortable and parental consent is in place, include one short pupil quote. Always anonymised.

> "[DRAFT QUOTE]"
>
> — Year [X] pupil, [SCHOOL NAME]

---

### What's next

A 100-word paragraph describing how the school is planning to use The English Hub going forward. This is where you signal scale: single class to whole department, one year group to KS3+KS4, one school to MAT.

**Drafting prompt:**
- Are you rolling out to the full department?
- Are you expanding to additional year groups?
- Is there interest from other schools in your MAT?
- What's the next feature you'd like to see?

---

### Facts at a glance

A sidebar that can be pulled out for the web version and social media.

- **Hours saved per teacher per week:** [NUMBER]
- **Feedback turnaround time:** improved from [BEFORE] to [AFTER]
- **Pupils using the platform:** [NUMBER]
- **Department growth:** rolling out from [X] class to [Y] classes

---

### About [SCHOOL NAME]

A one-paragraph "about" section describing the school in the school's own words. Take this from their website and lightly edit. Show it to the school before publishing.

## Production process

### Step 1 — The pilot review conversation (week 4 of pilot)

- 45-minute call with the HoD and pilot teacher.
- Walk through the numbers and the qualitative observations.
- Capture quotes verbatim.
- Ask permission to write up a case study. Explain exactly what it would say and how it would be published.

### Step 2 — First draft (within 5 working days)

- Draft the case study using this template.
- Include real numbers and real quotes.
- Keep it under 800 words.

### Step 3 — School review

- Send the draft to the HoD for comment.
- Offer to adjust any quote or number.
- Give the school a named approver on your side if they want to go via comms or SLT.

### Step 4 — Sign-off

- Get written sign-off from the named school contact.
- Save the sign-off email permanently.
- Note any changes requested in your case study library.

### Step 5 — Publication

- Publish the web version with the quotes and facts at a glance.
- Publish the PDF version.
- Post a LinkedIn announcement tagging the school, with the school's explicit permission.
- Add the quote and facts-at-a-glance to the pitch deck (`02-pitch-deck/pitch-deck-outline.md`, slide 10).
- Send the published version to the school for their own use.

### Step 6 — Ongoing use

- Reference the case study in all cold outreach to similar schools.
- Cite specific numbers from it in pitch meetings.
- Update the case study annually with fresh numbers from the next term's data.

## Rules for case studies

1. **Never fake or exaggerate a number.** Every claim must be traceable to a specific piece of evidence — teacher timesheet, platform analytics, direct quote. If challenged by a prospective school, you must be able to back it up.

2. **Never publish without school sign-off.** Even if the school has verbally agreed, get the written sign-off. Save the email.

3. **Protect pupil privacy.** No pupil names, no identifiable quotes or photos without explicit parental consent. Anonymise by year group and class.

4. **Use the school's preferred language.** If they call themselves an "academy" not a "school", match it. If they use "learners" not "pupils", match it.

5. **Keep them short.** A case study longer than two printed pages is less likely to be read.

6. **Version them.** Keep every version of every case study in a versioned folder. When a school updates their numbers, update the case study and note the version.

7. **Match the offer.** If a school was a pilot only and didn't sign, call it a "pilot case study" and make that clear. Do not imply a commercial relationship that doesn't exist.

8. **Thank the school every time you reference it.** A simple email: "Quick note — your case study generated three demo meetings this week. Thank you again for being willing to be public about this."

## Case study library index

Maintain a simple list of all published case studies in the sales pack, updated when a new one ships. Suggested location: `sales-materials/founding-schools/06-close/case-study-library.md` (create when the first case study is published).

For each case study, track:

- School name
- Date of publication
- Primary quote
- Headline number
- Version number
- Sign-off email reference
- Link to the web version
- Link to the PDF version
