# ROI Calculator — The English Hub

A plain-English model the founder can talk through with a Head of English or a business manager to show the return on investment of The English Hub. This isn't a spreadsheet — it is a set of clearly explained calculations you can walk through on a video call, backed by simple, defensible assumptions.

**Use this file:**
1. As a reference for commercial conversations.
2. To generate a per-school custom one-pager before a demo.
3. As the source of truth for any ROI claim you make in writing.

---

## The four sources of return

There are four quantifiable returns from The English Hub. A sensible commercial conversation covers at least two of them.

1. **Teacher time saved** (the biggest return)
2. **Grade uplift for pupils** (the hardest to prove, but the most valuable to schools)
3. **Reduced spend on off-the-shelf alternatives**
4. **Teacher retention** (high leverage, hard to quantify)

---

## 1. Teacher time saved

### Assumption set

- **Average hours per week spent marking extended writing, per English teacher:** 4 hours
- **Reduction with The English Hub:** 50 percent (confidence: medium — based on early pilot data, 4–8 weeks of use)
- **Hours saved per teacher per week:** 2 hours
- **Teaching weeks per year:** 38
- **Hours saved per teacher per year:** 76

### Valuing a teacher hour

There are two honest ways to value a teacher hour and schools respond to different framings.

**Option A — at replacement cost (what a cover supply teacher costs):**
- £150–£200 per day = £20–£27 per hour
- Conservative estimate: **£22 per hour**

**Option B — at total compensation (the "salary plus on-costs" view):**
- Average English teacher total compensation: ~£48,000 including pension and NI
- 1,265 hours of directed time per year
- Roughly **£38 per hour** of working time

Use Option A for the headline claim. It is conservative, defensible, and less likely to draw accusations of hype.

### Worked example — 8-teacher English department

- Hours saved per year: 8 × 76 = **608 hours**
- Valued at £22/hour: 608 × 22 = **£13,376**
- Valued at £38/hour: 608 × 38 = **£23,104**

### Comparison to licence fee

- Licence fee for 1,200-pupil KS3+KS4 school at standard rate: £26,400
- Licence fee at Founding Schools rate: £13,200
- Teacher time saving at conservative £22/hour: £13,376
- **At Founding Schools pricing, the teacher time saving alone roughly pays for the licence fee.**

### What to say in the commercial conversation

> "Let's stay conservative. If each of your English teachers gets 2 hours a week back on extended writing marking — and I'll happily prove or disprove that in the pilot — that's 76 hours a year per teacher. Valued at the cost of a supply teacher, that's about £22 an hour, so around £1,670 per teacher per year. Across a department of 8, that's £13,400 a year — which is roughly the Founding Schools licence fee. Everything beyond that — the grade uplift, the pupil feedback speed, the dashboard — is on top."

## 2. Grade uplift for pupils

### Assumption set

- **Observed skill progression improvement in pilot (analysis, inference, vocabulary):** 1 progress point per pupil over a half term, in the tracked skills
- **GCSE grade uplift correlation:** A sustained 1-point improvement in these skills across KS4 correlates with roughly a 0.3–0.5 grade uplift at GCSE English Literature and English Language
- **Confidence:** low to medium — based on a small pilot, not independent academic research

Be honest about confidence. Schools respect it.

### How to frame it

**Don't say:** "We'll improve every child's GCSE by half a grade."
**Do say:** "Based on early pilot data we're seeing meaningful progress in analytical writing skills within a half term. If that translates to the GCSE grade boundary, the effect is in the region of a 0.3–0.5 grade uplift — but I'd rather we prove or disprove that at your school specifically over the course of a pilot and the first term."

### Value to the school

- **Progress 8 impact:** Even a 0.2 increase in average English Language or Literature scores is Progress 8 visible.
- **League table impact:** Visible on the DfE performance tables.
- **SLT narrative:** "English is the bright spot this year."

### What to say in the commercial conversation

> "If this worked, your SLT would see it in the data before half term — not in a year. That's part of why we run a pilot. Four weeks is enough to generate the first signal. By the end of a full term, you'll know whether it's shifting the underlying skill. If it's not, we've both learned something and you can walk away."

## 3. Reduced spend on off-the-shelf alternatives

### Assumption set

Many schools are paying for one or more of the following:

- **Seneca Premium:** £3–£5 per pupil per year (approximate)
- **Educake:** £2–£4 per pupil per year
- **Sparx Reader:** £4–£8 per pupil per year
- **GCSE Pod:** £3–£5 per pupil per year

A medium-sized school might be spending £8,000–£15,000 across several of these.

### The caveat

The English Hub is **not** a one-for-one replacement for these tools. We are explicit about that in `01-positioning/unique-value-proposition.md`. Most schools should keep at least one other tool alongside The English Hub.

However, the presence of these tools demonstrates that the school has:
- Budget for edtech
- Procurement pathways for edtech
- An appetite to use digital tools in English

Use the comparison not to displace other tools but to make the licence fee feel proportionate.

### What to say in the commercial conversation

> "You're already investing meaningfully in English edtech — I saw [TOOL] on your website. I'm not suggesting you replace that. I'm suggesting we fill the gap that none of those tools touch: extended writing feedback. The Founding Schools price sits in the same band as the tools you're already running."

## 4. Teacher retention

### Assumption set

- **Cost to replace a secondary teacher who leaves:** £5,000–£10,000 (recruitment, cover, induction, lost productivity)
- **Sector churn rate in English departments:** roughly 14 percent per year
- **Proportion of leavers citing workload as a primary reason:** around 60 percent

### The claim we will make

We do not claim The English Hub stops teachers leaving. We claim it removes one of the biggest daily workload pains and reduces one of the reasons teachers give for leaving.

### The claim we will not make

We will not put a number on retention uplift until we have 12 months of data from Founding Schools. Don't guess.

### What to say in the commercial conversation

> "I'm not going to promise you'll keep teachers longer. What I can tell you is that when a new English teacher in one of our pilot schools told us that The English Hub had saved their first half term, I was more proud of that than any product metric. If the thing driving your best early-career teachers away is Sunday afternoon marking, this changes the shape of that Sunday afternoon."

## Rules for using ROI numbers in conversations

1. **Never present a single number as a guarantee.** Always show the conservative version first.
2. **Always base it on their real numbers.** If they have 7 teachers and 1,100 pupils, use those numbers — not generic averages.
3. **Be honest about pilot-stage data.** "We're seeing this in pilot" is very different from "this is proven".
4. **Offer to stand behind the claim.** If you tell a school "you'll save 2 hours a teacher a week", be ready to measure it at the end of the pilot.
5. **Never use ROI as the lead message.** Lead with pain (Message 1 in `key-messages.md`), not numbers. ROI is the confirmation step, not the hook.
