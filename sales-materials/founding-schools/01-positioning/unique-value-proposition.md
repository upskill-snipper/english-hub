# Unique Value Proposition — The English Hub vs Competitors

## Our one-line UVP

> **The English Hub is the only platform built specifically for the English department — one that marks extended writing, aligns to GCSE and KS3 mark schemes, and gives teachers back hours of marking without replacing their judgement.**

Every other tool in a school's edtech stack is either generic (Seneca, Quizlet), built for a different subject (Sparx, Educake), or treats English as a tagged-on afterthought.

---

## The four things only we do

1. **Extended writing feedback, not just multiple choice.** We assess paragraphs and essays against mark schemes. Most competitors assess MCQs and short answers only.
2. **Built on real English schemes of work.** Our content library starts from KS3/KS4 English curricula (AQA, Edexcel, OCR, Eduqas), not a generic question bank. Teachers can map tasks to their existing planning in under two minutes.
3. **Teacher-reviewed AI, not unsupervised AI.** Every piece of AI-generated feedback is surfaced to the teacher for a one-click approval or edit. No feedback reaches the student without a human in the loop.
4. **One-click Head of Department dashboard.** Progress by pupil, class, teacher and cohort — exportable, SLT-ready, mapped to GCSE grade boundaries.

## Competitor-by-competitor comparison

### vs Seneca Learning

**What Seneca is good at:** Free content at scale, student-driven self-study, broad subject coverage, recognisable brand.

**Where it falls short for English:**
- Multiple choice and fill-in-the-blank only. Cannot assess extended writing.
- Content is generic and spans every subject — depth in English is shallow compared to maths or science.
- No meaningful mark scheme alignment for English GCSE extended response questions.
- Teacher dashboard is a leaderboard, not a progress diagnostic.

**Our counter-message:** "Seneca is a great revision starting point for your students. But it doesn't mark essays, and it doesn't tell your Heads of Year why Amir's analytical writing slipped between mocks. We do both."

---

### vs Educake

**What Educake is good at:** Quiz-based homework, easy setup, syllabus-aligned question banks, teacher-friendly UI, fair pricing.

**Where it falls short for English:**
- Designed primarily for science and maths. English content is thinner and relies on recall-style questions.
- No extended writing assessment — the one thing English teachers most need help with.
- No AI feedback on pupil responses.
- Dashboards are quiz-based (right/wrong), not skill-based (analysis, inference, vocabulary).

**Our counter-message:** "Educake is a strong homework quiz tool, and if you're using it for science, keep it. But the reason it doesn't solve the English marking problem is that it was never built to. We were."

---

### vs Sparx (English / Reader)

**What Sparx is good at:** Adaptive homework with proven outcomes in maths, well-funded, well-branded, growing English offer (Sparx Reader), strong MAT sales team.

**Where it falls short for English:**
- Sparx Reader is a reading fluency tool, not a writing development tool. It doesn't help with extended response.
- The adaptive engine was built for maths and doesn't generalise cleanly to writing.
- Implementation cost and training overhead are significant — often a multi-term rollout.
- Lock-in and pricing model can be heavy for smaller schools.

**Our counter-message:** "If Sparx Reader is getting your students reading, fantastic — keep it. The Hub is what sits next to it: we take the reading they're doing and help them write about it well, with feedback the teacher would give if they had twice as many hours in the day."

---

### vs Quizlet

**What Quizlet is good at:** Student-made flashcards, free tier, social learning, huge existing user base of pupils.

**Where it falls short for English:**
- Flashcards and matching games only. No assessment, no feedback, no teacher workflow.
- Consumer product — no data protection posture serious schools can rely on at scale.
- Teacher dashboards are minimal; zero alignment to mark schemes or progress frameworks.
- Student-generated content means quality is inconsistent.

**Our counter-message:** "Quizlet is fine for vocab drilling, and students will use it whether you approve it or not. But it can't tell you how Zara is doing against her GCSE target grade. It isn't designed to. That's our job."

---

## Where we deliberately do not compete

Being honest about what we are **not** is part of the UVP. Tell schools this up front — it builds trust.

- **We are not a maths or science tool.** Full stop. If a school wants one platform for every subject, we are not the right answer. We are English-only and proud of it.
- **We are not a whole-school MIS.** We don't replace Arbor, Bromcom or SIMS.
- **We are not a parent-engagement app.** We don't push notifications to parents. We feed data into whatever parent-engagement tool the school already uses.
- **We are not free.** Free tools have their place. We are a paid, serious tool for departments that want measurable outcomes.
