# Pitch Deck Speaker Notes — 20-Minute Pitch + 10-Minute Demo

Full speaker notes for the founder to deliver the pitch deck in roughly 30 minutes. Designed to be spoken from memory with the deck as a prompt, not read off cue cards. Time markers are approximate.

## [00:02 — 00:05] Slide 2 — The problem

"I taught English for [X] years before I started this. The thing I remember most clearly is Sunday afternoons. A stack of books on the kitchen table. Thirty extended responses to mark. And the gnawing feeling that no matter how much feedback I wrote, half of it would bounce off the page when the students got it back on Wednesday.

Across the country, an English teacher marks something like 500 pieces of extended writing per term. Five hundred. That's not a typo. And the wild thing is that none of the edtech tools schools are spending money on actually touch that workload.

I've spoken to probably 300 heads of English in the last year. Almost every one of them says the same thing: marking is the bottleneck, and nothing on the market solves it.

Is that a fair description of what you see in your department?"

**Pause. Let them say yes or push back. If they push back — "That's interesting, tell me more." If they say yes — acknowledge and move on.**

## [00:05 — 00:08] Slide 3 — Why nothing else works

"Schools usually have at least one or two of these tools already. And they're all fine at what they do.

Seneca is brilliant if you want free revision content and the engagement of a student leaderboard — but it was never built to mark an essay. You can't give it a paragraph on Macbeth and get useful feedback.

Sparx Reader is great for reading fluency, particularly lower down the school. But Sparx is fundamentally a reading tool, not a writing tool. The reading Sparx gets you is the raw material — but the writing has to come from somewhere.

Educake works well for science and maths homework. The English content is quiz-based and thin.

Quizlet — students will use it for vocab regardless. But you can't run a department on flashcards.

None of these are bad. They just aren't solving the central workload problem of an English department. Which is how extended writing gets marked."

---

## [00:08 — 00:12] Slide 4 — The solution

"So The English Hub is a platform that sits next to what you already have and does one thing: it helps you mark extended writing, at pace, without sacrificing the quality of the feedback.

Four things make it different.

First — it assesses extended responses. Paragraphs, essays, analytical writing. Against real mark schemes — AQA, Edexcel, OCR, Eduqas. Not just multiple choice.

Second — the content library is built on the English curriculum. Not a generic question bank. You can map a task to your existing scheme of work in about two minutes.

Third — and this one matters — the AI doesn't mark anything without you. Every piece of feedback lands in a teacher review screen, and you either accept it with one click, or you edit it, or you throw it out. The teacher is always in the loop. Nothing goes to a student with no teacher signature on it.

Fourth — you get a Head of English dashboard that shows you how every pupil, every class, every teacher is doing, skill by skill, against mark scheme criteria.

That's the product in one minute. Let me show you."

## Delivery principles

- **Slow down.** Most founders talk at 180 words per minute when nervous. Aim for 140.
- **Ask questions.** You should be asking at least one question per major slide. This is a conversation, not a monologue.
- **Use the department's real language.** If they call it "Eng Lit", so do you. If they call it "Literature Paper 1", so do you.
- **Never oversell outcomes.** Be honest about the early stage of data.
- **Mention the founder pain.** Saying "I used to be the teacher with the red pen on Sunday night" earns enormous trust. Don't skip that line.
- **Close with a specific next step.** Before you leave the room, you should have either a pilot start date, a decision date, or a follow-up meeting in the calendar.
