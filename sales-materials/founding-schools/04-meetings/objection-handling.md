# Objection Handling — Top 15 School Objections

These are the objections that will come up. Having a prepared, concise response for each one is the difference between a confident sales conversation and a stumble. Never memorise these word for word — internalise the shape of the answer, then speak naturally.

**The general rule for all objections:**

1. Acknowledge the concern. Don't dismiss it.
2. Ask a question to understand it better.
3. Respond with a short, specific answer.
4. Offer a concrete next step.

---

## Objection 1 — "It's too expensive."

**Bad response:** "It's actually cheaper than X."
**Good response:**

> "I hear that a lot, and it's a completely fair first reaction. Can I ask what you're comparing it to — a specific tool, or just generally? Because the number that usually matters more is cost per pupil per week, not the headline licence fee. At Founding Schools pricing, you're looking at something like [X pence] per pupil per week. That's less than most schools spend on exercise books, and it replaces a meaningful chunk of the Sunday afternoon marking. Can I show you the ROI sheet I put together — it takes the teacher time saving and puts it against the licence fee so you can judge whether it's proportionate?"

---

## Objection 2 — "We already use Seneca / Educake / Sparx / Quizlet."

> "Good — those are solid tools and my advice isn't to rip them out. We sit alongside them, not instead. Can I ask what you use [TOOL] for specifically? Usually the honest answer is it's for vocabulary drill or quizzing or reading fluency. None of those tools mark extended writing against a mark scheme, which is the specific job we do. So the question isn't 'swap us for them', it's 'is there a workload problem in extended writing feedback that isn't being touched by anything you have'. Is there?"

---

## Objection 3 — "Our teachers are too busy to learn another tool."

> "That's exactly the problem we're trying to solve, so I take it seriously. Two things: first, the tool is designed for a 20-minute onboarding — we've built it so an English teacher who's never seen it can be setting tasks and reviewing feedback the same afternoon. Second, and more important — we'd never ask a department to do this cold. I personally onboard every Founding School. I come to you, in person or by video, and spend 90 minutes with your team walking through the first two lessons. Every Founding School has a private Slack channel with me. If it's breaking the workload promise, we pull it out — no questions. Can I show you the onboarding flow so you can judge how much friction there actually is?"

---

## Objection 4 — "We have concerns about data protection."

> "You should. Every school should. Three things specifically for you: we are fully GDPR compliant, we have a data processing agreement ready to share with your data lead today, and all our data is hosted in the UK. We also support single sign-on with Microsoft and Google, so your pupils don't create new accounts. Can I send the DPA and our privacy documentation to you and your DPO before we go any further? I'd rather you review that properly than rush it."

---

## Objection 5 — "We'd want to run a pilot first."

> "Brilliant — so would I. Every Founding School starts with a 4-week free pilot. One class, your choice — KS3 or KS4. No credit card, no integration, no long setup. I onboard your nominated teacher personally. At the end of the four weeks we sit down and look at what happened and you decide whether to go ahead. Want to pick two possible start dates right now?"

---

## Objection 6 — "I need my headteacher's approval."

> "Completely reasonable. What can I do to help that conversation? I can give you a one-page summary they can read in 90 seconds, or I'd be happy to join a 15-minute call with them if that's quicker. I can also come in on an INSET day and present to the leadership team if you'd like. What works best for how decisions get made at [SCHOOL NAME]?"

---

## Objection 7 — "What if the AI gets it wrong?"

> "Good question, and honestly the most important one. The short answer is: the AI never has the final word in our product. Every piece of feedback lands in a teacher review screen. You approve it, you edit it, or you throw it out. Nothing reaches a pupil without a teacher click. The AI is a first draft, not a verdict. Show you the review screen?"

---

## Objection 8 — "I'm worried this will de-skill teachers or encourage shortcuts."

> "I've thought about this a lot — it's the thing I care about most because I was a teacher. What we've seen in pilot so far is the opposite. When the drudge part of marking is drafted for them, teachers spend more time on the judgement calls that matter — the 'this is where the argument breaks down' moments. And because the teacher is editing every piece of feedback, they're still actively reading every pupil's response — they're just doing it in 30 seconds rather than 3 minutes. If at any point in the pilot you feel your team is de-skilling, I'd want you to tell me and we'd pull it."

---

## Objection 9 — "Will my pupils just get the AI to write their essays and submit those?"

> "They'll try. They do already, with or without us. Two things we do about it. First, the platform is designed around writing in class — the pupil response is typed into our environment, not pasted in, and we have typing analytics that flag sudden paste events or inconsistent typing speed to the teacher. Second, we have an 'author attribution' check that compares the submitted writing to the pupil's previous writing style — again, a signal for the teacher, not a verdict. We treat academic honesty as a teacher's judgement call and we give them the data to make it."

---

## Objection 10 — "We don't have budget until the next financial year."

> "Totally understood. The financial year rolls around in [APRIL / SEPTEMBER] — two options. Option one: run the free 4-week pilot now, while budget isn't an issue, so that you have real data when the budget conversation happens. Option two: we hold a Founding Schools slot for you with a named intent to sign at the start of the financial year. Which is more useful?"

---

## Objection 11 — "We need to see results from other schools first."

> "Fair. The awkward truth is that as a Founding School, you'd be one of the ones providing those results. We have [X] pilot schools running right now and I'm happy to share anonymised early data, and — if you want — put you on a call with one of them directly. Would a 20-minute reference call with another HoD help you get comfortable?"

---

## Objection 12 — "We tried a similar tool a few years ago and it didn't work."

> "Really helpful context. Can I ask which tool, and what went wrong? That's a conversation I really want to have, because the mistakes previous tools made are often the things we've deliberately designed against. And if there's something specific that would kill this for you on day one, I'd rather hear it now than three months in."

---

## Objection 13 — "Our English teachers are really resistant to new tech."

> "Understood, and I'd be more worried if they weren't. Here's what usually works: rather than pushing a tool on the whole department, we pilot with one keen teacher and one keen class. Let that teacher tell the rest of the department what they think. If it's a no, it's a no from them, not from the tool. If it's a yes, that teacher is the best internal advocate you'll ever have. Is there one person in your team who would give it a fair go?"

---

## Objection 14 — "This feels risky to be one of the first schools."

> "It is risky — I won't pretend otherwise. But the risk is asymmetric. The downside is a failed 4-week pilot with one class that you can walk away from at zero cost. The upside is being a named partner school on a tool built for your department, with 50 percent off Year 1, a direct line to me, priority on features, and your name on a case study that other HoDs will read. Also — the free pilot is the hedge. You only take on any real commitment after you've seen the pilot results. Does that change how it feels?"

---

## Objection 15 — "Just send me an email and I'll look at it."

> "I will — absolutely, I'll send you everything we've talked about. But I'd love to pin down one specific thing before I do. Can we agree a date I should come back to you? Not to chase — just so you know when to expect a follow-up and I know when to stop worrying I'm being ignored. [DATE] OK?"

---

## Underneath every objection — the three questions to keep asking yourself

1. **What's the actual concern?** Often the stated objection isn't the real one. "Too expensive" often means "I'm not convinced it works". "Teachers are too busy" often means "I'm not sure how to introduce it politically". Probe gently.
2. **Is this school a genuine Founding School?** If objections keep multiplying, the answer may be "they're not a Founding School — they're a Year 2 customer". Don't force it. Say so, invite them to come back in six months, and move on.
3. **Have I made the next step concrete?** Never leave an objection with a vague "let me think". Always end on a named date, a named person, or a named artefact being sent.

---

## When an objection is the right answer

Sometimes the objection is genuinely a good reason to walk away. For example:

- The school is in special measures and needs stability, not innovation.
- The HoD is leaving next term.
- The school's data protection posture is genuinely incompatible with an AI tool.
- The budget genuinely doesn't exist.

In these cases, the right move is to be gracious, close the file for 6–12 months, and spend your time on the next school. Founding Schools are forged, not forced.
